# Abstract 30 

Diffusion models are increasingly used to predict transcriptional responses to 31 

perturbations, but whether they improve on simpler generative and representation -based 32 

baselines remains unclear. Existing evaluations often do not separate the effects of model 33 

architecture, input representation, biological context and metric choice, making it difficult 34 

to determine where diffusion -based methods are useful. Here we introduce PertDiffBench, 35 

a standardized benchmark for diffusion -based transcriptomic perturbation prediction 36 

across single -cell and bulk RNA -seq datasets. PertDiffBench evaluates diffusion -based 37 

models across three complementary evaluation settings: standard prediction in known 38 

single -cell contexts and bulk perturbation conditions, generalization to unseen cell types, 39 

species, drugs and intermediate time points, and stress tests of feature dimensionality, 40 

input representation, noise type and gene ordering. Across these settings, diffusion models 41 

did not show a consistent advantage. scGen remained a strong baseline in common 42 

prediction tasks, whereas scDiffusion was the most competitive diffusion -based method in 43 

several generalization settings. Temporal imputation showed a different pattern, with a 44 

simple DDPM operating directly in expression space outperforming more specialized 45 

models. Stress tests showed that performance was model dependent and sensitive to 46 

feature dimensionality, encoder choice, noise type and gene ordering. Pretrained encoders 47 

did not consistently improve performance, with the classical scVI representation slightly 48 

exceeding STATE in seen -condition and unseen -cell -type settings. These results indicate 49 

that diffusion -model performance in perturbation response prediction depends strongly on 50 

task design and representation choice. PertDiffBench provides a practical framework for 51 

evaluating these models under biologically varied and stress -tested conditions. 52

# Introduction 54 

Single -cell perturbation technologies have made it possible to study gene function and 55 

cell -state transitions at high resolution  (Bunne, et al., 2024; Peidli, et al., 2024) . By 56 

measuring transcriptional responses to genetic or chemical perturbations in individual cells, 57 

these assays can reveal state -specific effects, population heterogeneity and rare 58 

responsive subpopulations that are not accessible from bulk readouts. Yet the experimental 59 

space grows quickly with perturbation type, cellular context, dose, time and species, and 60 

soon exceeds what can be measured systematically. Given the cost, throughput and 61 

applicability limits of current single -cell assays, exhaustive experimental coverage is not 62 

feasible  (Srivatsan, et al., 2020; Yao, et al., 2024) . Predicting cellular responses to 63 

unmeasured perturbations and unseen biological conditions from limited observations has 64 

therefore become a central goal of single -cell perturbation modelling  (Qian, et al., 2025; 65 

Rood, et al., 2024) .66 

Computational methods have evolved with this goal, moving from the description of 67 

measured perturbation effects toward generalization across unmeasured conditions. Early 68 

method s such  as scGen used variational autoencoders to model perturbation -induced 69 

shifts in latent space and predict responses in unmeasured cellular contexts  (Lotfollahi, et 70 

al., 2019) . CPA then extended this framework through compositional modeling of 71

time points and species  (Lotfollahi, et al., 2023) . More recent knowledge -informed graph 73 

models and single -cell foundation models, including GEARS, scGPT, scFoundation, 74 

STATE and Tahoe -x1, have further broadened the scope of out -of -distribution perturbation 75 

response prediction  (Adduri, et al., 2025; Cui, et al., 2024; Gandhi, et al., 2025; Hao, et al., 76 

2024; Roohani, et al., 2024) .77 

Diffusion models have recently entered this landscape as a new class of generative 78 

models for cellular response prediction. By learning conditional distributions through 79 

iterative denoising, they provide a flexible way to model heterogeneous and potentially 80 

multimodal cellular responses. Their formulation also allows perturbation identity, dose, cell 81 

state and other covariates to be included in the generation process. Early single -cell 82 

applications, including scVAEDer, scDiff and scDiffusion, explored diffusion -based 83 

generation of cellular states under specified biological conditions, with scVAEDer among 84 

the first to demonstrate perturbation response prediction using a VAE –diffusion framework 85 

(Luo, et al., 2024; Sadria and Layton, 2025; Tang, et al., 2023) . More recent methods have 86 

become increasingly specialized: dbDiffusion focuses on unseen genetic perturbations and 87 

connects generation with debiasing and downstream statistical inference  (Shang, et al., 88 

2025) . scPPDM addresses single -cell drug response prediction, with an emphasis on 89 

unseen drugs and covariate combinations  (Liang, et al., 2025) . Squidiff extends diffusion -90 

based modelling to developmental dynamics and mechanistic interpretation  (He, et al., 91 

2025) . In parallel, morphology -focused models such as MorphoDiff and MorphDiff apply 92 

diffusion models to perturbation -induced cellular imaging phenotypes  (Navidi, et al., 2025; 93 

Wang, et al., 2025) . These studies suggest that diffusion -based perturbation response 94 

prediction is emerging as a diverse family of methods with different architectures, 95 

conditioning strategies, modalities and prediction targets. 96 

However, it remains unclear what actually drives the performance of diffusion models 97 

in omics perturbation prediction. Diffusion models were first successful in domains such as 98 

imaging, where nearby features show strong local dependence and the data have an 99 

explicit spatial structure. Omics data differ in an important way: genes have no natural 100 

spatial ordering, and their dependencies are often nonlocal, context dependent and shaped 101 

by regulatory programs rather than local neighbourhoods. This raises a central question 102 

for perturbation modelling. When diffusion -based methods improve performance, do the 103 

gains come from the diffusion backbone itself, or from other parts of the pipeline, such as 104 

input representation, feature selection, dataset scale, noise structure or task design? 105 

Current benchmark studies do not yet answer this question. Existing comparisons 106 

differ in datasets, train/test splits, evaluation metrics and baseline selection, making results 107 

difficult to align across studies  (Csendes, et al., 2025; Li, et al., 2025; Li, et al., 2025; Viñas 108 

Torné, et al., 2025; Wei, et al., 2026; Wenteler, et al., 2025; Wu, et al., 2024) . Diffusion -109 

based models have also not been evaluated systematically within a single framework, so 110 

their relative strengths and limitations remain difficult to assess. This gap is important 111 

because recent benchmarks suggest that complex models are not consistently superior to 112 

simpler baselines and that model rankings can vary across tasks and metrics. A unified 113 

benchmark is therefore needed to determine when diffusion models offer meaningful gains 114 

and which components of the modelling pipeline drive their performance. 115

Here we present  PertDiffBench , a benchmark for evaluating diffusion models in 116 

single -cell perturbation response prediction. PertDiffBench compares diffusion -based 117 

methods, including scDiffusion, scDiff, Squidiff and DDPM -based baselines, with 118 

established generative baselines such as scGen within a unified framework covering seen -119 

condition prediction, unseen cell -type prediction, cross -species transfer, unseen 120 

mechanism -of -action prediction and temporal imputation. It also tests how performance is 121 

affected by feature dimensionality, data representation, input noise and feature ordering. 122 

By evaluating these factors in the same framework, PertDiffBench helps distinguish the 123 

contribution of the diffusion backbone from that of representation, data preprocessing and 124 

task design, and provides a clearer view of when diffusion models are useful for single -cell 125 

perturbation response prediction. 126

# Results 128 

Overview of PertDiffBench 129 

We construct PertDiffBench to assess how well diffusion models support single -cell 130 

perturbation response prediction (Figure 1). The framework is designed to test whether 131 

performance gains are driven by the diffusion backbone itself or from other factors, 132 

including the input data representation, data scale, noise structure and the difficulty of the 133 

extrapolation task. We selected models that represent distinct conditional generation 134 

strategies in current single -cell perturbation modeling: scDiffusion uses classifier -guided 135 

conditional generation, scDiff incorporates conditions through a cross -attention -based 136 

diffusion framework, and Squidiff integrates semantic features into the denoising process 137 

to model cell -state transitions and perturbation responses. To provide controlled references, 138 

we further implemented two DDPM baselines: one that generates directly in the original 139 

gene -expression space and one that generates in a 256 -dimensional latent space encoded 140 

by a three -layer MLP. We also included scGen as a classical VAE -based baseline for 141 

perturbation response prediction. This model panel allowed us to compare published 142 

diffusion architectures with simplified diffusion baselines and an established non -diffusion 143 

generative model under the same evaluation framework. 144 

PertDiffBench first evaluates prediction under measured perturbation conditions using 145 

two standard settings. The Kang18 dataset is used to test single -cell perturbation 146 

prediction in seen cell types, whereas the Ramaiahgari19 dataset is used to test bulk -level 147 

drug perturbation prediction. These tasks evaluate diffusion -based models against the 148 

VAE -based baseline scGen under standard perturbation  response  prediction settings, 149 

providing a reference point for the later analyses of generalization and robustness. 150 

The benchmark then extends to unseen -condition prediction across three 151 

extrapolation settings. Biological -context generalization is evaluated through unseen cell -152 

type prediction on Kang18 and cross -species prediction of LPS responses in the Hagai18 153 

dataset. Perturbation generalization is evaluated on Srivatsan20 by testing prediction for 154 

unseen drug perturbations. Temporal generalization is evaluated on Cao20 through a 155 

temporal imputation task, which reflects the sparse time sampling common in cell 156 

differentiation and dynamic perturbation experiments and tests whether models can 157 

recover cellular states at unobserved intermediate time points. These settings assess not 158

only the fit to observed perturbation patterns, but also the reliability of generalization across 159 

biological context, perturbation type and temporal dynamics. 160 

Beyond task -level comparisons, PertDiffBench examines how data representation, 161 

model components and data noise affect prediction. We compare performance across 162 

feature dimensions and evaluate how different single -cell data representation methods 163 

work with the DDPM backbone, including STATE, scFoundation, scGPT, Tahoe -x1, 164 

Geneformer, Scimilarity, and scVI. These experiments ask whether performance is mainly 165 

determined by the diffusion backbone or depends strongly on data representation. 166 

Because single -cell data are sparse, overdispersed and affected by dropout and technical 167 

noise, PertDiffBench also includes a noise robustness analysis. We introduce Gaussian 168 

noise, Log -Normal noise, Poisson noise and Zero -Inflation to measure how performance 169 

changes under different data perturbations and to test whether the denoising premise of 170 

diffusion models leads to a practical robustness advantage. 171 

At the model and metric levels, all models are evaluated with the same data 172 

processing pipeline, task splits and metrics. The primary metric is PCC -Delta, 173 

supplemented by PCC, MAE, MMD, E -distance and metrics based on top differentially 174 

expressed genes. Together, these metrics measure prediction quality at the levels of global 175 

expression state, perturbation response direction and key differentially expressed genes. 176 

PertDiffBench therefore addresses a diffusion model specific question: whether diffusion 177 

models are well suited to single -cell perturbation response prediction, and under which 178 

data conditions and task settings their strengths and limitations become apparent. 179

Diffusion models do not consistently outperform scGen under seen perturbation 181 

conditions 182 

To assess the baseline predictive performance of diffusion models under seen 183 

perturbation conditions, we compare diffusion -based models with  the VAE -based  model 184 

scGen in two standard perturbation response prediction tasks, one at the single -cell level 185 

and the other at the bulk level. In the Kang18 known -cell -type prediction task, scGen 186 

achieves the highest PCC -Delta and outperforms all diffusion -based models. Among the 187 

diffusion models, scDiffusion performs best, followed by Squidiff, DDPM, scDiff and 188 

DDPM+MLP, with progressively lower performance (Figure 2A, Supplementary Figure 1A 189 

and 1B). The bulk -level drug perturbation task shows a similar pattern. scGen and 190 

scDiffusion rank first and second, respectively, whereas the other diffusion models show 191 

weaker overall performance (Figure 2B, Supplementary Figure 1C). These results suggest 192 

that, under measured perturbation conditions, diffusion models do not consistently 193 

outperform classical VAE -based methods, and their performance varies substantially 194 

across model designs. 195

performance. As shown in Figure 2C, most models lose performance as the feature 197 

dimension increases. scGen remains the strongest model across all feature dimensions, 198 

followed by scDiffusion. DDPM, DDPM+MLP and scDiff stay at relatively low performance 199 

levels, whereas Squidiff performs well with low -dimensional features but declines rapidly 200 

as the feature dimension increases (Supplementary Figure 1D). For the known -cell -type 201 

prediction task, using CD4 T cells as the evaluation setting, we evaluated single -cell 202

Diffusion models show task -dependent generalization under unseen conditions 215 

To assess model generalization under unseen conditions, we design extrapolation 216 

tasks that span cell type, species and perturbation type. In the unseen cell -type prediction 217 

task on the Kang18 dataset, we retain 25% of control cells from the held -out cell type as 218 

input and compare the predictive performance of different models. scGen achieves the 219 

best across PCC, PCC -Delta, MMD and MAE, followed by scDiffusion, whereas DDPM, 220 

DDPM+MLP, scDiff and Squidiff show weaker performance (Figure 3A). These results 221 

indicate that  the  VAE -based model scGen  remains  strong for unseen cell -type prediction, 222 

and that diffusion models do not consistently outperform scGen in this setting. 223 

We next examine how the proportion of control cells affects unseen cell -type prediction. 224 

As the fraction of control cells in the held -out cell type increases from 0% to 25% and 50%, 225 

PCC -Delta remains stable for most models (Figure 3B). scGen showed consistently high 226 

and stable performance across all held -out cell types. Among diffusion -based models, 227 

scDiffusion performed best overall, but its performance was lower for FCGR3A+ 228 

monocytes, indicating context -dependent generalization. Other diffusion -based models 229 

remained substantially weaker, with Squidiff performing close to zero across settings. 230 

These results suggest that control cells provide useful state references, but generalization 231 

is still shaped by the complexity of cell -type -specific responses and by model architecture. 232 

We further evaluate cross -species prediction of LPS responses using the Hagai18 233 

dataset. This task requires models to learn perturbation responses from a subset of species 234 

and predict cellular state changes in an unseen species, making it more challenging than 235 

cell -type transfer within the same species. scGen and scDiffusion show the strongest 236 

overall performance, DDPM performs at an intermediate level, and scDiff, DDPM+MLP and 237 

Squidiff perform less well (Figure 3C) . These results show that cross -species 238 

generalization varies substantially across diffusion models, with only a few approaching 239 

the performance of the VAE -based baseline. 240 

Finally, we evaluated cross -MOA unseen drug prediction, where models were trained 241 

on perturbations from other mechanisms of action and tested on held -out MOA groups. We 242 

compared DDPM, DDPM+MLP and Squidiff, and included ChemCPA, an adversarially 243 

regularized autoencoder -based model for compound -conditioned perturbation response 244 

prediction, as a task -specific baseline. DDPM and DDPM+MLP achieved the best overall 245 

PCC -Delta, with mean values of 0.46 and 0.44, respectively, outperforming ChemCPA 246

Diffusion models show model -specific behavior in temporal imputation 262 

To assess whether diffusion models can infer missing time points, we design a 263 

temporal imputation task using single -cell omics data from DEX -treated A549 cells 264 

collected at 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 10 h . The models are trained on the remaining time points 265 

and used to predict the held -out 4 h and 6 h cell states (Figure 4A and B). The models 266 

show clear differences in this setting (Figure 4C). DDPM shows the strongest overall 267 

performance at both held -out time points. It achieves the lowest MMD and MAE and the 268 

highest PCC -Delta, indicating better recovery of both the expression distribution and 269 

perturbation -induced changes at 4 h and 6 h. scDiffusion achieves the highest PCC, 270 

suggesting accurate reconstruction of overall expression profiles, but it is weaker than 271 

DDPM on PCC -Delta and distribution -level metrics. DDPM+MLP and Squidiff perform 272 

poorly overall, with near -zero PCC and higher MMD and MAE. These results suggest that, 273 

for temporal imputation, a simple DDPM backbone can outperform more complex diffusion 274 

variants. 275 

The UMAP visualization reveals distinct patterns for each model (Figure 4D) . DDPM 276 

produces cells spread across a wider region, but local neighborhoods are less coherent. 277 

Cells generated by scDiffusion closely follow the reference distribution. Squidiff predictions 278 

are concentrated in specific branches, while DDPM+MLP forms a tight cluster of predicted 279 

cells, consistent with strong mode collapse. 280 

These results show that diffusion models can impute unobserved intermediate time 281 

points, but their ability to recover temporal structure varies across implementations. DDPM 282 

better reconstructs the overall expression  distribution .283

# Discussion 344 

Our benchmark shows that diffusion models are not a default improvement over 345 

established generative baselines for single -cell perturbation response prediction. Across 346 

the tasks examined here, scGen remained the strongest model in the seen -condition 347 

setting, while scDiffusion was the most competitive diffusion -based method. Other diffusion 348 

variants showed more variable performance. This result does not rule out the value of 349 

diffusion models, but it argues against treating the diffusion backbone itself as sufficient for 350 

improved prediction. 351 

A recurring pattern across the benchmark is the importance of representation. In the 352 

feature -dimensionality analysis, most models lost accuracy as the input space became 353 

larger. scGen remained robust across feature dimensions, followed by scDiffusion, 354 

whereas DDPM, DDPM+MLP and scDiff stayed at relatively low performance levels. 355 

Squidiff performed well with low -dimensional features but declined rapidly when more 356 

genes were included. These results point to a practical issue for diffusion -based modelling 357 

in omics: denoising in gene -expression space may be sensitive to the scale and structure 358 

of the input features. Unlike images, gene -expression matrices do not have an obvious 359 

spatial neighbourhood structure, and dependencies between genes are often nonlocal and 360 

context dependent. The effectiveness of a diffusion model may therefore depend heavily 361 

on whether the input representation exposes a learnable structure for the denoising 362 

process. 363 

The encoder experiments support this view. Pretrained representations did not provide 364 

a uniform benefit. In the unseen cell -type task, scVI and STATE gave stronger and more 365 

stable results than several larger trained encoders. This suggests that encoder quality 366 

should not be judged only by pretraining scale. For perturbation response prediction, a 367 

useful representation needs to preserve cell -state variation and perturbation -relevant 368 

expression structure in a form that is compatible with the downstream generative model. 369 

Generalization performance also depended on the biological setting. In unseen cell -370 

type prediction, scGen remained the strongest overall model, followed by diffusion -based 371 

methods. Adding control cells from the held -out cell type produced only modest changes, 372 

and the effect varied across cell types. This implies that cell -type extrapolation is not 373 

determined only by the amount of control information available, but also by how 374 

transferable the target cell state is from the training conditions. In cross -species prediction, 375 

scGen and scDiffusion alternated as the best -performing methods, with DDPM usually 376 

ranking below them. Thus, diffusion -based models can be competitive in some out -of -377

Temporal imputation showed a different behavior. In this task, the DDPM baseline 379 

applied directly to the original gene -expression space outperformed DDPM+MLP and 380 

scDiffusion, whereas Squidiff showed the weakest performance. This result suggests that 381 

temporal imputation is particularly sensitive to how intermediate cellular states are 382 

represented. Direct modeling in gene -expression space may preserve subtle, time -383 

dependent changes in gene abundance, while DDPM+MLP compresses the input through 384 

a three -layer MLP into a 256 -dimensional latent space, which may remove part of this 385 

information. These results indicate that the relative performance of diffusion models 386 

depends not only on the diffusion backbone, but also on whether the chosen representation 387 

preserves the structure required by the task. 388 

The robustness analysis further shows that model performance depends on the type 389 

of noise introduced into the input data. Log -Normal overdispersion produced the largest 390 

decrease in top -DE -gene PCC -Delta, whereas dropout had a more limited effect. This 391 

difference may reflect the fact that overdispersion alters gene -level variability and response 392 

magnitude, both of which are central to perturbation response prediction, while dropout 393 

mainly increases sparsity. Robustness to one form of noise therefore does not necessarily 394 

imply robustness to another. Perturbation -model benchmarks should include noise settings 395 

that capture distinct sources of technical and biological variation, rather than relying on a 396 

single generic corruption test. 397 

Taken together, these results suggest that the main determinant of performance is not 398 

the diffusion backbone alone, but its interaction with representation, feature space and task 399 

design. Diffusion models may be useful for single -cell perturbation response prediction, but 400 

their current advantage is conditional rather than universal. Future methods may need 401 

more omics -specific inductive biases, better task -aligned representations, and explicit use 402 

of regulatory and pathway structure to make the denoising process better matched to gene -403 

expression data. 404 

This study has several limitations. The benchmark focuses mainly on transcriptomic 405 

perturbation responses and does not yet cover the full range of multimodal perturbation 406 

readouts. The evaluated methods represent current diffusion -based approaches, but they 407 

do not exhaust all possible architectures. Some failure -mode analyses, including sensitivity 408 

to feature ordering, require further investigation. Finally, quantitative benchmarks can 409 

compare model behavior across controlled settings, but they cannot replace biological 410 

validation. Future work should extend these evaluations to larger perturbation atlases, 411 

multimodal measurements and more stringent out -of -distribution settings, with particular 412 

attention to separating the effects of representation, model architecture and task design. 413

Benchmarking tasks and experiment settings 445 

To systematically evaluate perturbation -response prediction, we organized the 446 

benchmark into four scenarios: known -condition prediction, un seen -condition 447 

generalization, robustness analyses, and representation -learning benchmarking. These 448 

settings were designed to cover both standard in -distribution prediction and biologically 449 

meaningful distribution shifts, including transfer across perturbations, cellular contexts, 450 

species, and treatment times. 451 

Known -condition prediction.  Known -condition prediction was used to assess whether 452 

models could generate post -perturbation expression profiles for perturbation contexts 453 

observed during training. For the scRNA -seq benchmark, we used seven cell types from 454 

the Kang PBMC dataset: B cells, CD14+ monocytes, CD4 T cells, CD8 T cells, dendritic 455 

cells, FCGR3A+ monocytes and natural killer cells. Each cell type was analysed separately, 456 

and models were evaluated on held -out control and perturbed profiles from the same cell 457 

type and perturbation context. 458 

For the bulk RNA -seq benchmark, we used the Ramaiahgari19 dataset from LINCS, 459 

which contains expression profiles before and after aflatoxin treatment across ten dose 460 

levels. The dataset included 12 control samples and 30 treated samples, with three 461 

replicates for each dose. We generated three random train -test splits. In each split, six 462 

control samples and 20 treated samples, two replicates per dose, were used for training, 463 

while the remaining six control samples and ten treated samples, one replicate per dose, 464

Biological -context generalization.  For cross -cell -type prediction, each cell type was held 468 

out in turn, and the remaining cell types were used for training. For each held -out cell type, 469 

we used two protocols. In the fully out -of -distribution setting, all samples from the held -out 470 

cell type were excluded from training. In the partially shared setting, control cells from the 471 

held -out cell type were included during training, but the corresponding perturbed cells were 472

context from prediction in a target context where baseline expression profiles were 474 

available. 475 

The cross -species task followed the same leave -one -context -out design. Each 476 

species was held out in turn, with the remaining species used for training under the same 477 

perturbation setting. Models were then evaluated on the held -out species, allowing us to 478 

test whether perturbation responses learned in one set of species could be transferred to 479 

another. 480 

Perturbation generalization.  Perturbation generalization was evaluated in two settings: 481 

intra -MOA unseen drug prediction and cross -MOA unseen mechanism prediction. For 482 

intra -MOA prediction, each mechanism -of -action (MOA) category was analysed 483 

independently. Within each MOA, drugs were split into training and test sets. Models were 484 

trained to predict drug -treated expression profiles from matched control profiles using the 485 

training drugs, and were evaluated on held -out drugs from the same MOA. This analysis 486 

was performed across 15 MOA -specific splits. 487 

For cross -MOA prediction, we used a leave -one -MOA -out design. In each split, drugs 488 

from one MOA were held out for testing, and drugs from the remaining MOAs were used 489 

for training. This procedure was repeated for all 15 MOA categories. Because this task 490 

requires prediction for drugs from unseen mechanisms, we restricted the benchmark to 491 

models that support drug -conditioned generation. Drug information was provided 492 

according to the input format required by each model. 493 

For DDPM, DDPM+MLP and Squidiff, compound identity and dose were encoded 494 

jointly as a structure –dose representation. Specifically, each compound was represented 495 

by a 1,024 -dimensional RDKit Morgan feature fingerprint using a radius of 2; for multi -496 

componen t SMILES strings, component -level fingerprints were averaged. The resulting 497 

molecular fingerprint was then scaled by log ₁₀ (dose + 1), so that dose information 498 

modulated the structural representation rather than being provided as an independent 499 

categorical  variable. During testing, held -out compounds were encoded from their SMILES 500 

strings using the same procedure, with the corresponding dose summarized by the median 501 

observed dose for that condition. Squidiff used the same structure –dose representation in 502 

the cross -MOA experiments. ChemCPA used a different encoding scheme. Each canonical 503 

SMILES string was converted into a 2,048 -bit RDKit Morgan fingerprint with radius 2, while 504 

the dose was encoded separately by ChemCPA ’s learned dose network before decoding. 505

the 6 h prediction used stronger guidance toward the 8 h state. Classifier guidance was 539 

applied during the final portion of the reverse -diffusion trajectory, and the resulting latent 540

these baselines, temporal information was inferred from the latent geometry between 546 

observed early and late response states. 547
