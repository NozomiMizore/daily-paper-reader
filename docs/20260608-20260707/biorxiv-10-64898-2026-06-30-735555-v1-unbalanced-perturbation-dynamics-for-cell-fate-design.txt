Abstract 14 

Large-scale single-cell perturbation sequencing provides an unprecedented opportunity to con-15 

struct virtual cells for the in silico simulation of cellular responses and the inverse design of 16 

optimal interventions. However, most perturbation-response models treat cellular responses 17 

primarily as mass-preserving shifts in transcriptomic state, whereas single-cell perturbation 18 

measurements are inherently unbalanced: the recovered endpoint population is shaped by tech-19 

nical sampling as well as biological perturbation-induced proliferation, apoptosis and selection. 20 

Here we introduce U-Pert, an unbalanced generative framework that learns condition- and 21 

context-dependent perturbation dynamics from unpaired single-cell snapshots. U-Pert jointly 22 

models transcriptomic state transitions and cell-number dynamics, enabling scalable and robust 23 

forward prediction of unseen perturbations and contexts, as well as inverse design to screen 24 

for desired genetic or pharmacological interventions that achieve user-defined transcriptomic 25 

or population-level outcomes. Across controlled simulations, genetic perturbation benchmarks, 26 

sciPlex3 drug responses and PBMC cytokine perturbations, U-Pert predicts unseen responses, 27 

captures both molecular and abundance changes, and performs inverse design for target gene-28 

expression programs and cell-type compositions. These results show that cell abundance is 29 

an integral component of the perturbation phenotype, providing a mass-aware framework for 30 

virtual-cell modeling and perturbation cell fate design. 31 

# Introduction 32 

Cell fate decisions arise from coordinated changes in gene expression, differentiation, prolifera-33 

tion and cell death [1–3]. A central goal of modern cell biology is therefore not only to observe 34 

these changes, but to predict how a cell population will respond when a gene, pathway or drug 35 

is perturbed. The emerging concept of an in silico virtual cell aims to support this goal by 36 

using computational models to prioritize experiments and anticipate cellular responses across bio-37 

logical contexts [4]. For such models to be biologically useful, however, perturbation responses 38 

cannot be simply reduced to transcriptomic state changes among recovered cells. Perturbations 39 

can indeed alter the abundance and composition of the endpoint population through effects on 40 

proliferation, apoptosis and selection [5]. Ignoring this abundance axis can confound perturbation 41 

analysis by forcing expansion, depletion or selective survival to be explained only as movement in 42 

transcriptomic state space. 43

Single-cell perturbation technologies, including Perturb-seq and sciPlex, now connect targeted 44 

genetic or chemical interventions with transcriptome-wide readouts at single-cell resolution [6, 7]. 45 

These assays have revealed gene functions, regulatory programs and genotype–phenotype relation-46 

ships across rich perturbation landscapes [8–11]. In addition, the resulting datasets also expose 47 

the unbalanced nature of perturbation responses: different conditions can yield markedly different 48 

numbers and compositions of recovered cells, reflecting both technical factors such as sampling, 49 

capture efficiency and experimental design [12] and biological effects of the perturbation itself. At 50 

the same time, the space of possible perturbations, doses, combinations, exposure times and bio-51 

logical contexts remains far larger than can be exhaustively tested. Because most single-cell assays 52 

are destructive, the cells measured before and after perturbation are not the same cells. This cre-53 

ates a need for models that can infer how an unperturbed population gives rise to a perturbed 54 

outcome from unpaired snapshots, while allowing both transcriptomic states and population mass 55 

to change. 56 

Substantial progress has been made in modeling single-cell perturbation responses at the gene 57 

expression level. For instance, representation-learning approaches learn latent spaces for coun-58 

terfactual state prediction across cells, conditions and drug perturbations [13–17]. Graph- and 59 

regulatory-network-based models use biological structure to predict or simulate genetic inter-60 

ventions [18–20]. Optimal-transport, causal and flow-based approaches infer distribution-level 61 

response mappings from unpaired single-cell data [21–24]. Recent foundation, language-informed 62 

and perturbation-specific models have further expanded the scale and conditioning capacity of 63 

response prediction [25–36]. 64 

Despite this breadth, most current methods still treat perturbation response primarily as a 65 

change in gene-expression state and leave population mass fixed or implicit. This mismatch is 66 

particularly consequential when perturbations induce cell killing, expansion or selection, or when 67 

balanced transport forces distributional differences into implausible cross-cell-type matching. More-68 

over, existing models are largely formulated for forward prediction, whereas many biological and 69 

therapeutic questions require inverse design, i.e. identifying genetic or pharmacological interven-70 

tions that steer cells toward user-defined outcomes, from target differential-expression programs 71 

to desired cell-type compositions or abundance phenotypes. Recent benchmark studies have also 72 

shown that perturbation prediction remains sensitive to dataset structure, confounding factors and 73 

may underperform the simple baselines [5, 37, 38], demonstrating the need for models that better 74 

match the biological and experimental structure of perturbation data. 75 

Here we introduce U-Pert (Unbalanced Perturbation), an unbalanced generative framework 76 

for learning perturbation-induced cell-state and cell-abundance dynamics from unpaired single-cell 77 

data. U-Pert learns condition- and context-dependent perturbation dynamics that couple tran-78 

scriptomic state transitions with cellular abundance change. This formulation allows the model 79 

to predict responses to unseen perturbations and biological contexts while also supporting inverse 80 

design to screen for desired genetic or pharmacological interventions that achieve user-defined tran-81 

scriptomic or population-level outcomes. The unbalanced modeling is particularly important for 82 

population-level inverse design, because targets such as cell-type proportions or counts depend on 83 

perturbation-induced expansion and depletion rather than transcriptomic state transitions alone. 84 

For perturbations specified by discrete identities, such as target genes or drug compounds, U-Pert 85 

performs design by optimizing a continuous perturbation representation and retrieving real candi-86 

dates by embedding similarity; for continuous variables such as dose, it can search the perturbation 87 

space directly. Across controlled simulations with known ground truth and broad genetic, phar-88 

macological and cytokine perturbation datasets, U-Pert predicts unseen responses, captures both 89 

molecular and abundance changes, and performs inverse design for target gene-expression pro-90 

grams and cell-type compositions. By treating perturbation responses as unbalanced changes in 91 

both cellular state and population structure, U-Pert provides a framework for perturbation biology 92 

in which virtual cells can move beyond response prediction toward the rational design of cell-fate 93 

outcomes. 94

## Overview of U-Pert 96 

External biological perturbations, such as gene knockouts [6, 9] or drug treatments [7], leave an 97 

immediate transcriptomic signature in single cells, most visibly through the up- or down-regulation 98 

of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) (Fig. 1a). This transcriptomic readout has been the pri-99 

mary target of many perturbation-response models [13, 15, 18, 21–24, 29, 31–35]. Across the 100 

datasets analyzed, however, perturbations also produced substantial differences in the number of 101

recovered cells (Fig. 1b Left). Because many of these experiments began with controlled initial 102 

cell inputs, the resulting endpoint differences reflect an abundance signal beyond initial load-103 

ing, although capture efficiency, sampling and screening design can also contribute. Perturbations 104 

are indeed known to alter proliferative capacity through cell-cycle regulation or apoptosis induc-105 

tion [5, 39, 40] (Fig. 1b Right). These observations motivate modeling perturbation responses as 106 

coupled endpoints, combining transcriptomic state changes with condition-dependent variation in 107 

recovered cell abundance (Fig. 1b). 108 

To capture such coupled processes, we develop U-Pert as a condition- and context-wise unbal-109 

anced flow matching framework. U-Pert models the temporal evolution of cell populations by 110 

coupling transcriptomic state transitions with changes in cell abundance (Fig. 1c and Methods). 111 

The state of a cell population is represented as a time-dependent distribution over gene-expression 112 

profiles, conditioned on both the perturbation condition and the biological context. Here, the per-113 

turbation condition can represent a specific gene knockout, drug treatment or dose, whereas the 114 

biological context can represent covariates such as cell line, tissue type or donor (Fig. 1d; Methods). 115 

Unlike standard balanced optimal transport, which preserves total mass, U-Pert uses an unbal-116 

anced transport-with-growth formulation to allow cell populations to expand or shrink during 117 

the response. In this formulation, the gene velocity field describes how cells transition through 118 

transcriptomic state space, whereas the growth rate function describes how cellular mass changes 119 

along the response. Biologically, for a given condition and context, the gene velocity field captures 120 

perturbation-induced gene-expression trajectories, while the growth rate function explicitly models 121 

changes in cell abundance caused by division and apoptosis (Fig. 1c,d). 122 

A major challenge in modeling single-cell perturbation responses is the destructive nature 123 

of sequencing, which yields highly unpaired unbalanced data. Consequently, one cannot observe 124 

the same cell both before and after perturbation. U-Pert addresses this challenge by learning 125 

response dynamics from unpaired control and perturbed snapshots. For each perturbation condi-126 

tion, it constructs an unbalanced matching between the control and perturbed cell distributions, 127 

and then learns condition- and context-dependent velocity field and growth rate from these 128 

matched population-level responses (Methods). This allows U-Pert to infer both transcriptomic 129 

state transitions and abundance changes directly from static unbalanced single-cell perturbation 130 

measurements. 131 

After U-Pert captures the unified perturbation dynamics across diverse conditions and contexts, 132 

it enables a suite of downstream tasks categorized into forward prediction and inverse design 133 

(Fig. 1e,f and Methods). 134 

Forward Prediction. Based on the learned velocity fields and growth rates, U-Pert provides 135 

predictive and interpretative capabilities (Fig. 1e). 136 

(1) Unseen condition/context generalization. By learning representations for both perturbation 137 

conditions and biological contexts, U-Pert can generalize to settings that were not directly observed 138 

during training. It simulates forward responses to predict cell distributions and transcriptional 139 

states for zero-shot scenarios, including responses to unseen perturbations and biological, such as a 140 

held-out gene/drug, or a held-out cell line absent from the training data (Fig. 1e Top and Methods). 141 

(2) Driver gene discovery. U-Pert also allows the learned dynamics to be interrogated mecha-142 

nistically. The gene-expression velocity field can be used to infer condition/context-specific gene 143 

regulatory relationships that drive state transitions. In parallel, the growth rate function can be 144 

used to identify genes associated with cellular proliferation or apoptosis, linking molecular features 145 

to population-level abundance changes (Fig. 1e Bottom and Methods). 146 

Inverse Design. A feature of U-Pert is its ability to perform inverse design, reversing 147 

the perturbation-to-phenotype workflow to identify interventions for desired biological outcomes 148 

(Fig. 1f). 149 

(1) Targeting transcriptomic states. Given a desired outcome defined by a set of DEGs, U-150 

Pert can trace the dynamics backward to search the embedding space for the optimal perturbation 151 

condition, such as a specific drug or genetic modification, that is predicted to drive control cells 152 

toward the target transcriptional state (Fig. 1f Top and Methods). 153 

(2) Targeting cell type proportions and counts. By modeling perturbation responses as unbal-154 

anced dynamics, U-Pert can use macroscopic population outcomes as inverse-design objectives. It 155 

therefore designs perturbations not only toward desired transcriptional states, but also toward tar-156 

get cell-type proportions or absolute cell counts by allowing population mass to expand or contract 157 

through the growth field. Given a target composition, such as enriching a rare therapeutic cell 158 

population or eliminating a malignant cluster, U-Pert infers the external perturbation condition 159 

predicted to achieve this goal (Fig. 1f Bottom and Methods). 160

## U-Pert enables forward prediction and inverse design of perturbation 161 

## responses in a controlled dynamical system 162 

We first evaluated U-Pert to recover both components in a controlled synthetic system, where 163 

the true perturbation dynamics were explicitly defined (Fig. 2a). The system consists of three 164 

genes ( G1, G2, G3), whose expression levels (mRNA X1, X2, X3) are governed by an underlying 165 

gene regulatory network (GRN) coupled with apoptosis dynamics. Specifically, G1 and G2 form a 166 

toggle-switch circuit characterized by mutual inhibition, with both genes also exhibiting positive 167 

autoregulation. The third gene, G3, acts as an upstream regulator that suppresses both G1 and 168 

G2 while being activated by G2. In addition, G3 directly promotes apoptosis, thereby linking 169 

gene expression dynamics to cell survival dynamics. The transcription rates of X1 and X2 are 170 

parameterized by ρ1 and ρ2, respectively, while the apoptosis rate is controlled by α (Methods). In 171 

our experiments, only these three dynamical parameters are perturbed, with all other parameters 172 

fixed throughout. 173 

To evaluate forward prediction under unseen perturbations, we defined a control population 174 

of 1,163 cells under a fixed parameter setting and generated perturbed populations by varying 175 

(ρ1, ρ 2, α ) (Fig. 2b). Evaluation on 100 unseen perturbation conditions shows that U-Pert-predicted 176 

populations closely match the ground-truth perturbed populations after projection onto the 177 

(X1, X 2) expression space. Representative examples from several selected perturbation conditions 178 

are shown in Fig. 2b, illustrating that U-Pert faithfully captures cellular responses to perturbations. 179 

Beyond forward prediction, U-Pert further enables recovery of underlying perturbation con-180 

ditions via inverse design. Using target perturbed populations with ground-truth parameters, we 181 

inferred the corresponding perturbation conditions from the cellular states. Across 100 unseen per-182 

turbation conditions, the inferred conditions achieved an average cosine distance of 0.09 from the 183 

true conditions. After projection into PCA space, paired inferred and true perturbation parame-184 

ters showed strong overlap, indicating accurate recovery of the underlying dynamical parameters 185 

(Fig. 2c). For visualization clarity, we show 10 representative perturbation conditions selected from 186 

the full evaluation set in this PCA projection. 187 

To further design the desired cell fates, we considered two inverse-design tasks enabled by 188 

U-Pert. At the gene level, U-Pert infers perturbations that induce prescribed differential expres-189 

sion patterns, such as decreasing X1 while increasing X2, resulting in clear shifts in expression 190 

distributions relative to the control (Fig. 2d). At the cell-type composition level, U-Pert enables 191 

control over cell-type proportions, where cell types are defined by dominant gene expression pat-192 

terns (Methods). Starting from a control population dominated by T1 cells, U-Pert successfully 193 

generates populations enriched for T2, T3, or T4 cells, achieving final proportions of 99.9%, 99.3%, 194 

and 94.4%, respectively (Fig. 2e). 195 

Of note, this controllability indeed did not come from arbitrarily forcing prescribed target com-196 

positions. U-Pert respected the intrinsic dynamical constraints of the underlying gene regulatory 197 

system. For instance, a 50% T1 and 50% T2 composition cannot be robustly achieved by a sin-198 

gle perturbation applied to the fixed initial population. Mechanistically, this behavior is consistent 199 

with the toggle-switch structure of the GRN, in which T1 and T2 correspond to two competing 200 

stable attractors driven by mutually inhibitory X1 and X2 programs. Perturbations attempting to 201 

balance the two fates inevitably place part of the population near the separatrix and the associated 202 

saddle-like transition region between the two basins of attraction. Cells in this weakly committed 203 

regime do not fully converge to either stable fate and are therefore classified as the intermedi-204 

ate state T4. Consequently, U-Pert remains faithful to the attractor geometry of the underlying 205 

dynamical system, revealing biologically constrained design limits instead of generating physically 206 

implausible population mixtures. 207 

Overall, rather than merely matching endpoint clouds, U-Pert can recover both imposed state 208 

dynamics and imposed survival constraints in the synthetic perturbation datasets. 209

## genetic perturbation benchmarks 211 

We next evaluated the predictive benefit of the unbalanced formulation in real biological datasets. 212 

Large-scale single-cell genetic perturbation screens provide a stringent benchmark because pertur-213 

bation responses must be predicted for unseen genetic interventions, while the recovered endpoint 214 

populations vary substantially across conditions. In pooled Perturb-seq experiments, cells are 215

To determine whether an abundance-aware formulation improves genetic-perturbation predic-228

Replogle K562 using standard perturbation-response metrics (Fig. 3c; Methods). The compar-230

changes relative to control (Fig. 3d). U-Pert therefore retained robust transcriptomic prediction 242 

performance while introducing an additional mass readout. 243

turbation with the observed target-to-source abundance ratio for each condition and benchmarked 245 

this readout against a supervised MLP mass baseline using the same condition information. U-Pert 246

0.014 to 0.247 and 0.368 in Replogle K562. This result suggests that the learned growth field pro-250 

vides a useful condition-level abundance readout that is not captured by balanced transcriptomic 251 

predictors alone. 252 

Taken together, these benchmarks clarify the value of unbalanced modeling in pooled genetic 253 

screens. Although recovered cell number reflects both biological and technical sources of varia-254 

tion, modeling condition-dependent mass helped U-Pert better match the measured perturbation 255 

endpoint, improving transcriptomic prediction beyond merely balanced state-only predictors. 256

## U-Pert captures drug-induced state changes and cell-abundance changes 257 

## in sciPlex3 258 

Genetic perturbation benchmarks provide evidence that U-Pert improves prediction across unseen 259 

interventions. To examine a setting in which abundance change is a more explicit component of 260 

the perturbation phenotype, we next investigated chemical perturbations, where dose and cell-line 261 

identity can produce transcriptional state changes, cell survival effects or both. We used sciPlex3 [7] 262 

to evaluate U-Pert’s ability to jointly recover transcriptional state and abundance changes in real 263 

drug responses. The dataset included 188 compounds, three cancer cell lines, four dose levels and 264 

two time points (Fig. 4a). Each held-out condition was defined by a tuple of drug, cell line, dose 265 

and time. Predictions were generated from matched control cells. 266 

To assess U-Pert’s ability to recover drug-induced abundance changes, we compared the 267 

observed cell-abundance fold change with the mean predicted mass for each held-out condition. 268 

We evaluated gain over a no-change baseline in log2 fold-change space (Fig. 4b). Conditions were 269 

stratified by whether the observed fold change fell within the no-change band from 0.8 to 1.25. In 270 

conditions with measurable abundance shifts, U-Pert improved 79 of 81 held-out cases. The median 271 

error reduction was 0.466 log2 units (Wilcoxon P = 6 .46 × 10 −15 ). This result was not explained 272 

by a generic tendency to move predictions away from the no-change value of one. In conditions 273 

within the no-change band, only 14 of 43 cases improved over the no-change baseline. The median 274 

delta error was -0.069 (Wilcoxon P = 0 .0074). Thus, U-Pert added value specifically when the 275 

perturbation required a change in population mass. 276 

To assess how learning mass affected transcriptional-state prediction, we evaluated U-Pert 277 

across the same 124 held-out perturbation tuples. U-Pert retained strong state accuracy while 278 

adding an abundance readout (Fig. 4c,e). Relative to CellFlow, mean delta-expression correla-279 

tion increased from 0.152 to 0.380, and mean gene-level R2 increased from 0.644 to 0.736. Latent 280 

energy distance decreased from 52.0 to 33.5, and latent Wasserstein distance decreased from 169.7 281 

to 103.6. On these four displayed metrics, U-Pert also outperformed identity, chemCPA, Con-282 

dOT and BioLord. Because supplementary state metrics showed less uniform separation among 283 

the strongest baselines, we do not interpret these results as uniform dominance across every tran-284 

scriptional metric. Rather, the sciPlex3 benchmark supports a more specific conclusion: U-Pert 285 

recovered transcriptional responses while adding condition-specific abundance prediction. 286 

To evaluate U-Pert’s generalization to unmeasured drug doses, we then applied the learned 287 

model to belinostat (PXD101) on a log-spaced dose grid across A549, K562 and MCF7 cells at 288 

24 h (Fig. 4d). The predicted particle-level mass distributions followed the observed abundance 289 

trajectories across the measured dose points. This zero-shot interpolation converted dose into an 290 

explicit survival-like response. This expanded perturbation prediction beyond comparing expression 291 

states across doses. 292 

Finally, the velocity-growth decomposition further separated distinct components of the drug 293 

response. We separated these components for belinostat at 10 μM and 24 h (Fig. 4f), an HDAC 294 

inhibitor setting with cell-line-specific sensitivity. In this response, MCF7 was relatively less sen-295 

sitive, and the observed effect was dominated by state displacement. Consistent with this biology, 296 

the velocity-only trajectory captured much of the MCF7 response. K562 showed the opposite pat-297 

tern. The dominant effect was loss of cellular mass, and the growth-only trajectory moved closest 298 

to the observed treated population. A549 showed both effects, requiring both velocity and growth 299 

to match the observed response. 300 

Together, these results show that in drug perturbation task, explicit mass modeling helps 301 

describe cellular responses that cannot be fully represented by expression-state shifts alone. 302

## abundance phenotypes 304 

We next asked whether the sciPlex3-trained U-Pert model could identify perturbations matching 305 

specified transcriptional or abundance phenotypes. For each design task, we kept the trained model 306 

fixed and optimized a continuous drug embedding to minimize the U-Pert objective for the desired 307 

response (Fig. 5a; Methods). The optimized embedding was then mapped to real compounds in 308 

an approximately 2,000-drug ChEMBL-derived library [43], and candidate compounds were re-309 

evaluated with the same objective. Known pharmacology was used only after this step to assess 310 

the biological plausibility of the selected candidates. 311 

To design a transcriptional response, we targeted a K562 module-level response centered on cell-312 

cycle regulation rather than isolated marker genes. The desired program increased a cellular-stress 313 

gene group, including HSPA5, MDM2, GCLC, GCLM and FTL, while suppressing a proliferative 314 

cell-cycle gene group marked by TOP2A, CENPF and CDC45 (Fig. 5b). These target modules 315 

reflect biological processes recurrently connected to unfolded-protein stress responses and cancer 316 

cell-cycle control [8, 39]. The optimized embedding strongly activated the target module, with a 317 

module score of 10.71. Mapping this designed embedding to the external compound library selected 318 

Venetoclax, whose predicted response preserved the intended module direction, increasing the 319 

cellular-stress gene group and suppressing the cell-cycle gene group while satisfying all specified up-320 

and down-regulation directions (Fig. 5c,d). This candidate is biologically plausible: Venetoclax is a 321 

selective BCL-2 inhibitor with documented activity in BCL-2-dependent hematologic malignancy 322 

models, including chronic myeloid leukemia stem and progenitor contexts [44, 45]. 323 

To design an abundance phenotype, we then optimized a broad-killing objective intended to 324 

induce cell death across A549, K562 and MCF7 (Fig. 5e). The designed embedding reduced pre-325 

dicted survival across all three cell lines, and candidate mapping selected Vincristine as the top 326 

real compound, with predicted survival of 18%, 29% and 32% in A549, K562 and MCF7 at 10 μM327 

and a broad-killing score of 0.65 (Fig. 5f,g), which also shows a strong dosage dependence. Vin-328 

cristine is a Vinca alkaloid microtubule-targeting chemotherapy, and microtubule disruption is a 329 

well-established anticancer mechanism that blocks mitotic progression and induces cell death [46]. 330 

Other high-scoring candidates in this screen (Alpelisib, Vorinostat, Eribulin, Arsenic Trioxide and 331 

Paclitaxel) also fell into recognizable oncology-related mechanisms, including PI3K inhibition, 332 

HDAC inhibition, microtubule targeting and pro-apoptotic leukemia therapy [47, 48]. The selected 333 

compound is therefore consistent with the broad-killing phenotype produced by the inverse design 334 

objective. 335 

Finally, to evaluate U-Pert’s inverse-design capability for selective killing, we specified an 336 

abundance objective that reduced K562 survival while preserving the other cell lines. The tar-337 

get predicted survival values were 100%, 40% and 100% for A549, K562 and MCF7, respectively 338 

(Fig. 5h). The optimized embedding matched this target closely. After mapping the design to real 339 

compounds, Navitoclax ranked highest by target-profile score rather than by embedding similarity, 340 

reducing predicted K562 survival to 42% while keeping A549 near baseline at 102% and par-341 

tially sparing MCF7 at 78% (Fig. 5i,j). This candidate is also mechanistically coherent: Navitoclax 342 

inhibits BCL-2, BCL-xL and BCL-w, and BCL-family blockade engages mitochondrial apoptosis 343 

and can accelerate apoptosis during mitotic stress [49, 50]. Because K562 is a BCR-ABL-positive 344 

chronic myeloid leukemia-derived line, whereas A549 and MCF7 are non-leukemic solid-tumor cell 345 

lines, this leukemia-associated BCL-family vulnerability provides a plausible mechanism for the 346 

predicted K562-selective response. 347 

Interestingly, the three tasks recovered pharmacologically coherent compounds: Venetoclax for 348 

the K562 transcriptional module, Vincristine for broad killing and Navitoclax for K562-selective 349 

killing. Notably, two K562-centered designs converged on BCL-family biology despite being driven 350 

by distinct objectives, consistent with studies showing that prosurvival BCL2-family programs 351 

support chronic myeloid leukemia stem/progenitor cells and that BCL-2/BCR-ABL co-targeting 352 

can eliminate these cells [45, 51]. This convergence suggests that U-Pert can prioritize biologically 353 

plausible perturbations for both transcriptional programs and cell abundance phenotypes. 354

## matching in PBMCs 356 

We next considered a complementary regime in which large population-size changes are not the 357 

dominant signal. PBMC cytokine perturbations [52] provide such a setting, where responses are 358 

expected to be largely cell-type-specific, and the coupling of balanced transport may introduce 359 

implausible cross-cell-type matching. The Parse 10M PBMC dataset spans 12 donors, 90 cytokines 360 

and 16 immune cell types, providing a test of U-Pert’s ability to improve donor-held-out prediction 361 

by reducing forced matching and correcting response direction (Fig. 6a). 362 

To compare forced cross-cell-type matching between balanced and unbalanced formulations, we 363 

quantified the fraction of predicted mass assigned to cell-type changes inconsistent with the anno-364 

tated source and target cell types. Balanced transport-based prediction enforces mass-preserving 365 

matching between control and treated distributions, which can encourage cross-cell-type assign-366 

ments when the cytokine response is mainly a local transcriptional shift. By relaxing this constraint, 367 

U-Pert can avoid explaining every distributional difference through state transport across cell types. 368 

U-Pert reduced this wrong-change mass ratio relative to CellFlow across donor-generalization set-369 

tings (Fig. 6b), from 0.109 to 0.094 when no donors were seen for the test cytokine, and from 0.131 370 

to 0.118 at the 8-donor setting. 371 

To link this reduction in forced matching to response geometry, we compared predicted and 372 

observed perturbation directions across held-out cytokine-donor conditions. The median cosine 373 

gain over CellFlow increased from 0.055 with no seen donors to 0.186 with 8 seen donors and 374 

0.211 with 10 seen donors (Fig. 6c). At the 8-donor setting, U-Pert improved 85.4% of held-out 375 

conditions, with 48 condition-level summaries and a paired Wilcoxon test of P = 6 .7 × 10 −7. All 376 

non-zero seen-donor settings were repeated across three donor subsets. 377 

To examine this directional correction in biologically relevant contexts, we analyzed known 378 

cytokine-responsive cell types treated with IFN-β and IL-15. For IFN-β, U-Pert better aligned 379 

predicted response vectors with observed responses in monocyte, dendritic, B cell and NK cell 380 

contexts. For IL-15, the same pattern appeared in NK, CD8 T cell and MAIT cell contexts (Fig. 6d). 381 

These cases show what the cosine metric captures: U-Pert keeps the response vector closer to the 382 

observed cell-type-specific cytokine direction, whereas balanced transport can distort the prediction 383 

direction through implausible assignments. 384 

Notably, the gain in response geometry did not come at the expense of standard perturbation-385 

prediction accuracy. At the 8-donor setting, U-Pert improved DEG50 R2 per cell type from 0.854 386 

to 0.894, increased gene response Pearson correlation per cell type from 0.319 to 0.379, and reduced 387 

latent energy distance from 2.91 to 2.48 relative to CellFlow (Fig. 6e). Thus, U-Pert improved 388 

response direction while also achieving stronger gene-level and latent-distribution accuracy. 389 

In summary, the PBMC analysis shows that unbalanced modeling remains useful even when 390 

large abundance shifts are not the main response. By relaxing mass-preserving matching, U-391 

Pert reduced forced cross-cell-type assignments, improved response directions and retained strong 392 

gene-level and latent-distribution accuracy. In this setting, the unbalanced formulation of U-Pert 393 

achieved prediction robustness by preventing distributional mismatch from being converted into 394 

biologically implausible state transitions. 395

# Discussion 396 

To capture the coupled effects of gene-expression change and cell-abundance variation from destruc-397 

tive and unpaired single-cell snapshots, we developed U-Pert, a mass-aware framework for single-cell 398 

perturbation modeling. By learning condition- and context-aware velocity and growth fields, U-Pert 399 

predicts responses to unseen perturbations and supports in silico prioritization of interventions for 400 

user-defined transcriptomic or population-level outcomes. The velocity field describes displacement 401

cytokine perturbation settings, the endpoints measured by single-cell profiling were better repre-404 

sented as coupled molecular and population-level phenotypes than as purely transcriptomic states. 405 

These findings support treating recovered cell abundance as an explicit component of the pertur-406 

bation response, while keeping separate the biological and technical factors that may contribute 407 

to this signal. 408 

Existing single-cell perturbation models have achieved substantial progress in counterfactual 409 

expression prediction, response mapping and generative modeling [13–15, 21, 23, 24, 29]. Their main 410 

object, however, is usually the perturbed expression distribution. When the recovered endpoint 411 

population differs in size or composition, balanced formulations may absorb depletion, expansion or 412 

selective survival into apparent transcriptomic movement. Related unbalanced and growth-aware 413 

approaches in trajectory inference have shown that proliferation, apoptosis and changes in popu-414 

lation size can be incorporated into dynamical models of single-cell data [41, 42, 53–56], where 415 

unbalanced transport and growth-aware models reconstruct cellular dynamics while accounting for 416 

unbalance sampling, proliferation, apoptosis and shifts in population size. These methods show that 417 

population-size changes can be treated as part of the dynamical process rather than as a nuisance 418 

variable. However, they primarily address autonomous temporal progression, whereas perturbation 419 

biology requires models that are conditioned on external genetic or pharmacological interventions, 420 

biological context and design objectives. U-Pert brings this unbalanced perspective into single-cell 421 

perturbation modeling by coupling transcriptomic state transitions with perturbation-induced pro-422 

liferation and apoptosis. This separation provides a more interpretable description of perturbation 423 

responses, allowing drug effects to be decomposed into state-transition and survival components, 424 

and cytokine responses to be modeled without forcing implausible cross-cell-type matching. It 425 

also creates a route to inverse design, because desired outcomes can be specified at the level of 426 

gene-expression programs, cell-type compositions or abundance phenotypes. 427 

While this study demonstrates the potential of unbalanced perturbation modeling, several lim-428 

itations remain. First, observed cell-count changes can reflect both biological effects and technical 429 

factors, including sampling depth, capture efficiency and batch variation. More carefully controlled 430 

experimental designs will be important for separating true proliferation or apoptosis from technical 431 

sampling noise. Second, because U-Pert learns dynamics from unpaired snapshots, the inferred tra-432 

jectories should be interpreted as plausible transport paths rather than directly observed single-cell 433 

lineages. Future integration with lineage tracing [57], live-cell imaging [58] or densely sampled time-434 

resolved perturbation measurements [24] could further constrain the learned dynamics. Third, the 435 

current inverse-design workflow relies on learned perturbation or drug embeddings and candidate 436 

retrieval in an embedding library; therefore, experimental validation is required before designed 437 

perturbations can be interpreted as causal or therapeutically actionable interventions. 438 

Future extensions could broaden the biological scope of unbalanced perturbation modeling. Spa-439 

tial perturbation technologies now make it possible to measure genetic perturbations together with 440 

spatial transcriptomic or imaging measurements in native tissue contexts [59–63]. Extending U-Pert 441 

to these data would allow perturbation responses to be modeled across transcriptomic state, popu-442 

lation mass, physical location and local cell–cell communication. Such models could distinguish how 443 

perturbations alter the targeted cells themselves from how they reshape neighboring cells through 444 

ligand–receptor signaling, niche remodeling and collective tissue-level responses [64]. Multimodal 445 

measurements, perturbation time courses and prospective validation of designed interventions 446 

would also further clarify whether the mass-aware virtual-cell models can support experimental 447 

decision-making. 448 

Overall, U-Pert provides a unified unbalanced generative framework for learning perturbation-449 

induced cell-state and cell-abundance dynamics from unpaired unbalanced single-cell snapshots. 450 

By combining forward simulation, mechanistic interpretation and inverse design, U-Pert extends 451 

virtual-cell modeling from predicting transcriptional responses to designing perturbations that 452 

control both molecular programs and population outcomes. More broadly, our results support an 453 

unbalanced view of perturbation biology, in which virtual-cell models incorporate population-level 454 

changes alongside transcriptional state to predict and design cell fate. 455

## Learning condition- and context-aware perturbation responses 492 

To apply WFR-FM to single-cell perturbation modeling, U-Pert distinguishes between perturba-493 

tion conditions, biological contexts and the matching strata used to construct WFR couplings. 494 

The perturbation condition, denoted by cond, specifies the intervention and intervention-related 495 

variables, such as a genetic knockout, drug identity, dose or treatment duration. The biological 496 

context, denoted by cont, specifies the basal or experimental background in which the perturbation 497 

acts, such as donor identity, cell line or cell type. 498 

In practice, WFR couplings are constructed within dataset-specific matching strata. Each 499 

observed stratum s is associated with a perturbation condition cond s and, when available, the 500 

context covariates used to match control and perturbed cells. For example, when control and per-501 

turbed cells from the same donor or cell line are available, the stratum can include that donor 502 

or cell-line label; otherwise, the coupling may be constructed at a coarser condition level while 503 

the context is provided as an additional neural-network input. This design allows U-Pert to use 504 

matched controls whenever the experimental design supports them, while still sharing information 505 

across contexts through the learned condition- and context-aware fields. 506

## Preprocessing and implementation of U-Pert 524 

Cell embedding. Single-cell transcriptional states were preprocessed before U-Pert training. For 525 

all real biological datasets, count matrices were normalized, log-transformed, filtered to retain 526 

highly variable genes (HVGs), and projected to a 100-dimensional PCA representation. The PCA 527 

model was fitted using control cells and training perturbation conditions. U-Pert learned transport 528 

and growth in this PCA space. Predicted PCA states were subsequently reconstructed in the HVG 529 

expression space for gene-level analyses. 530 

Condition embedding. Each perturbation was represented by a fixed external embedding. 531 

The embedding source depended on the perturbation modality and could include textual descrip-532 

tions, molecular features or protein annotations. In the real-data applications, compound and 533 

cytokine descriptions were embedded before model training. U-Pert mapped each raw perturbation 534 

embedding through a multilayer condition encoder consisting of layer normalization, two feed-535 

forward hidden layers and a final linear projection. The hidden dimension was 512, and the output 536 

condition representation was 256-dimensional in both applications. 537 

Context embedding. Experimental and biological contexts were encoded separately from 538 

perturbation identity according to a dataset-specific rulebook. Each prediction target was rep-539 

resented as a condition tuple, and the rulebook specified which covariates were used for control 540 

matching and model conditioning. Categorical covariates were encoded by trainable embedding 541 

layers, whereas continuous covariates were transformed as specified by the rulebook and encoded 542 

by two-layer multilayer perceptrons. During training and inference, control cells were selected with 543 

the same matching covariates used to define the biological context. 544 

Model architecture. U-Pert comprised two conditional output networks: a velocity network 545 

for transcriptomic transport and a growth network for particle mass dynamics. The networks had 546 

separate parameters but shared the same conditioning inputs. For each sampled particle, the model 547 

encoded the perturbation, WFR interpolation time and context covariates, and then concatenated 548 

these representations to condition the output networks. WFR time was represented by sinusoidal 549 

features followed by a feed-forward time encoder. The processed time representation was projected 550 

to 128 dimensions. 551 

The velocity and growth networks used FiLM-conditioned residual multilayer perceptron 552 

blocks [69, 70]. Each block first mapped the particle state into a hidden representation, normal-553

## Real-data perturbation benchmarks 563 

For real biological data, each prediction target was defined by a perturbation identity together with 564 

a biological or experimental context. U-Pert used matched control cells as the starting population 565 

for each target context and predicted both terminal cell states and particle masses. State-level pre-566 

dictions were evaluated in the shared PCA and HVG expression spaces, whereas mass predictions 567 

were used to assess abundance changes when cell-count information was available. 568 

In the genetic perturbation benchmarks, we evaluated unseen-condition prediction on Norman, 569 

Adamson and Replogle K562 Perturb-seq datasets [8, 9, 11]. Prediction targets were genetic pertur-570 

bation conditions, and matched control cells were used as source populations. We compared U-Pert 571 

with CellFlow, scGPT, GEARS, perturbation-mean and zero-change baselines for expression and 572 

distributional metrics, and with a supervised MLP baseline for condition-level abundance-ratio 573 

prediction. 574 

In sciPlex3 datasets [7], we evaluated chemical perturbation responses across 188 compounds, 575 

three cancer cell lines, four dose levels and two time points. A prediction target was defined by a 576 

compound-cell line-dose-time tuple. We used a held-out-drug split to test generalization to unseen 577 

compounds and evaluated both transcriptional-state recovery and drug-induced cell-abundance 578 

change. Using the trained response field, we further performed inverse drug screening for user-579 

defined gene-expression and abundance objectives. 580 

In the PBMC cytokine-perturbation datasets [52], we evaluated responses across 12 donors and 581 

90 cytokines profiled after 24 h. A prediction target was defined by a cytokine-donor pair. We used 582 

held-out-cytokine and seen-donor generalization settings with donor-matched control cells, and 583 

assessed whether U-Pert preserved cytokine response direction while reducing forced cross-cell-type 584 

transport. 585

## Inverse Design of Cell Type Proportion/Quantity Task 697 

Beyond targeting specific gene expression profiles, a major goal in regenerative medicine, cell 698

population dynamics. Practical applications include directing stem cells to differentiate into a 700

## sciPlex drug inverse design and candidate retrieval 780 

For the sciPlex inverse-design analysis, we used the trained sciPlex U-Pert model as a fixed differ-781 

entiable forward operator and optimized only the continuous drug embedding. Cell-line context, 782 

dose and time covariates were fixed according to the target task. We considered three objectives: a 783 

K562 transcriptional gene-regulation objective, a broad-killing objective that minimized predicted 784 

mass across A549, K562 and MCF7, and a selective-killing objective that matched target mass 785 

ratios across the three cell lines. 786 

Candidate drugs were selected from an external merged library of 2,009 ChEMBL-derived and 787 

curated clinical candidate records [43]. For each drug, an LLM first generated a structured phar-788 

macology description covering targets, mechanisms, pathways, expected transcriptional programs 789 

and cell-line-specific effects, and this text was embedded with a text-embedding model. In total, 790 

1,985 candidates had valid embeddings. After optimizing a continuous embedding, we retrieved 791 

nearest real-drug candidates by cosine similarity and then re-ran U-Pert forward inference for each 792 

candidate under the same task settings. Final compounds were selected by task-specific validation 793 

scores from this re-inference step, not by cosine similarity alone. 794
