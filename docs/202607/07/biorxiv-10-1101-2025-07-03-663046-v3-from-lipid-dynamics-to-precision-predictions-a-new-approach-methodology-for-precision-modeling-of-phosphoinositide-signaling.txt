Abstract 37 38 39 Precision medicine requires models that can translate rich molecular measurements into individualized 40 predictions of biological response. This challenge is particularly acute for phosphoinositide signaling 41 disorders that often exhibit cell-type-specific responses to identical genetic or pharmacological 42 perturbations. Here, we develop a New Approach Methodology (NAM) demonstrating that basal 43 phosphoinositide pool composition, determined by the size of the PI(4)P reserve, determines the 44 robustness of lipid signaling. The NAM comprises a core kinetic model of phosphatidylinositol (PI), 45 phosphatidylinositol 4-phosphate (PI(4)P), phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PI(4,5)P 2), and 46 inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP 3 ) dynamics. The model also incorporates phospholipase C (PLC)-47 mediated hydrolysis and phosphatase-mediated turnover and explicitly accounts for IP 3 biosensor 48 binding during parameter optimization. Parameters were optimized using experimental measurements 49 from superior cervical ganglion (SCG) neurons and validated against independent dose-dependent 50 PI(4,5)P 2 depletion data. Local and global sensitivity analyses were performed to identify the dominant 51 parameter drivers of pathway behavior. These sensitivity relationships were then used to generate a 52 population of model variants that captured phosphoinositide dynamics observed in tsA201 cells, human 53 neuroblastoma cells, and hippocampal neurons. To infer cell-specific models, we developed two 54 complementary inverse methods: sensitivity fingerprinting derived from mechanistic model sensitivities 55 and a neural network trained on synthetic phosphoinositide time series. Both approaches reproduced 56 experimental PI(4)P, PI(4,5)P 2, and IP3 dynamics across cell types while preserving the baseline model 57 structure. Importantly, the inferred models predicted experimentally observed differential vulnerability 58 to kinase perturbation without additional fitting. Hippocampal neurons with large basal pools of PI(4)P 59 maintained PI(4,5)P 2 and IP 3 signaling under phosphatidylinositol 4-kinase alpha (PI4KA) inhibition, 60 whereas cells with small basal PI(4)P pools exhibited signaling failure. Simulations of PI4KA and 61 phosphatidylinositol-4-phosphate 5-kinase type 1 gamma (PIP5K1C) loss-of-function mutations under 62 repeated stimulation further revealed progressive signaling collapse in small-pool neurons but sustained 63 function in large-pool neurons, demonstrating that basal lipid composition can determine genetic 64 vulnerability. Together, this NAM provides a predictive, cell-specific framework for translating dynamic 65 lipid measurements into mechanistic models that support precision medicine applications in 66 phosphoinositide-related disorders. 67 68 69 70 71 72 73 74 75 76

Introduction 84 Precision medicine aims to tailor treatment by accounting for patient-to-patient variation in genes, 85 proteins, and cellular physiology, differences that are averaged in traditional approaches. Achieving this 86 goal requires not only richer molecular measurements but also dynamic, mechanistic models that can 87 translate measurements into individualized predictions of biological response. Computational New 88 Approach Methodologies (NAMs) provide a path toward this translation by replacing animal 89 experiments with predictive, data-driven modeling frameworks that can ultimately be applied to patient 90 and cell-specific inference. This need is acute in lipid signaling disorders, where nonlinear pathway 91 dynamics and chemically diverse lipid intermediates make mechanistic interpretation difficult without 92 explicit dynamic models (Zandl-Lang et al., 2023). Accordingly, lipid signaling represents both a major 93 challenge and a major opportunity for precision medicine. 94 Lipidomics represents a critical frontier for precision medicine. Phosphatidylinositol 4,5 bisphosphate 95 (PI(4,5)P 2 ) is fundamental to this effort because it acts as a central regulator of cellular signaling, 96 membrane dynamics, and metabolism, and these processes can vary substantially across individuals 97 (Balla, 2013; Viaud et al., 2016). Lipid dysregulation contributes to diseases including cancer, 98 neurodegeneration, and metabolic disorders, making lipid pathways valuable both as biomarkers and as 99 therapeutic targets (Burke et al., 2023). However, lipids remain challenging to study in practice because 100 of their structural diversity, transient dynamics, and strong context dependence, requiring sophisticated 101 tools and multidisciplinary strategies. 102 PI(4,5)P 2 plays a central role in plasma membrane signaling through a tightly coupled and highly 103 dynamic metabolic network. PI(4,5)P 2 synthesis proceeds through sequential phosphorylation of 104 phosphatidylinositol (PI) to phosphatidylinositol-4-phosphate (PI(4)P) and then to PI(4,5)P 2, catalyzed 105 by PI 4-kinase (PI4K) and PIP 5-kinase (PIP5K), respectively, with each step consuming ATP. PI is the 106 most abundant phosphoinositide and serves as the precursor for the entire pathway; the PI pool is 107 maintained through resynthesis in the endoplasmic reticulum and transport to the plasma membrane via 108 lipid transfer proteins at membrane contact sites (Grabon et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2015; Pemberton et al., 109 2020). PI(4)P functions as an obligate intermediate whose concentration is dynamically coupled to that 110 of PI(4,5)P 2, such that changes in flux through PI(4)P rapidly propagate to PI(4,5)P 2. During receptor-111 mediated activation of phospholipase C (PLC), PI(4)P and PI(4,5)P 2 are depleted in parallel on a 112 seconds timescale, reflecting the continuous consumption of PI(4)P to sustain PI(4,5)P 2 synthesis while 113 PI(4,5)P 2 is simultaneously hydrolyzed by PLC (Falkenburger et al., 2010; Willars et al., 1998; Xu et al., 114 2003). Hydrolysis of PI(4,5)P 2 generates diacylglycerol (DAG) and inositol trisphosphate (IP3), two 115 second messengers with distinct temporal and spatial dynamics: IP 3 diffuses rapidly through the 116 cytoplasm to trigger calcium release from intracellular stores, whereas DAG remains membrane-117 associated and activates protein kinase C), two second messengers that propagate downstream responses 118 (Berridge, 1993, 2009; Berridge & Irvine, 1984, 1989). Because IP 3 has a short half-life (approximately 119 5-30 seconds) (Falkenburger et al., 2010), its concentration tracks the instantaneous rate of PLC activity 120 rather than cumulative production, making it a direct readout of pathway flux. Although PI(4,5)P 2 plays 121 a structural role in maintaining the compositional and functional organization of the inner leaflet of the 122 plasma membrane and supporting membrane trafficking, ion channel function, and cytoskeletal 123 organization (Hille et al., 2015; Dickson and Hille, 2019), the pathway is fundamentally governed by 124 rapid turnover and tightly regulated feedback. The phosphoinositide pathway is inherently nonlinear 125

As a result, identical changes in kinase activity can generate markedly different signaling 129 outcomes depending on the starting lipid composition of the cell. These nonlinear dynamics make the 130 outcomes of genetic or pharmacological perturbations difficult to predict without explicit quantitative 131 models. 132 Here, we utilized measurements of PI(4)P, PI(4,5)P 2 , and IP 3 with sufficient temporal resolution for 133 kinetic parameter extraction across multiple cell systems: superior cervical ganglion (SCG) neurons 134 (Kruse et al., 2016), tsA201 cells (Dickson et al., 2013), human neuroblastoma cells (Willars et al., 135 1998), and hippocampal neurons (de la Cruz et al., 2022). Despite differences in cell type, receptor 136 systems, and experimental protocols, the datasets revealed a qualitatively consistent pathway 137 architecture: rapid PI(4)P and PI(4,5)P 2 depletion, coordinated IP 3 production, and gradual recovery, all 138 following the same sequence of biochemical reactions. However, the magnitude and kinetics of these 139 responses varied substantially across cell types, reflecting quantitative differences in enzyme expression, 140 lipid pool sizes, and cellular geometry. The shared pathway topology suggested that a common 141 biochemical architecture governs phosphoinositide metabolism, with quantitative differences arising 142 from systematic parameter variation rather than distinct pathway organization. The observation 143 motivated a strategy: optimize a kinetic model to one well-characterized reference dataset, then use 144 sensitivity analysis to guide parameter variation across other cell types. 145 We next developed a NAM that transforms optimized kinetic models into cell-specific inference tools 146 through systematic sensitivity analysis. The framework integrates ordinary differential equations with 147 multi-start Nelder-Mead optimization (Hernandez-Hernandez et al., 2024; Kernik et al., 2019; Moreno et 148 al., 2016) to establish a reference baseline, then employs local and global Partial Rank Correlation 149 Coefficient (PRCC) sensitivity analysis to map how individual kinetic parameters control distinct 150 signaling features. The sensitivity relationships define a low-dimensional parameter structure that 151 supports three complementary analyses: population modeling, sensitivity-guided inference of cell-152 specific kinetic parameters, and neural network-based parameter estimation from phosphoinositide time-153 series data. This integration of mechanistic modeling and machine learning provides an interpretable and 154 scalable framework for constructing cell-specific phosphoinositide models. 155 156 We applied the framework to phosphoinositide signaling by optimizing a kinetic model for SCG 157 neurons, then extending it across cell types. The analysis revealed that basal lipid pool composition 158 determines signaling robustness and disease vulnerability. Simulations of loss-of-function mutations in 159 PI4KA and PIP5K1C, lipid kinases implicated in neurodevelopmental and neuromuscular disorders 160 (Burke et al., 2023; Narkis et al., 2007; Salter et al., 2021; Verdura et al., 2021), showed that identical 161 genetic perturbations produce severe signaling impact in cells with small lipid reserves but allow partial 162 compensation in cells with large reserves. The cell type-dependent predictions demonstrate how 163 computational NAMs can translate genetic variants into mechanistic predictions by linking genotype to 164 cellular phenotype. While demonstrated here with established cell lines, the NAM could be extended to 165 enable patient-specific kinetic fingerprinting: measuring phosphoinositide dynamics in patient-derived 166 cells, inferring cell-specific parameters via sensitivity-guided analysis, and predicting vulnerability to 167 genetic or pharmacological perturbations. 168

Results 172 

173 We developed a New Approach Methodology (NAM) to transform an optimized kinetic signaling model 174 into a mechanistic inference framework, enabling systematic analysis of cell-specific variability. 175 Conceptually, the framework addresses four questions: What is the baseline signaling behavior? Which 176 parameters control observable features? How do those parameters differ between cell types? Do those 177 inferred differences predict responses to perturbation? The overall strategy follows four steps: (1) 178 optimize a baseline kinetic model to a reference cell type; (2) use sensitivity analysis to map which 179 parameters control which observable features; (3) use that mapping to infer cell-type-specific models 180 from experimental time series, either manually or via a neural network; and (4) test whether the inferred 181 models correctly predict the effects of genetic perturbations. We demonstrate this strategy using 182 phosphoinositide signaling to generate cell-specific models relevant to precision medicine applications. 183 

184 We constructed a kinetic model of PI(4)P, PI(4,5)P 2 , and IP 3 dynamics using differential equations 185 governed by six enzymatic rate constants constrained by biochemical conservation. The model 186 represents lipid phosphorylation, dephosphorylation, PLC-mediated hydrolysis, and IP 3 degradation, 187 while explicitly accounting for IP 3 biosensor binding (Figure 1) . IP 3 is measured experimentally with 188 the LIBRAvIII biosensor, which is a genetically encoded fluorescent reporter that binds free IP 3 and 189 sequesters a fraction it. The model accounts for the sequestration by converting free IP 3 to sensor-bound 190 IP 3 , to allow model comparison with experimental fluorescence signals (Figure 1B) . Unless otherwise 191 noted, IP 3 traces throughout represent biosensor-bound IP 3 (IP 3 -LIBRAvIII), denoted as IP 3 *, which is 192 the experimentally measured quantity in most datasets. PI(4)P and PI(4,5)P 2 model outputs are 193 compared directly with experimentally measured lipid reporter signals across all cell types. 194 195 The model was optimized using experimental time-course data from superior cervical ganglion (SCG) 196 neurons ( Figure 2A ), where 10 μM Oxo-M applied for 20s activates muscarinic M1 receptors and 197 drives PLC-mediated hydrolysis of PI(4,5)P 2, producing coordinated depletion of PI(4)P and PI(4,5)P 2198 and generation of IP 3. Six kinetic rate constants (k PI4K , k 4P , k PIP5K , k 5P , k PLC , and k deg ) were estimated 199 using the Nelder-Mead optimization algorithm ( Table 1 ). The optimized model reproduced the timing 200 and magnitude of PI(4)P ( Figure 2A-i ), PI(4,5)P 2 (Figure 2A-ii ), and IP 3 (Figure 2A-iii ), transients 201 observed experimentally. The model reproduced experimental dynamics with normalized root mean 202 square errors of 8.9% for PI(4)P, 3.2% for PI(4,5)P 2 , and 4.5% for IP 3, well below the 10% threshold 203 typically considered acceptable for biological time-series fitting. 204 205 To validate the model, we tested dose-dependent PI(4,5)P 2 responses using a different dataset not used 206 in optimization ( Figure 2B ). Agonist concentrations were simulated by adjusting PLC activity levels 207 (PLC 1 = 0.15 for 1.0 μM and PLC 1 = 0.7 for 10 μM Oxo-M) while holding all kinetic parameters 208 constant. The model accurately reproduced experimentally observed PI(4,5)P 2 depletion measured via 209 IK(M) (KCNQ2/3) tail currents (Kruse et al., 2016) ( Figure 2B-ii ), with approximately 60% depletion 210 at 1.0 μM Oxo-M and 80-90% depletion at 10 μM Oxo-M. The validation supports that six parameters 211 are sufficient to capture the signaling in SCG neurons under different stimulation protocols. The model 212 also captured dose-dependent IP 3 dynamics ( Figure 2B-iii ), explicitly accounting for IP 3 biosensor 213 (LIBRAvIII) binding by distinguishing between free and biosensor-bound IP 3 pools, reproducing both 214 the magnitude and kinetics of IP 3 accumulation and clearance across different agonist concentrations. 215

The ±20% 218 parameter variation range was selected based on reported coefficients of variation in phosphoinositide 219 pool sizes and kinase activity levels across individual cells of the same type, which typically fall within 220 15-25% (Kruse et al., 2016). This range reflects within-cell-type biological variability rather than cross-221 cell-type differences, and its appropriateness is supported by the observation that SCG neuron model 222 variants within ±20% captured the experimental variability in that reference dataset. Sensitivity metrics 223 were extracted for three experimentally observable features: minimum PI(4)P, PI(4,5)P 2 recovery, and 224 peak IP 3* amplitude. 225 226 Local sensitivity analysis used a one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) approach to quantify the isolated effect of 227 each parameter, varying each parameter ±20% independently, while holding all other parameters 228 constant. Minimum PI(4)P was affected by k PI4K and k 4P (Figure 3A-i ), with similar effects from k PIP5K 229 and k 5P (Figure 3B-i ) but minimal effects due to k PLC and k deg (Figure 3C-i ). Discussion 345 

346 Here, we present a computational framework that integrates mechanistic modeling, sensitivity analysis, 347 and parameter inference to predict cell-specific signaling behavior, which together comprise a novel 348 NAM (new approach methodology). Using phosphoinositide signaling as a proof of concept, this work 349 demonstrates how dynamic biochemical measurements can be translated into cell-specific predictive 350 models, provide new disease insights, and serve as a potential tool for precision medicine. 351 352

9We first validated a kinetic model of PI, PI(4)P, PI(4,5)P 2, and IP 3 signaling ( Figure 1 ). The model 353 includes only the enzymatic steps required to reproduce experimentally measured dynamics and 354 conserves lipid mass across pathway components. Kinetic parameters were optimized and validated 355 using independent experimental datasets from superior cervical ganglion neurons ( Figure 2A, B ). 356 Agreement between model predictions and experimental measurements provided a stable and 357 biologically grounded foundation for subsequent analyses. 358 359 Local and global PRCC sensitivity analysis revealed how kinetic parameters subtly control different 360 aspects of signaling, rather than a dominant rate-limiting step ( Figures 3 and 4 ). PI(4)P depletion, 361 PI(4,5)P 2 recovery, and IP 3 production were governed by overlapping but distinct subsets of rate 362 constants ( Figure 3A-E ). Building on this structure, we tested whether sensitivity analysis could guide 363 inference of cell-specific parameters from experimental data. Bounded parameter variation (±20%) 364 generated a population of models that captured phosphoinositide dynamics across multiple cell types 365 without refitting the model ( Figure 5 ). 366 367 We demonstrated two approaches to determine rate parameters in the model ( Figure 6 ). One method 368 used sensitivity analysis to identify the most important parameters that needed to be adjusted to match 369 the data. The other method used a neural network to automatically estimate rates from the data. Both 370 methods were able to closely match the measured changes over time in PI(4)P, PI(4,5)P 2 , and IP 3 in 371 three different cell types (tsA201 cells, neuroblastoma cells, and hippocampal neurons). Because the two 372 approaches gave similar results, this suggests that time-based measurements of lipid signaling contain 373 enough information to uncover real differences in the underlying biology, assuming that model structure 374 is plausible. The convergence of the two methods also provides practical evidence of parameter 375 identifiability: when independent approaches starting from different assumptions recover consistent 376 parameter sets, it reduces concern that the data are compatible with a broad family of degenerate 377 solutions. Formal identifiability analysis remains an important caveat, however. The six-parameter 378 model is deliberately parsimonious, and some parameter combinations that control similar observables 379 (for example, k PI4K and k 4P both influence minimum PI(4)P) may be partially compensatory. We note 380 that sensitivity analysis explicitly maps which parameters are distinguishable through specific 381 observables, providing a practical guide to which parameters can be reliably inferred from a given 382 dataset. For the neural network inference method, the experimentally measured PI/PI(4)P ratio for each 383 cell type was supplied as a fixed input channel, and network predictions were refined by steady-state 384 correction to ensure biologically consistent pre-stimulus conditions. 385 386 We next simulated disease-relevant perturbations in PI4KA and PIP5K1C ( Figures 7 and 8 ) by 387 implementing variants as a reduction in k PI4K or k PIP5K , to predict how naturally occurring human genetic 388 mutations may alter lipid synthesis by reducing enzyme catalytic activity or lipid abundance, or by 389 impairing membrane recruitment or protein complex stability. Importantly, experimental perturbation of PI4KA shows that it is required to maintain 393 plasma-membrane PI(4,5)P 2 during strong PLC-coupled receptor stimulation. 394 395 A key prediction from the model simulations indicates the role of basal lipid pool composition in 396 determining genetic vulnerability. While the simulations presented here focus on loss-of-function mutations, 410 PIP5K1C gain-of-function variants have also been reported and are associated with excessive IP 3411 production and pathological calcium signaling (Morleo et al., 2023). Extension of the framework to 412 simulate gain-of-function conditions suggests uncontrolled IP 3 generation as a potential mechanism for 413 disrupted signaling and cellular dysfunction. 414 415 One future opportunity is to address the limitation that the model presented herein does not explicitly 416 account for the energetic regulation of kinase activity. Because plasma-membrane PI(4)P and PI(4,5)P 2417 can be depleted rapidly under hypoxia and ATP inhibition, the energetic state represents an additional 418 axis of biochemical regulation captured by k PI4K and k PIP5K (Lu et al., 2022). In excitable tissues, ATP 419 can fluctuate on rapid timescales (including beat-resolved ATP dynamics in cardiac myocytes), raising 420 the possibility that ATP microdomains modulate phosphoinositide resynthesis capacity during repeated 421 demand (Rhana et al., 2024). Future extensions incorporating energetic constraints would complement 422 established lipid-homeostatic feedback, such as PIP4K-dependent restraint of PI(4,5)P 2 synthesis by 423 PIP5Ks (Wang et al., 2019; Wills et al., 2023). 424 425 Together, the work demonstrates a New Approach Methodology (NAM) in which mechanistic 426 modeling, sensitivity analysis, and constrained inference translate dynamic lipid measurements into 427 interpretable, cell-specific kinetic parameters for modeling and simulation. In a precision-medicine 428 context, the framework naturally supports a workflow in which a patient genotype is paired with time-429 resolved PI, PI(4)P, PI(4,5)P 2, and IP 3 measurements in patient-derived cells to infer effective changes 430 resulting from genetic perturbation. The arrival of increasingly sophisticated biosensors makes this 431 plausible in the not-too-distant future. More broadly, the increasing therapeutic interest in 432 phosphoinositide kinases underscores the value of quantitative, context-aware models for connecting 433 variant mechanisms to predicted signaling phenotypes. 434

Nelder-Mead Parameter Optimization of the Phosphoinositide Signaling Model 481 

482 The Nelder-Mead method was used to estimate the kinetic parameters governing the time-dependent 483 behavior of the phosphoinositide intermediates PI(4)P, PI(4,5)P 2 , and IP 3 . Nelder-Mead is a derivative-free optimization method 485 well-suited for biological parameter estimation problems where the cost function landscape contains 486 multiple local minima (Hernandez-Hernandez et al., 2024; Kernik et al., 2019). 487 488 The cost function for optimization was defined as the total sum of squared errors (SSE) across all time 489 points: 490

Local Sensitivity Analysis 509 

510 We performed one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) local sensitivity analysis to quantify how individual 511 parameter perturbations affect model outputs. Global Sensitivity Analysis 525 

526 To assess parameter interactions and system robustness beyond one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) analysis, 527 we performed global sensitivity analysis by simultaneously varying all six kinetic parameters (k PI4K , k 4P ,528 k PIP5K , k 5P , k PLC , k deg ), each sampled uniformly in log-space across the ±20% range used for local 529 analysis. We generated 5,000 parameter sets using Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS), a space-filling 530 quasi-random sampling method that ensures efficient coverage of multidimensional parameter space by 531 dividing each dimension into N equal-probability intervals, sampling one value per interval, then 532 permuting samples across dimensions to minimize correlation. 533 To quantify parameter importance in the global analysis, we calculated Partial Rank Correlation 534 Coefficients (PRCCs) between each parameter and output metrics to measure monotonic association 535 strength after removing linear effects of all other parameters. PRCC values range from −1 to +1, where 536 values near ±1 indicate strong monotonic relationships and values near 0 indicate weak or complex non-537 monotonic effects. 538 

Neural Network Parameter Estimation 539 To enable rapid parameter estimation from experimental time-series data, we trained a hybrid 540 convolutional neural network-long short-term memory (CNN-LSTM) neural network to predict kinetic 541 parameters from normalized PI(4)P, PI(4,5)P2, and IP 3 trajectories. Convolutional layers extract local 542 temporal features while LSTM layers capture long-range sequential dependencies across the full-time 543 course. The network comprised two 1D convolutional layers (64 and 128 filters, kernel size 5) with 544 batch normalization and max pooling for temporal feature extraction, followed by a 2-layer LSTM (128 545 hidden units) to capture sequential dynamics, and three fully connected layers (128, 64, 5 units) for 546 parameter prediction. The experimentally measured PI/PI(4)P ratio for each cell type, derived from basal 547 lipid pool compositions, was supplied as a fixed fifth input channel rather than treated as a predicted 548 output. The network predicted five kinetic parameters (k PI4K , k PIP5K , k 5P , k PLC , k deg ), with k 4P derived 549 analytically as k 4P = (PI/PI(4)P ratio) × kPI4K. 550 Training data consisted of 50,000 simulated trajectories generated using Latin Hypercube Sampling 551 across parameter ranges spanning ±40–60% of baseline values for the five kinetic parameters, with the 552 PI/PI(4)P ratio sampled uniformly from 3 to 50. This range captured parameter variations observed in 553 cell types beyond SCG neurons, particularly hippocampal neurons and neuroblastoma cells . Variable 554 stimulus durations (20, 50, or 60s) matching experimental conditions were encoded as an additional 555 input channel. Each simulation trajectory was interpolated to 100 time points over 300s, with each 556 species (PI(4)P, PI(4,5)P2, IP 3) normalized independently to its maximum value within that trajectory 557 and paired with its corresponding parameter set as ground truth. 558 Training used log10-transformed parameters with mean squared error loss, Adam optimizer (learning 559 rate 0.001, weight decay 10 −5 ), batch size 64, and learning rate scheduling using a 90/10 train-validation 560
