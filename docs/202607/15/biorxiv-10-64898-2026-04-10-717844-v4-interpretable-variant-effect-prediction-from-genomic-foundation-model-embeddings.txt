## Abstract 1

Scientific foundation models learn high-dimensional repre-2

sentations from diverse data modalities, yet what they en-3

code and how to extract that knowledge remain open ques-4

tions. Here we show that probing the internal represen-5

tations of Evo 2, a 7-billion-parameter genomic foundation 6

model, enables accurate and interpretable genetic variant 7

effect prediction. We introduce a covariance-based probe 8

that captures second-order structure from Evo 2 sequence 9

embeddings to predict variant pathogenicity across vari-10 

ant types and functional consequences, matching or ex-11 

ceeding specialized predictors within their domains. To 12 

ground these predictions in known biological mechanisms, 13 

we train a complementary panel of probes on existing an-14 

notations to detect which genomic properties are disrupted 15 

by a variant. This categorized evidence is then integrated 16 

with each variant’s genomic context through a language 17 

model to generate variant-specific mechanistic hypothe-18 

ses. Our pathogenicity predictions correlate with experi-19 

mental measures of variant function, clinical penetrance, 20 

and biobank disease associations while the mechanistic hy-21 

potheses are consistent with expert reviews, known mecha-22 

nism classes, and downstream molecular readouts. We re-23 

lease pathogenicity scores, disruption profiles, and contex-24 

tualized interpretations for 4.2 million variants from the Clin-25 

Var database as an open resource through the Evo Variant 26 

Effect Explorer (EVEE). More broadly, this structured prob-27 

ing approach offers a general framework for interrogating 28 

foundation models across scientific disciplines and ground-29 

ing their outputs in existing domain concepts. 30 

## Main 31 

Scientific foundation models trained on biological sequence 32 

data encode substantial information about the systems they 33 

represent [1, 2], yet two questions remain central to their 34 

translational utility: what have these models learned, and 35 

how can that knowledge be extracted in a form that is both 36 

predictive and interpretable? Few problems test this ques-37 

tion more directly, or carry higher stakes, than genomic vari-38 

ant effect prediction. The task requires estimating whether 39 

a genetic variant causes disease and identifying its biolog-40 

ical mechanism to support clinical interpretation. This re-41

mains a major bottleneck in genomic medicine, as DNA se-42 

quencing routinely surfaces more variants than can be inter-43 

preted [3, 4] with most ambiguously classified as Variants of 44 

Uncertain Significance (VUS) [5, 6]. 45 

Despite a proliferation of variant-effect predictors—protein 46 

language models (EVE [7], ESM2 [1], AlphaMissense [8]), 47 

supervised sequence-to-function models (Enformer [9], 48 

Borzoi [10], Nucleotide Transformer v3 [11, 12], Al-49 

phaGenome [13]), evolutionary DNA models (GPN-50 

MSA [14], Evo 2 [2]), and classical meta-predictors 51 

(CADD [15])—two related limitations persist. First, they 52 

are fragmented across biological regimes: protein-based 53 

methods are largely restricted to missense substitutions, 54 

sequence-to-function models focus primarily on regulatory 55 

effects, and genome-wide meta-predictors compress het-56 

erogeneous evidence into a single score [13, 15, 16]. Sec-57 

ond, most methods provide a prediction without a mecha-58 

nism. For clinical use, a score is rarely sufficient on its own; 59 

variant classification relies on categorized evidence for why 60 

a variant is damaging, as formalized in frameworks such as 61 

the ACMG/AMP guidelines [6]. 62 

These limitations need not be intrinsic to foundation mod-63 

els. A single genome-wide model represents coding and 64 

non-coding sequence alike in one shared space suggesting 65 

the potential to unify prediction across variant classes. The 66 

challenge lies in how to query these signals. Variant effects 67 

are commonly inferred from output likelihoods: the change 68 

in probability a model assigns to a sequence when a variant 69 

is introduced. This is powerful, but it collapses the internal 70 

state of multi-billion-parameter networks into a single score 71 

with documented failure modes [2, 17]. The internal rep-72 

resentations are far richer. They capture evolutionary con-73 

straints across the genome and organize sequences into in-74 

terpretable biological features, including gene structure and 75 

functional protein elements [2, 12, 18]. We therefore asked 76 

whether systematically probing these representations could 77 

recover predictive power beyond likelihood scoring and the 78 

mechanistic information absent from scalar predictors. 79 

Here we show that structured probing of Evo 2, a 7-billion-80 

parameter genomic foundation model, answers this ques-81 

tion: its representations yield variant-effect predictions that 82 

are simultaneously accurate and biologically interpretable. 83

## Results 94 

A single covariance probe matches or exceeds special-95 

ized predictors across variant types 96 

To predict pathogenicity, we probed Evo 2 representations 97 

directly, passing each variant’s reference and alternate se-98 

quences through the model and computing the difference in 99 

per-position embeddings. Rather than mean-pooling these 100 

differences, we compute a learned low-rank covariance of 101 

the embedding differences from both sequence directions, 102 

capturing second-order structure—feature co-occurrences 103 

across positions—that mean pooling discards [19–21] (Fig-104 

ure 1a; Supplementary Figures S1–S3). The result-105 

ing predictions are well calibrated across 833,970 single-106 

nucleotide variants (SNVs) from ClinVar, a public archive of 107 

clinically reported variant classifications (Methods; Supple-108 

mentary Figure S4). We refer to this calibrated pathogenic-109 

ity prediction as the EVEE score, after the Evo Variant Ef-110 

fect Explorer (EVEE), the open resource through which we 111 

release the system. 112 

Variant consequence type is a strong determinant of ex-113 

pected pathogenicity rates: in this dataset, 98.3% of non-114 

sense variants are pathogenic, compared with 0.1% of syn-115 

onymous variants. An aggregate AUROC (0.997 across all 116 

SNVs) is therefore largely confounded by a model’s ability 117 

to recover consequence-type priors rather than variant-level 118 

discrimination [18, 22]. To account for this, we evaluated 119 

performance within each consequence type, using gene-120 

level holdout so that no gene was shared between train 121 

and test sets (Methods). Stratified this way, our covariance 122 

probe discriminates pathogenic from benign variants (rang-123 

ing from AUROC 0.900 for nonsense variants to 0.984 for 124 

intronic; n ranges 3,498–292,369; Figure 1b), outperform-125 

ing Evo 2 loss-based likelihood scoring, CADD, GPN-MSA, 126 

NTv3, and AlphaGenome across consequence types and 127 

matching AlphaMissense, the missense-specialized com-128 

parator, on missense variants. It also outperformed a mean-129 

pooled probe trained on the same Evo 2 embeddings, iso-130 

lating covariance structure as a major source of the gain. 131 

To further remove consequence-type priors, we evaluated 132 

performance on a deconfounded benchmark that balances 133 

pathogenic and benign labels within each consequence 134 

type (consequence-only accuracy 96% →73%; Supplemen-135 

tary Figure S5). The Evo 2 covariance probe remained com-136 

petitive, matching or exceeding the strongest available com-137 

parator across consequence types (Figure 1c). Finally, al-138 

though trained only on SNVs, the probe generalized zero-139 

shot to 73,961 indels, a variant class that many existing 140 

methods fail to accommodate, outperforming CADD v1.7 141 

across indel types (overall AUROC 0.991 vs. 0.980; Fig-142 

ure 1d). Performance was notably lower for frameshifts 143 

(0.772), a weakness likely exacerbated by severe class im-144 

balance (98.7% pathogenic). 145 

Predictions generalize beyond conservation to held-out 146 

functional assays 147 

High accuracy on ClinVar could reflect a coarser signal— 148 

evolutionary conservation, which flags positions where vari-149 

ation is rarely tolerated but says little about the specific vari-150 

ant, and predictors that rely heavily on it can lose variant-151 

level resolution where conservation is highest. Across 152 

the conservation spectrum (phyloP), the covariance probe 153 

maintained high AUROC from fast-evolving to highly con-154 

served sites, whereas CADD and GPN-MSA degraded 155 

at the extremes (Figure 2a), indicating that Evo 2 repre-156 

sentations encode functional information beyond sequence 157 

conservation alone. UMAP visualization of reference-158 

subtracted Evo 2 embeddings further showed that the 159 

learned representation space organizes variants by both 160 

pathogenicity and consequence type (Figure 2b,c). 161 

As a direct experimental validation beyond ClinVar, we eval-162 

uated transfer to deep mutational scan (DMS) datasets— 163 

high-throughput assays that measure the functional effect 164 

of thousands of variants in a gene—for four genes held out 165 

from probe training: BRCA1 [23], BRCA2 [24], TP53 [25], 166 

and LDLR [26] (Figure 2d). We measured correlation be-167 

tween model predictions and continuous DMS functional 168 

scores (Spearman |ρ|), comparing Evo 2 mean-pooled and 169 

covariance-pooled probes with CADD, AlphaMissense, and 170 

Evo 2 loss-based scoring. Across all four genes, Evo 2 171 

embedding probes outperformed loss-based scoring. The 172 

covariance probe was competitive with AlphaMissense and 173 

CADD: it led on LDLR ( |ρ| = 0 .41 versus 0.34 for AlphaMis-174 

sense and 0.32 for CADD), matched AlphaMissense on 175 

BRCA1 ( |ρ| = 0 .55 ) and TP53 ( |ρ| = 0 .70 ), and was com-176 

parable to CADD on BRCA2 ( |ρ| = 0 .44 versus 0.46 ). Co-177 

variance probes consistently outperformed mean-pooled 178 

probes, corroborating the ClinVar results and confirming 179 

that second-order embedding structure captures functional 180 

signal lost under mean pooling. Together, these results 181 

show that Evo 2 representations generalize from clinical la-182 

bels to experimental measures of variant effects. 183

reference and alternate sequences, these probes measure 191 

how a variant changes each property, yielding a disruption 192 

profile which we pass to a language model to synthesize 193

## Discussion 343 

This work establishes that representations from a genomic 344 

foundation model can support both accurate variant effect 345 

prediction and mechanistic interpretation. The central re-346 

sult is not simply that a probe improves performance over 347 

likelihood scoring, but that both prediction and interpreta-348 

tion can be obtained from the same underlying represen-349 

tations. Our covariance probe reads second-order repre-350 

sentation changes to produce accurate pathogenicity esti-351 

mates across SNVs, indels, coding, splicing, and proximal 352 

non-coding effects. The annotation probes read named bio-353 

logical properties from the same representation space, pro-354 

ducing disruption profiles that explain how a variant is pre-355 

dicted to act. Together, these results reframe interpretability 356 

not as a post hoc compromise on accuracy, but as a way 357 

to expose the biological structure that makes the prediction 358 

possible. 359 

The approach also defines a general translation layer be-360 

tween specialized scientific foundation models and general-361 

purpose reasoning systems. Evo 2’s embeddings are not 362 

themselves interpretable to a clinician or researcher; nor 363 

can a language model natively reason over billions of hid-364 

den activations. Annotation probes bridge this gap by con-365 

verting latent representation changes into a compact panel 366 

of named, domain-grounded evidence. A language model 367 

can then integrate that evidence with variant metadata to 368 

generate mechanistic hypotheses aligned with clinical inter-369 

pretation frameworks. In this sense, the disruption profile 370 

functions as an evidence interface, making the foundation 371 

model’s internal knowledge legible and usable without re-372 

quiring the reasoner to access the raw model state. 373 

The validation suite supports the claim that our probes cap-374 

ture true biology rather than ClinVar memorization. Beyond 375 

ClinVar benchmarks, EVEE scores correlate with direct ex-376 

perimental measurements in held-out DMS genes, predict 377 

penetrance among Mayo Clinic Tapestry LDLR carriers, en-378 

rich for disease associations across 2,489 FinnGen end-379 

points, and nominate six candidate disease variants with 380 

sufficient structured ACMG/AMP evidence to support Clin-381 

Var submission. The mechanistic layer is likewise experi-382 

mentally grounded: for BRCA1 c.5278 −1G >A, EVEE pre-383 

dicted activation of a cryptic splice acceptor eight bases into 384 

exon 20, and RNA-seq in a carrier cell line confirmed the 385 

altered splice junction at nucleotide-level resolution. These 386 

orthogonal validations are critical because they test whether 387 

representation-derived predictions transfer from labels to 388 

measurable biological and clinical outcomes. 389 

For all 4.2 million clinically reported ClinVar variants, EVEE 390 

provides a pathogenicity prediction, disruption profile, and 391 

contextualized interpretation, accessible through a web ap-392 

plication (https://evee.goodfire.ai/), bulk download, or a pro-393 

grammatic interface. This resource is intended to sup-394 

port variant prioritization, mechanistic hypothesis genera-395 

tion, and research into how genomic foundation models en-396 

code biological information. It is not a substitute for expert 397 

review or clinical classification, but it provides structured, in-398 

spectable evidence that can be integrated into established 399 

interpretation workflows. 400 

Several limitations warrant discussion. Evo 2’s broad evolu-401 

tionary prior is well suited to many Mendelian disease mech-402 

anisms, but may be less complete for regulatory, epistatic, 403 

or polygenic effects that lack clear evolutionary signatures. 404 

Because the pathogenicity probe is supervised on ClinVar 405 

labels, within-ClinVar benchmarks carry a home-domain ad-406 

vantage over predictors not trained on the same label distri-407 

bution; the DMS, penetrance, RNA-seq, and biobank anal-408 

yses are therefore the stronger evidence for generalization. 409 

The clinical and population-level validations are subject to 410 

cohort and ascertainment constraints, including the small 411 

presymptomatic-carrier class in Tapestry ( n = 16 ) and the 412 

use of a single biobank. The annotation probes are limited 413 

to known, labeled features; unsupervised methods such as 414 

sparse autoencoders applied to genomic foundation-model 415 

representations [2, 29, 30] may reveal additional features 416 

and could be integrated with disruption profiling. The EVEE-417

LLM-based interpretation pipeline 666 

For each variant, the top-k disruptions (ranked by |∆|) were 667 

assembled alongside variant metadata (gene name, con-668 

sequence type, genomic position, LOEUF constraint score) 669 

into a structured prompt. Claude Sonnet 4.6 [36] was 670 

used to synthesize a natural-language interpretation includ-671 

ing: a one-sentence summary of the predicted molecular 672 

mechanism, a detailed explanation contextualizing the dis-673 

ruption profile against known biology, calibration informa-674 

tion (percentage of ClinVar pathogenic variants at similar 675 

pathogenicity scores), and bullet-pointed key evidence. 676 

The system prompt (Table S1) describes the disruption sig-677 

nal framework: a probe trained on reference genome ac-678 

tivations is run on both reference and variant sequences, 679 

producing for each biological feature (helix, conservation, 680 

domain membership, etc.) a reference prediction, a variant 681 

prediction, and a delta (var − ref). The prompt explains that 682 

large negative deltas indicate feature disruption while near-683 

zero deltas indicate preservation, and instructs the LLM to 684 

lead with the disruption story, use gene function context, 685 

and avoid referencing external clinical prediction tools. 686 

The user prompt is assembled programmatically from 687 

variant-specific data. It includes: gene name and variant 688 

coordinates, consequence type, HGVSp/HGVSc notation, 689 

protein domain annotations with LOEUF constraint score, 690 

gene function summaries, calibrated pathogenicity percent-691 

age, a Region Disruptions table (peak probe signal change 692 

across ±32 kb for the top-10 features by |∆| ≥ 0.2, with ref-693 

erence, variant, delta, and distance columns), and a Site 694 

Disruptions table (probe signal change at the variant po-695 

sition for the top-10 features by |∆| ≥ 0.1, with reference, 696 

variant, and delta columns). An example of the full prompt 697

Both include identical writing-style constraints: direct, concise prose; no em dashes; no superlatives or hedging language; no repetition of the variant ID; specific numbers preferred over vague qualifiers. 

Prompt Content 

Baseline (without Evo2) You are a clinical genomics expert. Based on the variant information provided, interpret the likely biological impact and pathogenicity of this variant. Be specific about biological mechanisms when the information supports it. 3–5 sentences for summary, 1 sentence for mechanism. Writing style: Write like a senior geneticist. Be direct and concise. Never use em dashes. Never use “notably”, “critically”, “importantly”, “striking”. Don’t use superlatives or hedging language. State facts plainly. Don’t repeat the variant ID or coordinates in the summary. Specific numbers are good. Vague qualifiers (“profound”, “massive”, “broad”) are not. Return JSON: {summary, mechanism, confidence, key_evidence} Evo2 (with Evo2) You are a clinical genomics expert interpreting variant pathogenicity predictions from Evo2, a 7-billion-parameter DNA foundation model. For each biological feature (helix, conservation, domain membership, etc.), you see: ref (the probe’s prediction on the reference genome), var (the prediction on the variant genome), and ∆ (var − ref, the disruption signal). This is the key signal: pathogenic variants show large disruptions, benign variants show near-zero deltas. Two independent judges (Claude Opus 4.6) evaluate each interpretation. Each judge receives the variant identifier, the LLM-generated interpretation, and ClinVar expert panel evidence as context. 

Judge Prompt 

Mechanism coverage You are evaluating an automated variant interpretation generated by an AI system that uses probe features from a genomic language model (Evo2) and an LLM without internet access. The system has no access to patient data, population frequencies, functional assays, or experimental results. Use the ClinVar expert panel evidence as ground truth for the molecular mechanism; ignore any non-expert submissions. 

Variant: ⟨variant_id ⟩

LLM Interpretation: ⟨interpretation ⟩

ClinVar Expert Panel Evidence (ground truth): ⟨clinvar_evidence ⟩

Mechanism Coverage Rubric (1–5). Score = highest specificity level of mechanism correctly identified, using ClinVar expert panel SCV as ground truth. Only correct claims count. Level 1: Gene-level only. Names gene/disease/pathway but no variant-specific mechanism. Level 2: Correct variant-type. Identifies consequence type and expected effect but not which do-main/residue. Level 3: Correct functional class or protein region. Names the right domain or functional category. Level 4: Specific mechanism with biological reasoning. Connects variant to consequence through biological logic. Level 5: Level 4 + additional biological detail. Second affected function, biophysical basis, downstream pathway, or non-obvious mechanism. 

Judge procedure: (1) Extract mechanism claims from the expert SCV; (2) extract mechanism claims from the LLM interpretation; (3) compare, where correct claims match or are consistent with the SCV and incor-rect claims contradict it; (4) assign the score as the highest rubric level among correct claims. 

Scoring rules: Wrong pathogenicity direction caps the score at 1; hedged claims score at the level de-scribed if correct; missing clinical, population, or experimental data is not penalized. Returns JSON: {mechanism_score: 1–5, reasoning: 2–3 sentence justification}. Biological accuracy & specificity You are evaluating an automated variant interpretation generated by an AI system that uses probe features from a genomic language model (Evo2). 

Variant: ⟨variant_id ⟩

LLM Interpretation: ⟨interpretation ⟩

ClinVar Submitted Evidence (human-curated): ⟨clinvar_evidence ⟩

Compare the LLM interpretation against the ClinVar evidence. Score on two axes (1–5): (1) biological_accuracy : Are the biological claims in the interpretation consistent with the ClinVar evi-dence? (5 = fully consistent, 1 = contradicts evidence) (2) specificity : Does the interpretation make specific, concrete claims rather than generic statements? (5 = very specific to this variant, 1 = could apply to any variant) Returns JSON: {biological_accuracy: 1–5, specificity: 1–5}. 

Table S4. Example interpretation: mechanism score 1 (gene-level only). ETHE1 c.55G>T (chr19:43506794:C:A), classified as benign by the ClinGen Mito Variant Curation Expert Panel, generated at the Evo 2 context configuration by Claude Opus 4.6. The LLM misidentifies the coding variant as a 3’ UTR variant (c.*55G>T), building its entire mechanism reasoning on an incorrect premise. While the benign conclusion is correct, only gene-level identification is accurate. 

Component Content 

LLM summary This 3’ UTR variant in ETHE1 shows a 0% pathogenicity prediction, consistent with the absence of any meaningful disruption to protein-coding or structural features. As a variant located 55 bases past the stop codon, it falls outside the translated region and is not expected to alter protein structure, active site residues, or conserved functional domains. The LOEUF score of 0.979 indicates ETHE1 is relatively tolerant to loss-of-function variation, further reducing concern. The model correctly identifies this as a benign variant given the lack of disruption to any feature relevant to ETHE1’s sulfur dioxygenase or iron-binding functions. LLM mechanism A 3’ UTR variant at this position does not disrupt the coding sequence, protein folding, or any catalytic domain required for ETHE1’s mitochondrial sulfur dioxygenase activity. Expert SCV The filtering allele frequency of the c.55G>T variant in the ETHE1 gene is >0.7% by the Exome Aggregation Consortium, which is a high enough frequency to be classified as benign based on thresholds defined by the ClinGen Mito Variant Curation Expert Panel (BA1). Judge reasoning The LLM correctly identifies the gene (ETHE1) and reaches the correct benign conclusion, but incorrectly classifies the variant as a 3’ UTR variant (c.*55G>T) when the expert panel identifies it as c.55G>T (coding region). Since the variant-type mechanism reasoning is based on an incorrect premise about the variant’s location, the highest correct claim is gene-level identification with correct pathogenicity direction. Table S6. Example interpretation: mechanism score 3 (correct functional class). FOXN1 p.Arg255Ter (chr17:28529156:C:T), classified as pathogenic by the ClinGen SCID VCEP, generated at the Evo 2 context configuration by Claude Opus 4.6. The LLM correctly localizes the nonsense variant to the forkhead DNA-binding domain, but proposes protein truncation with domain loss rather than the expert-annotated nonsense-mediated decay mechanism. 

Component Content 

LLM summary This nonsense variant introduces a premature termination codon at residue 255 in FOXN1, a forkhead box transcription factor essential for thymic epithelial cell development and hair follicle differentiation. The model detects catastrophic loss of the downstream DNA-binding forkhead domain, with homeodomain-like superfamily membership dropping from 0.74 to 0.18 ( ∆ = −0.56) and DNA binding probability collapsing from 0.61 to 0.05 ( ∆ = −0.56). The downstream region transitions from structured (beta strand 0.69 to 0.13) to disordered (0.22 to 0.81), consistent with complete loss of the folded forkhead domain that mediates target gene regulation. FOXN1 is highly intolerant to loss of function (LOEUF = 0.442), and biallelic loss-of-function variants cause nude/SCID phenotype with T-cell immunodeficiency and alopecia, making truncation upstream of the DNA-binding domain unambiguously damaging. LLM mechanism Premature stop codon at position 255 eliminates the forkhead DNA-binding domain, abolishing FOXN1 transcriptional activity required for thymic epithelial and keratinocyte differentiation. Expert SCV The NM_001369369.1(FOXN1):c.763C>T (p.Arg255Ter) nonsense variant occurs in exon 5 of 9 and is pre-dicted to result in nonsense-mediated decay (PVS1). The highest MAF in gnomAD v2.1.1 is 0.000008790 in the European (non-Finnish) population, below the SCID VCEP threshold (PM2_supporting). At least one patient is described with congenital alopecia, nail dystrophy, and very low T cell number, highly specific to FOXN1 deficiency (PP4). ACMG/AMP criteria: PVS1, PM2_supporting, PP4, PP1_strong, PM3. Judge reasoning The LLM correctly identifies the nonsense variant in FOXN1, the forkhead DNA-binding domain as the crit-ical affected region, and provides biological reasoning about loss of transcriptional activity. However, the expert panel explicitly states the primary mechanism is nonsense-mediated decay (PVS1, exon 5 of 9), while the LLM describes protein truncation with domain loss. This mechanistic discrepancy (NMD vs trun-cated protein) prevents scoring at Level 4, but correct domain identification and functional class earns Level 3. PAH p.Leu213Pro (chr12:102855203:A:G), classified as pathogenic by multiple clinical laboratories, generated at the Evo 2 context configuration by Claude Opus 4.6. The LLM connects proline-mediated helix breaking to destabilization of the catalytic domain required for enzymatic activity. 

Component Content 

LLM summary This Leu-to-Pro substitution in the PAH catalytic domain produces a clear alpha helix disruption at the vari-ant site ( ∆ −0.245, from 0.975 to 0.731), with a concurrent gain in beta strand propensity (+0.218) and increased disorder (+0.166). Proline is a well-known helix breaker, and the probe captures exactly this structural consequence: a position that was confidently helical in the reference is shifted toward irregular or strand-like conformation. The variant also increases the predicted protein-protein interaction interface signal (+0.147), suggesting altered surface properties at a region involved in PAH oligomerization or cofac-tor interaction. PAH requires precise active-site geometry for iron coordination and BH4 cofactor binding, and destabilization of a helix within the catalytic domain (PF00351) is a recognized mechanism for loss of phenylalanine hydroxylase activity, consistent with classical PKU. LLM mechanism Proline substitution disrupts an alpha helix in the PAH catalytic domain, increasing local disorder and desta-bilizing the structural framework required for iron binding and enzymatic activity. Expert SCV This sequence change replaces leucine, which is neutral and non-polar, with proline, which is neutral and non-polar, at codon 213 of the PAH protein (p.Leu213Pro). This variant is not present in population databases (gnomAD no frequency). This missense change has been observed in individuals with phenylke-tonuria (PMIDs: 8632937, 9521426, 19292873, 22112818, 23430547). Advanced modeling indicates that this missense variant is expected to disrupt PAH protein function with a positive predictive value of 80%. PAH-specific ACMG/AMP criteria applied: PP4_Moderate, PM3_Very-strong. Judge reasoning The LLM correctly identifies the pathogenic direction, places the variant in the PAH catalytic/hydroxylase domain (PF00351), and provides specific biological reasoning connecting proline-mediated helix disruption to destabilization of the structural framework required for iron binding and enzymatic activity. This is con-sistent with the expert panel’s pathogenic classification and PP3 computational evidence, reaching Level 4 specificity with mechanistic biological logic beyond just naming the domain. 

Table S8. Example interpretation: mechanism score 5 (multi-mechanism with biophysical detail). GAA p.Met1Val (chr17:80104586:A:G), classified as pathogenic by the ClinGen Lysosomal Diseases VCEP for Pompe disease, generated at the Evo 2 context configuration by Claude Opus 4.6. The LLM traces a multi-step biological chain from start codon loss through signal peptide elimi-nation to failed lysosomal targeting, entirely from disruption profile signals. 

Component Content 

LLM summary This variant abolishes the initiator methionine codon of GAA, with the model detecting a start codon prob-ability drop from 0.976 to 0.477 at the variant site and from 0.959 to 0.079 in the peak region (+2 bp). The downstream consequences are severe: the model predicts loss of the signal/transit peptide (0.382 to 0.029), loss of the protein chain (0.863 to 0.230), and near-complete destruction of the transmembrane sig-nal peptide region (0.923 to 0.007 at +48 bp). Without a functional start codon, GAA acid alpha-glucosidase cannot be synthesized or properly targeted to lysosomes, which is the direct cause of Pompe disease (glycogen storage disease type II). The model also detects aberrant CDS and polypyrimidine tract signals upstream, consistent with the translational machinery failing to recognize this region as a normal coding sequence initiation site. LLM mechanism Loss of the initiator methionine codon prevents translation of GAA acid alpha-glucosidase, eliminating the signal peptide required for lysosomal targeting and abolishing enzymatic glycogen degradation. Expert SCV The NM_000152.5:c.1A>G (p.Met1Val) start loss variant in GAA may cause a truncated or absent protein by altering the start codon (PVS1_Strong). This variant is absent in gnomAD v2.1.1 (PM2_Supporting). This variant has been detected in at least 5 individuals with Pompe disease, including three compound heterozygous for another pathogenic variant (PM3_Strong). At least two patients had documented GAA deficiency with <10% of the normal mean control level of GAA activity in dried blood spots. One homozy-gous patient had documented severe infantile onset and was CRIM negative (PP4_Moderate). ACMG/AMP criteria: PVS1_Strong, PM3_Strong, PP4_Moderate, PM2_Supporting. Judge reasoning The LLM correctly identifies the start codon loss in GAA causing absent or truncated protein, matching the SCV’s p.Met1Val start loss, and goes beyond by explaining the biological chain: loss of initiator methionine, loss of signal peptide, failure of lysosomal targeting, abolished glycogen degradation, and Pompe disease. This provides Level 4 biological reasoning (connecting variant to consequence) plus additional biological detail about the signal peptide and lysosomal targeting pathway, reaching Level 5.
