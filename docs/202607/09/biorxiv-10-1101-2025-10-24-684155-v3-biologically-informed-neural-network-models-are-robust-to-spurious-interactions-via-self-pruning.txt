# Abstract 12 

Computational models of cellular networks hold promise to uncover disease mechanisms and 13 

guide therapeutic strategies. Biology -informed neural networks (BINNs) is an emerging approach 14 

to create such models by combin ing the predictive power of deep learning with prior knowledge, 15 

a vital aspect of biological research . The architectures of BINN’s enforces a network structure 16 

from which mechanism can ideally be inferred. However, a key challenge is to evaluate the 17 

reliability of these models , as cells are inherently complex , involving intricate and sometimes 18 

unknown interactions . Currently, analysis mainly focuses on selected pathways rather than a19 

more comprehensive perspective. In this work we demonstrate an alternative holistic approach: 20 

we measure to which extent purposefully introduced spurious interactions are down -weighted by 21 

a BINN during training (self -pruning ). The metric suggested RRA (Relative Residual Area) allows 22 

for direct distribution comparison with perfect self -pruning achieved at zero and a failure to self -23 

prune if above one . To enable rapid testing, we updated LEMBAS (Large -scale knowledge -24 

EMBedded Artificial Signaling -networks), our recurrent neural network framework for intracellular 25 

signaling dynamics, with full GPU acceleration. Our implementation achieves a >7-fold speedup 26 

compared to the original while preserving predictive accuracy. We evaluate d self -pruning in 327 

different datasets and found that when spurious interactions are introduced at random , the model 28 

prunes these to a larger extent than those from the prior knowledge network (PKN), provided the 29 

model is regularized with a sufficiently large L2 norm. This suggests that BINNs can be robust to 30 

uncertainty in the PKN. 31

# In troduction 36 

Intracellular signaling pathways allow cells to process and react to their environment. This 37 

includes transmission of signaling events from ligand -receptor interactions to transcription factors 38 

(TFs) (1) . Disruptions in signaling networks are common in diseases such as cancer (2 –4) , and 39 

therefore, understanding the systemic effects of such perturbations is central to deciphering 40 

disease mechanisms and developing new drugs (5) . However, this is challenging for several 41 

reasons, including the vast combinatorial space of possible interaction in a signaling network, the 42 

non -linearity of these interactions, and inter -pathway signaling interactions across the network (6) .43 

Therefore , computational modeling approaches have high utility for understanding signaling 44 

activity and the potential to transform therapeutic approaches by enabling rapid evaluation of 45 

interventions (5,7) .46 

Traditionally, modeling approaches, such as ordinary differential equations (ODEs) and Boolean 47 

models, have been used to simulate cellular signaling (8) . These models are constructed using 48 

known molecular interactions in cells and thus serve not only as predictive tools but also have 49 

potential to reveal mechanistic relations. However, such models face limitations in scalability and 50 

expressiveness (9 –12) . Specifically, ODE methods require detailed and comprehensive 51 

knowledge of the parameters describing molecular interaction; and when this is not available, 52 

these parameters are highly computationally expensive to infer (13) . Additionally, boolean models 53 

require extensive manual curation and are qualitative rather than quantitative by construction. As 54 

an alternative, artificial neural networks (ANNs) have emerged as a versatile and effective tool for 55 

approximating complex functions, with many biological applications (14) .56 

Recently, ANNs that make use of prior biological knowledge have been developed. The aim of 57 

these models is to enable predictions that are (at least in principle) biologically interpretable and 58 

thereby bridge the gap between predictive accuracy and mechanistic insight. These models are 59 

termed Biologically Informed Neural Networks (BINNs) (5,15) , analogous to Physics -Informed 60 

Neural Networks (PINNs). Whereas PINNs embed physical knowledge (e.g., differential 61 

equations) into their loss functions or model design to ensure physical realism in outputs (16) ,62 

BINNs integrate biological priors such as interaction graphs into the architecture (15) . For 63 

example, we developed a BINN termed LEMBAS  (Large -scale knowledge -EMBedded Artificial 64 

Signaling -networks) that uses a recurrent neural network to simulate intracellular signaling and 65 

explain steady -state TF activities in response to ligands and other perturbations (17) . It embeds 66 

known signaling interactions as structural constraints, mapping weights to real molecular 67 

interactions and setting all others to zero, making predictions interpretable and traceable (17,19) .68 

Unlike conventional deep learning models with randomly initialized parameters with no 69 

correspondence to biological processes, LEMBAS links predictions to molecular mechanisms, 70 

offering a path towards constructing interpretable models of disease such as cancer (5) . And in 71 

general, BINNs has been proven to offer mechanistic insight in many biological tasks (17 –20) ,72 

suggesting that prior knowledge -informed networks could form a foundation for system biology 73 

analysis (17) .74 

While BINNs hold promise to explain and predict biological data, several challenges that are 75 

associated with Graph Neural Networks (GNN) also translate to BINNs. One issue is 76 

misannotation of edges, which may be particularly challenging for biological systems where there 77 

are many methods to infer prior knowledge networks, and where the results can differ markedly, 78

including spurious connections (false positives) as well as missing interactions (false negatives) 79 

(21) . Additionally, cell types differ in the structure of their connectivity due differences in which 80 

pathways they express , annotation of this is limited, thus unrealistic interaction can emerge when 81 

using PKN that don’t separate cell types. Failure to use the correct graph structure may cause 82 

incorrect relations to emerge in the network. We reason that spurious connections may be less 83 

detrimental, as GNN models can (at least in principle) down -weight (prune) interaction but most 84 

implementations cannot introduce new edges (22,23) , which also applies to BINNs (17,18) .85 

However, the propensity of these networks to down weight incorrect edges through training on 86 

experimental data is typically not systematically evaluated. 87 

So far analysis of BINNs has mostly centered on its predictions for specific pathways 88 

(17,19,20,24,25) , rather than on system level network properties. But analysis of the internal 89 

structure of deep learning networks can be a useful tool to understand how the algorithms behave 90 

during training (26,27) . An example of this is Grokking (28) , a phenomenon where after training a91 

model for many epochs following an overfit to the training data, there is rapid progress to a perfect 92 

generalization on the test set. This is hypothesized to be the result of the model finding an efficient 93 

algorithmic -like solution to fit the data (29,30) , and it has been observed that this is accompanied 94 

by a large fraction of the weights approaching zero, suggesting that the algorithmic solution 95 

constitutes a sparse network of strong interactions. We reason that this resembles the structured 96 

sparsity observed in biological networks and that therefore systems level analysis from the 97 

grokking literature may translate to the domain of BINNs. 98 

Here we propose a systems -level , heuristic metric for network soundness that tests if false edges 99 

introduced in the network are suppressed during training, thus mimicking false positives in prior 100 

knowledge. We argue that in practice, performing well on this test is a necessary, but insufficient, 101 

requirement for system -level trust in PKN -based models. To systematically analyze how a BINN 102 

performs on this metric, we implemented a GPU -accelerated version of LEMBAS, that preserves 103 

accuracy while reducing training time. We find that false edges were consistently assigned 104 

negligible weights and were therefore effectively pruned over the course of training across 105 

synthetic and experimental datasets and across hyperparameter settings. While the work is 106 

inspired by grokking, where models shift from memorization to algorithmic solutions, we note that 107 

perfect recovery is unlikely in current BINN formulations, due to their extreme under 108 

parameterization . Additionally, there are structural differences between the formulations that may 109 

influence how training progresses. Indeed , we find that unlike the sharp transitions seen in 110 

grokking, pruning in this setting unfolded gradually. Overall, these results suggest BINNs are 111 

resilient to false interactions, supporting permissive prior inclusion and demonstrating their 112 

potential to infer mechanistic network structure. 113

## False interactions vanish during training 210 

False positives in the context of biological graphs can be viewed as the existence of entries 211 

connecting biomolecules that do not directly interact. It is well known that modern methods to 212 

produce these interaction graphs can produce inconsistent and incorrect results, this manifest as 213 

incorrect signs (activation/inhibition) in interactions (38) , and false positives and false negatives 214 

in the adjacency graph (39) . A neural network that is conditioned on such false interactions may 215 

potentially produce unrealistic structures that fail to model real biological processes. Ideally such 216 

false interactions should be removed during training, and it is standard practice in deep learning 217

## A metric for self -pruning in BINNs 292 

To track performance across training, we saved the model at regular intervals (checkpoints), 293 

along with the initial parameterization before training started. We recorded the weights of true and 294 

false edges and calculated their magnitude (absolute value). By plotting the CDF (Cumulative 295 

Distribution Function) of the weights in the middle checkpoint we find a clear separation between 296 

added and true edges upon visual inspection (Figure 3a, Supplementary Figure S9 ).  To quantify 297 

this separation, we calculate the residual area i.e the area of the upper left corner between the 298 

CDF and the point (0,1.0) a larger area of this type imply tendency towards larger weights, we 299 

compare the distribution of these areas for the true and added edges with a Wilcox test . With this 300 

metric , we find significant self -pruning for all datasets and L2 settings except the two lower L2 301 

settings for the high -coverage dataset (Figure 3b, Supplementary Figure S10 ).  We inspect this 302 

residual area metric across training time , and find that the distributions do not deviat e at the start 303 

of training , and that they begin to deviate as training proceeds , indicat ing that it is the training 304 

dynamics that result in the separation of residual areas  (Figure 3c, Supplementary Figure S11) .305 

Generally, we also see that the dataset and L2 norm affect the overall weight norm of weights in 306 

a trained model. Thus, to compare performance across models we need a metric that accounts 307 

for the model -specific distribution of weights. 308 

For this we introduce the relative residual area (RRA ) to assess the self -pruning effect compared 309 

to the internal weight’s distribution of the model . For this we divide the residual area of added 310 

edges by the residual area of the true edges , introducing an internal baseline that allows 311 

comparison across condition s. This metric has clearly interpretable reference points, an RRA of 312 

zero implies perfect self -pruning, an RRA of 1 implies no self -pruning and an RRA of greater than 313 

one implies that added edges are preferred. Similar to our comparative analysis, using this metric, 314 

we find that a higher L2 tends to lead to a lower RRA and that in seven out of the nine tested 315 

conditions , we observe an RRA lower than one. The lowest RRA is in the synthetic dataset , which 316 

is expected as there is a perfect correspondence between the PKN and the relations expressed 317 

in the data, and the guarantee that the model is not under parameterized (Figure 3d) . Additionally, 318 

we made an analysis of self -pruning when varying the spectral radius  (Supplementary Figure 319 

S1 2-13) and of including the output bias (Supplementary Figure S1 4), observing limited effects 320 

on RRA, .321 

We also investigated if RRA can be used as a heuristic metric indicative of the mechanistic 322 

relevance of learned edge weights . For the synthetic setting where the ground truth is available, 323 

a direct measure can be constructed as the correlation between learned and ground truth weights .324 

We found a correlation between the RRA score and this metric across models (r = –0.379 ,325 

Supplementary Figure S15 ), indicating that lower RRA indeed generally reflects a higher 326 

similarity with ground truth . However , there was also considerable variability between these 327 

scores , indicating that further research into suitable mechanistic proxies could be needed .328 

However , this is only possible to test synthetic models , as there is no ground truth available in the 329 

other setting . For the se we have instead investigated if there is an association between a high 330 

weight and the number of references that support the edge in literature, we indeed find such an 331 

association (Supplementary Figure S1 6),  this shows that more supported edges were more 332

# Discussion: 335 

In this work, we provide evidence that biologically informed neural networks (BINNs) can exhibit 336 

an emergent self -pruning behavior . For this we implement a new GPU version of our BINN 337 

framework LEMBAS and find that it is faster to train and produces comparable results. 338 

Additionally, the GPU implementation appears to generate fewer outliers with poor performance 339 

resulting in better mean performance. This re -implementation aligned the framework better with 340 

more recent methods in machine learning, including support for CUDA and new design choices 341 

such as the gradient noise, paving the way for future work on LEMBAS with an additional speed 342 

up. Together, these improvements enable a systematic investigation of self -pruning as an 343 

emergent property of the training of a BINN, and we test this by stochastically adding false -positive 344 

edges to the PKN and tracking their behavior during training. For this we used a synthetic dataset 345 

with a known ground truth, and on two datasets from stimulated macrophages. Across all 346 

datasets, we found that the distribution of weights learned for false edges changed over the 347 

course of training, and that self -pruning was enhanced by increased levels of L2 regularization. 348 

Pruning and self -pruning is studied in the broader deep learning literature, particularly in the 349 

context of model compression (41)  and as a theoretical tool to understanding how deep learning 350 

models learn (42) . However , its emergence in the context of knowledge -informed network 351

introduced in addition to the true biological structure are being removed throughout training. 370 

A key limitation in comparing BINNs to the systems normally studied in the Grokking literature 371 

stems from the intrinsic complexity of cells. Unlike synthetic tasks with compact solutions, cellular 372 

signaling is not expected to conform to the simplified functional forms of current BINNs such as 373 

LEMBAS. Consequently, despite its utility, self -pruning was not universal: in the high coverage 374 

dataset, the residual -area metric showed no preferential down -weighting of false edges at either 375 

of the two lower L2 regularization levels, despite the latter demonstrating the best predictive 376 

performance. Therefore self -pruning is more likely to be observed in stronger regularization 377 

regimes. Moreover, we reason that instances where self -pruning is not observed mat exemplify 378 

two main limitations: only a subset of all cellular processes are included in the models; and models 379 

naturally make simplifying assumptions that do not holistically capture all the intricacies of the 380 

biological mechanisms they represent . For example, in LEMBAS the contribution of each 381 

interaction is modeled independently, ignoring the interdependent relations between molecules. 382 

Another example is subcellular compartmentalization, which is ignored in LEMBAS but critical in 383 

real cells. As a result, the model is heavily under parameterized and LEMBAS instead uses the 384 

network edge weights to compensate for this lack of complexity . This is supported by the fact that ,385 

when training on synthetic data, the model shows stronger pruning, consistent with its well -386 

parameterized functional structure. It is therefore encouraging that self -pruning emerges despite 387 

these constraints, suggesting that BINNs still learn meaningful approximations to underlying 388 

biological mechanisms. This points to their potential not only as predictive tools but also as 389 

engines for hypothesis testing and rejection in cellular systems. Here, the architectural structure 390 

is more important than the exact parameter count: a large, dense network without biological 391 

grounding, while technically not under parameterized , may fit data but would remain opaque and 392 

less mechanistically informative. So, to meaningfully mirror biological systems and learn 393 

mechanistically plausible representations, BINNs must strike a balance between expressiveness 394 

and structural constraints. 395

As highlighted in many recent articles (5,7,15) , the field of BINNs is in need of robust evaluation 396 

frameworks and systematic strategies for hypothesis generation. We propose that the emergence 397 

of self -pruning holds promise of progress for both challenges. Specifically, the selective removal 398 

of non -contributory or biologically implausible edges during training can serve as an implicit 399 

validation mechanism. A framework that fails to prune added edges and instead relies on them 400 

for prediction, may indicate a failure to replicate biological reality, that would likely extend to false -401 

positive interactions in the prior knowledge network (PKN). Frameworks that promote self -pruning 402 

enable a more permissive initial construction of PKNs that include uncertain interactions, with the 403 

expectation that training will refine this structure. This could be specifically useful in defining cell 404 

type specific interactomes as these interactomes are not fully mapped out and databases often 405 

have limited separation between cell types. Importantly, systems level metrics such as RRA 406 

proposed here provide a simple, architecture -agnostic test that does not rely on manual pathway 407 

curation, unlike many existing tests of biological feasibility. In LEMBAS , the RRA metric serves 408 

as a tool that partially assesses mechanistic relevance of learned edge weight magnitude. 409 

Consequently , the RRA can be modified and extended to use other metrics of edge -importance 410 

tha n weight magnitude , such as integrated gradients (45) , where the distribution s of the saliency 411 

between edges would serve as the feature compared , A limitation of the RRA metric is that it is 412 

defined relative to a reference model which could allow many real interactions to be pruned out 413 

as long as added edges are also pruned. We emphasize that self -pruning alone is limited in scope 414 

and should be combined with complementary metrics to assess how well models approximate 415 

the real mechanism inside of cells . We therefore interpret self -pruning as a system -level indication 416 

of biologically meaningful structure, rather than as direct evidence regarding individual edges. 417 

# Methods 418 

## Da tasets 419 

Throughout this paper we make use of three data sets: a high -coverage (17) , low -coverage (32) 420 

and synthetic data set (17) . The high and low coverage data set are both instances of 421 

macrophages simulated with ligands with a read out of gene expression. The synthetic data set 422 

was created using the CPU implementation of LEMBAS. 423 

## Matrix multiplications and gradient calculations 424 

We replaced the sparse matrix computations that were used in the original implementation of 425 

LEMBAS with dense matrix computations performed on a GPU, using python (v3.12.3) pytorch 426 

(v2.4.0), and cuda (v12.4). Originally, LEMBAS used accelerated sparse matrix computations that 427 

are possible on CPU, but the porting to a GPU required the use of dense operations. To ensure 428 

that only allowed interactions are maintained throughout training with the dense implementation, 429 

we expand our weight -update rule to set non -existing interaction to zero after each weights update 430 

has been performed. To make use of the sparse structure of the prior knowledge network, the 431 

original implementation of LEMBAS used a manually derived implementation of backpropagation 432 

and gradient calculation. The GPU -enabled version instead makes use of the standard autograd 433 

method that is integrated in pytorch. 434

## Spectral radius computation 435 

Originally, a spectral radius loss term was introduced to ensure convergence to a steady state. 436 

For this, sparse eigenvalue calculations were performed using the scipy library, which is based 437 

on the ARnoldi PACKage (ARPACK) implementation (4 6). This method was not available on the 438 

GPU, and we replaced it with a power iteration method to approximate the dominant eigenvalue 439 

directly on the GPU. Instead of checking for convergence at each step of the algorithm, a fixed 440 

number of iterations (5) was used, as this reduces the computational overhead. 441 

## Weight norm regularization and gradient noise 442 

In this implementation, we penalize the model weights using standard L2 regularization. The 443 

original implementation included an additional term that also penalized weights near zero to 444 

prevent neuron death. In this implementation, neuron death is instead addressed by injection of 445 

gradient noise. We set the default regularization strength of this term to be the same as in the 446 

original CPU implementation (λ= 1e -6). To mitigate neuron death during training and potentially 447 

improve generalization, we applied gradient noise injection to parameters during training (47). The 448 

noise was drawn from a standard normal distribution scaled by a tunable hyperparameter. 449 

Formally, if g is the original gradient and ϵ∼N(0,I), the updated gradient becomes g′=g+ 𝛾 ϵ where 450 

𝛾 is the scaling hyperparameter. We set 𝛾  = 1e -9 for all analyses, except for cross validation on 451 

the hyperparameter optimized high -coverage ligand dataset, in which a varying noise scheme 452 

was used. Specifically, the noise was scaled according to the current learning rate: g′=g+ α𝛾 ϵ453 

where α is the learning rate for this setup and we set 𝛾  = 1e -6. To measure the similarity of effect 454 

of the new regularization implementation on the steady state, a correlation was calculated on the 455 

gradient between the two implementations periodically (every 5 epochs) throughout training. 456 

## State regularization 457 

LEMBAS regularizes the predicted output to penalize deviation from a uniform distribution. This 458 

state regularization serves as a bayesian prior of diversity between samples. The original 459 

implementation penalized the mean and variance of a uniform distribution. The new 460 

implementation uses evenly spaced values (via linspace) to approximate a uniform distribution 461 

prior more directly. That is, we approximate the result of uniform distribution and penalize the 462 

deviation of the output from this distribution using MSE. We also penalize output values that fall 463 

outside the unit interval in alignment with the original implementation. A regularization strength of 464 

λ= 1e -6 was used for all analyses. To evaluate the similarity in the state regularization the 465 

gradients were stored and a final correlation was calculated at the end of the training. The 466 

methods was originally used in the CPU version of LEMBAS to enable faster training as without 467 

it the model had a hard time imposing significant variance in internal states however testing shows 468 

that this is no longer the case in the GPU version. The reason for this remains unclear but is likely 469 

due to the new gradient calculations. 470

model output, bypassing the need to apply additional transformation on inferred or measured TF 474 

activities, which may include negative values. As such, the predicted TF activity can be matched 475

## Other minor modifications 478 

The gradient clipping term was changed to a norm gradient as it is a less biased scaling step. The 479 

frequency of convergence checks within the signaling network's RNN propagation was reduced 480 

from being performed every iteration to every 10 iterations. This is due to the check requiring an 481 

expensive synchronization operation in the GPU setting. 482 

## Performance Evaluation and Comparisons 483 

We employed Cross -Validation (CV) to evaluate the effect of each algorithmic modification, 484 

independently, and together. Performance was assessed using Pearson correlation between 485 

predicted values and data. Execution time was assessed by the wall time for each fold in the CV. 486 

For the low -coverage dataset, the original hyperparameters were used to isolate for effects 487 

caused by the GPU implementation directly. For the high -coverage dataset, hyperparameter 488 

optimization was manually performed to decrease computational time while retaining predictive 489 

performance. This focused mainly on gradient noise schedules and batch size as these were 490 

found to drive most of the variance in performance. For the high -coverage dataset the CPU 491 

version of LEMBAS was run 3 times and used for the time comparison, for CV the reported 492 

correlations from the original publication were used. 493 

## GAMM 494 

All GAMMs were fitted using the R package mgcv (v1.9 -4) with the number of basis functions for 495 

smooth terms set to k = 5. Partial effects and F-test p-values were obtained from models fit using 496 

restricted maximum likelihood (REML), while likelihood ratio tests were performed using 497 

maximum likelihood (ML) to ensure valid comparison of nested models differing in smooth terms. 498 

To model prediction sensitivity (AAD), we fit a GAMM with AAD as the response, including smooth 499 

terms for learned edge weight (log -transformed mean absolute weight), edge connectivity, and 500 

the number of edges removed, fixed effects for dataset, regularization strength, and edge type, 501 

and a random intercept for model identity. To assess whether the relationship between edge 502 

weight and AAD differed by edge type, we fit a second GAMM allowing the smooth effect of 503 

learned edge weight to vary by edge type, while retaining the same covariates and random -effects 504 

structure as the main model. 43 505 

## Self -pruning 506 

To assess the sensitivity of the signaling module to weight pruning. We investigate how big of an 507 

effect removing all weights lower than a specific cutoff has on the final output. 508 

We trained an ensemble of models for each dataset and level of L2 regularization, adding 100 509 

stochastic edges to each PKN , in a conditioned way to preserve the connectivity distribution 510 

(Supplementary Algorithm A1 ). The model weights were saved at checkpoints throughout 511 

training. The CDFs of edge weights were retrieved at the middle checkpoint. The 512 

hyperparameters for the model were the same as for the cross validation study. For the synthetic 513 

data set we reused the low -coverage models pick of hyperparameters, with state regularization 514 

removed. 515
