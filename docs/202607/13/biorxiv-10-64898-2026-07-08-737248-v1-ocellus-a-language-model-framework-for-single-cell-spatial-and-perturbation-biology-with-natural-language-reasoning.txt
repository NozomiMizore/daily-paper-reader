The first limitation is modality fragmentation. Single -cell foundation models such as scGPT, 1

Geneformer , and scFoundation process dissociated RNA -seq profiles and produce strong cell -2

state representations but cannot directly incorporate spatial context. Spatial foundation models 3

such as SToFM  and HEIST extend representation learning to tissue context , yet they  require 4

dedicated spatial -aware architectures —hierarchical graph transformers in HEIST and SE(2) -5

equivariant networks in SToFM — that cannot share parameters with single -cell counterparts. 6

Consequently,  a model trained on millions of dissociated cells  cannot directly inform spatial 7

tissue analysis, and spatial models lack the broad cell -type coverage of single -cell atlases. 8

Nicheformer partially bridges this gap by jointly training spatial and dissociated data within a 9

unified transformer . As a result,  currently ther e is  no existing foundation model  that 10 

simultaneously unifies dissociated single -cell, spatial transcriptomics, and perturbation 11 

prediction within a single, unmodified backbone. 12 

The second limitation is curated -knowledge dependence in perturbation generalization. The 13 

standard Replogle 2022 benchmark (Replogle et al., 2022) requires predicting transcriptional 14 

responses for 457 knockout genes that share zero overlap with the 1,830 genes observed during 15 

training. Under these conditions, even GEARS achieves direction accuracy of only 54.6 16 

percent —barely exceeding the 50 percent random baseline —because Gene Ontology annotations 17 

are incomplete for many genes and do not capture the full complexity of regulatory relationships. 18 

The fundamental problem is that models must generalize perturbation effects to genes whose 19 

functional profiles are only partially characterized. 20 

The third limitation is opacity. All current virtual -cell models operate as black -box predictors: 21 

they output numerical predictions without biological reasoning. A researcher using GEARS 22

# Results 3

The OCellus framework 4

OCellus  is organized around three components that share a single Qwen3.5 -9B backbone: a 5

multi -task language model that serves simultaneously as encoder, predictor, and explainer; a 6

plug -in graph neural network that handles continuous perturbation prediction; and an agent layer 7

that exposes the full capability suite through natural language (Figure 1). The same nine -billion -8

parameter model thus produces cell -type embeddings ranked first among fourteen foundation 9

models, executes twenty -two biological tasks at expert -level accuracy, predicts transcriptional 10 

responses to unseen genetic knockouts with state -of -the -art fidelity, and generates natural -11 

language explanations of its own outputs. The three roles —encoder, predictor, explainer —are 12 

co -located on a shared backbone via dynamic adapter swapping. This arrangement distinguishes 13 

OCellus from prior foundation models that populate at most one or two of these roles with 14 

separate models; the roles share the same backbone weights but are activated by separate LoRA 15 

adapters trained on different task mixes. 16

The agent layer addresses a complementary limitation: even a powerful model is accessible only 8

by writing custom code per task (loading h5ad, applying LoRA, decoding predictions, plotting 9

results). OCellus -Agent organizes the full capability suite as a Coordinator –Expert –Verifier 10 

system over the same Qwen3.5 -9B backbone. The Coordinator contains a trained Planner LoRA 11 

that decomposes natural -language queries into executable directed acyclic graphs of typed tool 12 

calls, a rule -based Router that dispatches each step to the appropriate expert role, and a three -13 

layer Verifier that provides biological plausibility checks. Five expert roles —Data Loader, 14 

Annotator, Perturbation, Spatial, and Explainer —are implemented as dynamic LoRA swaps on 15 

the shared backbone, and a Tool Registry of fifteen registered tools is exposed via a Gradio  web 16 

interface. Six interchangeable runtime configurations are loaded on demand through dynamic 17 

adapter swapping, so the entire agent occupies approximately twenty -two gigabytes of GPU 18 

memory on a single GPU rather than five separate model instances. For spatial transcriptomics, 19 

EvenClock  discretizes two -dimensional coordinates into eighteen clockface sectors of text, 20 

allowing the same backbone to consume spatial context with no architectural modification; the 21 

spatial -encoding scheme is detailed in Results § 2.4. 22

EvenClock: text -only spatial reasoning 3

Spatial transcriptomics in the language -model paradigm requires representing two -dimensional 4

coordinates in text, and existing spatial foundation models achieve this by extending the 5

architecture with graph neural network layers over spatial neighborhoods or by injecting 6

coordinate embeddings as auxiliary features. We asked whether a vanilla language model could 7

instead consume spatial context directly through a text encoding. EvenClock  discretizes the 8

continuous two -dimensional plane into a clockface of six angular directions —clock positions 9

twelve, two, four, six, eight, and ten —crossed with three concentric distance rings labeled sec, 10 

min, and hr, yielding eighteen sectors whose neighboring -cell gene lists are concatenated into a 11 

text prefix. The full encoding specification, including ring -radius normalization against the 12 

dataset median nearest -neighbor distance, appears in Methods. 13 

The EvenClock  formulation was designed to fit our model’s training -time context window 14 

economically. The eighteen -sector encoding preserves both directional (clock -position) and 15 

radial -distance information while remaining compact enough that a center cell’s full ranked -gene 16 

sentence and its complete neighborhood context fit within the model’s effective input length 17 

together, allowing the model to attend to both at every training step. The formulation is also 18 

invariant to coordinate scale because ring radii are normalized to the median nearest -neighbor 19 

distance, allowing pixel -scale and micrometer -scale datasets to mix, and empty sectors are 20 

silently omitted. When the  EvenClock prefix is omitted, the input reduces to a ranked -gene cell 21 

sentence and the model performs single -cell -style annotation; when it is included, the model 22

# Discussion 1

The plug -in graph neural network’s success validates a broader design strategy for virtual -cell 2

modeling: a frozen language model serves as a universal representation backbone, and 3

lightweight, domain -specific components inject structured biological knowledge without 4

modifying backbone weights. Each component in our framework requires fewer than one million 5

parameters —three orders of magnitude smaller than the nine -billion -parameter backbone —and 6

trains in hours rather than days, which makes the strategy practically extensible. Drug -response 7

prediction over drug -target interaction graphs, regulatory -network inference over transcription -8

factor networks, and multi -modal integration over protein -structure embeddings are natural next 9

targets; the key requirement is that the domain -specific structure can be formulated as a graph 10 

amenable to neural message passing, with the language model providing initial node 11 

representations. Our ablation results indicate that this combination of learned representations 12 

plus domain -specific graph structure is more effective than either component alone, but the 13 

precise decomposition of the gain —how much comes from graph topology versus node features 14 

versus the language model’s functional knowledge —remains an open question that the present 15 

ablation only partially resolves. 16 

The perturbation results have implications beyond our specific architecture. GEARS achieves 17 

competitive perturbation prediction by relying on manually curated Gene Ontology annotations 18 

to establish functional similarity between genes, enabling transfer from observed to unobserved 19 

perturbations. Our findings indicate that, on the Replogle 2022 perturbation -generalization task, 20 

the functional knowledge encoded by a language model trained on diverse biological tasks is 21 

more effective than the Gene Ontology annotations GEARS relies on. Whether this advantage 22

EvenClock spatial encoding 10 

For each spatial dataset we compute the median nearest -neighbor distance over all cell pairs, 11 

build a cKDTree  on the cell coordinates, and for each center cell query all neighbors within a 12 

radius of twelve times the median nearest -neighbor distance. Each neighbor is assigned to one of 13 

eighteen sectors obtained by crossing six angular directions —clock positions 12, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 14 

10 o’clock —with three concentric distance rings labeled sec (zero to three times the median 15 

nearest -neighbor distance), min (three to six times), and hr  (six to twelve times). The angular 16 

boundaries are ± 30°around each clock direction, with ties broken by absolute angle difference. 17 

Within each sector we sort neighboring cells by descending expression of their top -ranked genes 18 

and concatenate the top five marker genes per neighbor into a comma -separated string. Empty 19 

sectors are silently omitted to keep prompt length bounded. This encoding is appended to the 20 

center cell’s own ranked -gene sentence for spatial tasks and omitted for non -spatial tasks, 21 

allowing the same language model to handle both modes without architectural change. 22
