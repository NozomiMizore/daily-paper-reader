# Abstract 15 

Large language models (LLMs) can process diverse forms of information once they are 16 represented as tokens in a shared sequence space. However, single-cell transcriptomes remain 17 a foreign modality to LLMs because they are continuous, high-dimensional molecular profiles 18 rather than discrete linguistic units. Here, we propose CellTok, a tokenized single-cell 19 language modeling approach that converts transcriptomic profiles into compact cellular token 20 sequences and incorporates them into the vocabulary of a pretrained LLM. By representing 21 cells as native tokens, CellTok enables cellular measurements, textual instructions, biological 22 context, and multi-cell populations to be jointly processed within the same autoregressive 23 modeling framework. Across diverse tasks, CellTok enable LLMs to recognize individual 24 cells, interpret homogeneous and heterogeneous cell populations, infer disease-associated 25 cellular states, predict cell–cell communication, model developmental trajectories, and 26 generate cellular states. Moreover, prompt-based experiments show that providing appropriate 27 biological context improves performance, indicating that CellTok can leverage LLM 28 knowledge and contextual reasoning to support cellular data interpretation. These results 29 demonstrate that single-cell transcriptomes can be transformed from a foreign molecular 30 modality into a native language for LLMs, establishing a unified interface for modeling cells, 31 populations, and biological knowledge in a shared token space. 32

# Introduction 34 

Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable capacity to process, compose, and 35 reason over information expressed as tokens 1,2 . Their success across natural language, code, 36 structured records, and multimodal inputs suggests that token-based sequence modeling can 37 provide a general interface for representing diverse forms of knowledge 3–5 . However, this 38 interface has not yet extended to single-cell biology. Single-cell transcriptomes are 39 continuous, high-dimensional molecular measurements rather than discrete linguistic units. To 40 an LLM, a cell is therefore still largely a foreign language: it can be described by text, 41 summarized by a label, or projected into an embedding space, but its molecular state cannot 42 be directly read, composed, predicted, or generated as part of the model’s native token 43 language. 44 Recent large cellular models (LCMs) have substantially advanced the representation of 45 cellular states by learning from large-scale transcriptomic data 6–13 . Many of these models are 46 inspired by language modeling, but they are not native languages for general-purpose LLMs. 47 As a result, cellular states remain external to the token space of LLMs and cannot be 48 composed with text or conditioned on by prompts. Recent efforts have therefore explored 49 more direct uses of LLMs in single-cell analysis 14–19 , for example by serializing 50 transcriptomic profiles as gene-level textual sequences 14 , or aligning continuous cellular 51 embeddings with textual representations through cross-modal learning 15 . These studies 52 suggest that LLM knowledge can help interpret cellular data, but the connection between cells 53 and LLMs remains indirect. In these formulations, cellular information is first converted into 54 textual summaries, projected features, or task-specific representations, rather than being 55 provided to the LLM as native cellular token sequences. 56 This indirect interface becomes limiting when cellular analysis requires contextual reasoning 57 over cells. Many central problems in single-cell biology do not concern isolated 58 transcriptomes alone, but how cells are grouped into populations, how cells communicate 59 with one another, and how cellular states are ordered along developmental or disease 60 processes 20–22 . These problems require cells to be represented as compositional units that can 61 be arranged with other cells, interpreted under task-specific instructions, and connected to 62 biological knowledge. This is precisely the type of contextual reasoning for which LLMs are 63 well suited, but it requires cells to be part of the model’s token space rather than external 64 vectors or textual summaries. We therefore reasoned that cellular tokenization could provide a 65 native interface between single-cell transcriptomes and LLMs. If transcriptomic profiles can 66 be converted into compact discrete cellular tokens and incorporated into the vocabulary of a 67

These cellular tokens are then 74 added to the vocabulary of a pretrained LLM and modeled together with text tokens under an 75 autoregressive objective. This design allows individual cells, cell populations, task 76 instructions, and biological context to be represented within a shared token space. 77 We evaluate CellTok across tasks that test whether LLMs can read and reason over cellular 78 data. CellTok enables LLMs to recognize cellular identities, interpret homogeneous and 79 heterogeneous cell populations, infer disease-associated cellular states, predict cell–cell 80 communication, model developmental trajectories, and generate cellular states under specified 81 contexts. We further show that biologically informative prompts improve performance, 82 suggesting that CellTok can use LLM knowledge and contextual reasoning to support cellular 83 interpretation. Together, these results demonstrate that single-cell transcriptomes can be 84 transformed from a foreign molecular modality into a native language for LLMs, establishing 85 a route toward cellular analysis in which cells, populations, task instructions, and biological 86 knowledge are modeled in a shared sequence space. 87 88

We developed CellTok, an approach that transforms single-cell transcriptomes into discrete 92 token sequences and enables their native modeling within large language models (LLMs) 93 through an early-fusion design ( Fig. 1A ). At the core of CellTok is a learned tokenization 94 mechanism that represents each cell’s gene expression profile as a compact sequence of 95 discrete tokens. Inspired by vector-quantized variational autoencoders (VQ-VAE) 23 , we 96 employed an encoder–quantizer–decoder architecture to compress high-dimensional 97 transcriptomic profiles into a short sequence of discrete codes designed to preserve 98 biologically relevant expression patterns. These learned codebook vectors are incorporated 99 directly into the vocabulary of a pretrained LLM (Qwen 2.5 7B 24 ) as cell tokens, where they 100 participate in autoregressive modeling in the same manner as conventional text tokens. CellTok enables LLMs to interpret cell populations through multi-cell contexts 162 

and task semantics 163 

Many downstream analyses in single-cell studies are inherently defined at the level of cell 164 populations, such as cluster annotation, comparative analysis across conditions, and 165 identification of disease-associated states. However, many existing approaches such as LCMs 166 remain fundamentally cell-centric: cells are modeled independently, and population-level 167 conclusions are derived through post hoc aggregation of individual predictions. This paradigm 168 limits the ability to incorporate contextual information arising from population composition, 169 co-occurring transcriptional patterns, and shared biological states across cells. 170 CellTok addresses this limitation by representing each cell as a compact token sequence and 171 composing multiple tokenized cells within a shared autoregressive context. In this 172 formulation, predictions can be made directly from a multi-cell input, without first reducing 173 the population to independently generated cell-wise outputs. We therefore asked whether 174 CellTok could use both multi-cell transcriptomic context and the semantic information 175 provided by task descriptions to support population-level inference across different settings. 176 We first evaluated CellTok in a setting that mimics a standard single-cell annotation 177 workflow, in which cells are first grouped into clusters and then assigned labels ( Fig. 2A ). To 178 simulate this process, we constructed homogeneous cell populations from a held-out lung 179 dataset 34 , with each population containing cells from a single cell type and population sizes 180 ranging from 10 to 100 cells ( Methods ). Using donor ctr53 for training and ctr55 for testing, 181 we fine-tuned CellTok-base to assign a shared label to each population, resulting in CellTok-182 cluster ( Methods ). Because existing methods are not designed to directly annotate cell 183

Beyond assigning identities or states to cells, a central goal of biological analysis is to 269 understand how cells influence each other through intercellular interactions. In single-cell 270 transcriptomics, such interactions are typically manifested as cell–cell communication (CCC), 271 mediated by coordinated ligand–receptor signaling pathways 21 . Unlike cell identity or state, 272 communication is not an intrinsic property of an individual cell, but a relational property 273 conditioned on both interacting cellular contexts. CCC therefore provides a natural setting for 274 testing whether CellTok can predict relational outputs directly from paired cellular inputs, and 275 whether task semantics can help the model map cellular contexts to biologically meaningful 276 communication programs. 277 We investigated this capability in two complementary settings. First, in a non-spatial setting, 278 we formulated CCC inference as predicting ligand–receptor signaling pathways between a 279 source cell population and a target cell population from their transcriptomic profiles. Using 280 the same held-out lung dataset 34 , we derived putative signaling pathway annotations across 281 healthy and COVID-19 donors with CellChat 36 , and fine-tuned the CellTok-base model, 282 resulting in CellTok-CCC ( Fig. 3A, Methods ). 283 CellTok-CCC achieved strong performance with and without providing cell-type labels ( Fig. 284 

3B ), indicating that CCC signaling pathway inference does not depend on predefined cell 285 identities. Furthermore, pathways predicted by CellTok-CCC were most 289 similar to ground truth from the corresponding test donor rather than to training donors ( Fig. 290 

3C–D, Methods ). This result argues against simple reproduction of training-donor pathway 291 profiles and indicates that the model uses donor-specific transcriptomic information from the 292 paired populations. 293 We further asked whether the semantic content of pathway names contributes to CCC 294 prediction. CellTok enables LLMs to resolve developmental ordering from cellular tokens 370 

A central property of cellular systems is their continuous evolution over time. We thus asked 371 whether CellTok can recover temporal structure directly from transcriptomic profiles by 372 inferring the developmental ordering of cellular states. We evaluated this capability using a 373 scRNA-seq dataset of brain organoids derived from three induced pluripotent stem cells 374 (iPSCs) and one embryonic stem cell (ESC) 38 , spanning a developmental trajectory from day 375 4 to 2 months and covering multiple stages of differentiation ( Fig. 4A ). 376 To examine temporal ordering at different resolutions, we designed two settings ( Fig. 4B ): a 377 large-span setting with widely separated time points (days 4, 12, 21, 31, and 61), and a small-378 span setting with more densely sampled and transcriptionally similar stages (days 4, 7, 11, 12, 379 and 16). In both settings, CellTok was trained with sampled developmental days as 380 supervision and asked to infer the relative temporal positions of held-out cells from their 381 transcriptomic profiles. Performance was evaluated using accuracy, Kendall rank correlation 382 coefficient, and Spearman correlation coefficient ( Fig. 4C, Methods ). 383 Across both settings, CellTok achieved high temporal ordering performance ( Fig. 4D ). As 384 expected, performance was stronger in the large-span setting, where developmental stages are 385 more transcriptionally separated. Importantly, CellTok also maintained reliable performance 386 in the small-span setting, indicating that it could resolve relatively fine-grained temporal 387 differences among nearby developmental stages rather than only separating distant cell states. 388 To assess whether CellTok learned temporal relationships that generalized beyond the exact 389 time points used during training, we performed an out-of-distribution (OOD) local temporal 390 generalization experiment in the large-span setting. We replaced increasing numbers of 391 training-observed days with nearby but unseen days while preserving the overall ordering task 392 (Fig. 4B ). Performance gradually decreased as more time points were substituted, but 393 remained robust even when four of the five stages were replaced ( Fig. 4D ). # Discussion 459 

In this work, we propose CellTok, a tokenized single-cell language modeling approach that 460 enables LLMs to read transcriptomic profiles as discrete cellular tokens. By converting 461 continuous expression profiles into compact token sequences, CellTok provides a language-462 compatible cellular representation and an LLM-native modeling interface through which 463 individual cells can be arranged, composed, predicted, and generated within structured 464 cellular contexts. This formulation moves single-cell modeling beyond treating cells primarily 465 as endpoints of molecular representation, and instead makes cellular states native, composable 466 elements in the token language of LLMs for modeling populations, intercellular relationships, 467 and developmental processes. 468 A key implication of CellTok is that diverse population-level analyses can be reformulated as 469 sequence modeling problems within LLMs. Instead of relying exclusively on downstream 470 aggregation of cell embeddings or separately designed task-specific architectures, CellTok 471 allows multiple cells, biological descriptions, and relational outputs to be represented within a 472 shared token space and processed through the same autoregressive interface. Population 473 annotation, disease-state inference, cell–cell communication prediction, trajectory reasoning, 474 and stage-conditioned generation can therefore be expressed as different conditional 475 prediction tasks over cellular and textual tokens. 476 The integration of cellular and textual tokens also creates a direct interface between molecular 477 measurements and natural language in scientific research. In CellTok, cellular tokens can 478 condition textual descriptions, while textual descriptions can condition the generation and 479 interpretation of cellular states. This bidirectional grounding makes cellular tokens usable not 480 only as internal representations, but also as input and output elements of language-model-481 based analysis. Importantly, our experiments suggest that CellTok can benefit from task-482 relevant biological information expressed in natural language. These results provide initial 483

evidence that cellular tokenization may allow LLMs to use biological task semantics and prior 484 knowledge when analyzing cellular contexts. 485 This capability is central to the motivation of CellTok. Many biological questions require not 486 only recognizing molecular patterns, but also relating them to phenotypes, pathways, 487 conditions, and mechanistic hypotheses. Conventional LCMs provide powerful 488 representations of cellular states, but these representations often remain external to the 489 linguistic space in which biological knowledge and scientific questions are formulated. By 490 making cellular states available as tokens within the same modeling space as biological 491 language, CellTok provides a route for connecting molecular measurements with knowledge-492 guided inference. The current study demonstrates this possibility at an early stage, and 493 systematic evaluation of knowledge usage and reasoning behavior will be important for future 494 work. 495 CellTok should therefore be viewed as a proof of concept for cellular tokenization as a shared 496 representation and task interface, rather than as a task-specific model optimized for a single 497 benchmark or as a single parameter set that solves all single-cell problems. Most analyses in 498 this study use task-specific fine-tuning, while CellTok-mix provides an initial demonstration 499 that the same cellular vocabulary and autoregressive interface can be reused across several 500 related population-level tasks. Notably, joint training introduced only a modest performance 501 trade-off in most settings and even improved performance in the disease-state identification 502 task, suggesting that some forms of population-level reasoning may benefit from shared 503 training across related cellular contexts. 504 Several limitations and future directions remain. First, although discretization provides 505 compactness and compositionality, it may compress or discard fine-grained quantitative 506 information from continuous gene expression profiles. Future work should characterize which 507 biological signals are preserved or lost during tokenization and develop tokenizers that better 508 balance compression, interpretability, and quantitative fidelity. Second, the semantics and 509 transferability of learned cellular tokens depend on the training data, gene panel, codebook 510 design, and tokenizer architecture. More expressive tokenization schemes, including 511 tokenizers initialized from or built upon pretrained cellular representations from LCMs, may 512 improve token fidelity and transferability while reducing dataset-specific biases. Third, 513 although CellTok supports several tasks through a shared token-based interface and can 514 benefit from task-relevant prompts, its ability to transfer knowledge across tasks and 515 biological contexts remains to be systematically evaluated. Larger multitask corpora, more 516 explicit instruction tuning, and systematic transfer experiments will be needed to determine 517 whether knowledge learned in one cellular context can improve performance in another. 518

Gene expression generation along developmental trajectories 817 

Using the brain organoid dataset 38 , we formulated trajectory-conditioned generation as a 818 temporal imputation problem along developmental trajectories. Given cell code sequences 819 from a set of observed time points (context), the model was trained to generate the gene 820 expression profile at a held-out target day, enabling both interpolation and extrapolation along 821 the timeline. 822 We constructed a mixed suite of 19 generation tasks (total n = 260,000), grouped into four 823 regimes, to train CellTok-trajectory. Each task was defined as predicting a target day from a 824 set of context days: three local interpolation tasks used nearby temporal neighbors as context, 825 where the target lies within the local neighborhood (e.g., context Days 4, 9, and 11 with target 826 Day 7); two dense interpolation tasks incorporated richer multi-point context (e.g., context 827 Days 4, 7, 9, 12, and 16 with target Day 11); five mid-range interpolation tasks placed the 828 target between temporally distant anchors (e.g., context Days 4, 12, and 21 with target Day 829 16); nine extrapolation tasks predicted early or late endpoints from boundary observations 830 (e.g., context Days 4, 7, and 9 with target Day 11). For each task, we performed a stratified 831 split with a 7:2:1 ratio for training, validation, and testing, and then merged splits across tasks, 832 yielding 182,000 training, 52,000 validation, and 26,000 test instances in total. 833 To assess whether CellTok-trajectory can generalize beyond task definitions observed during 834 training, we evaluated the trained model under a target-day out-of-distribution (OOD) setting. 835 In this setting, the conditioning set was kept fixed, while the target day was replaced with an 836 unseen temporal node. For example, using context cells from Days 4, 12, and 21, the model 837 was required to generate cells for Day 15 rather than the training-observed target Day 16. For 838 each OOD task, we randomly sampled 5,000 instances to construct a dedicated test set. The 839 model was evaluated directly on these held-out tasks without further fine-tuning. 840 841
