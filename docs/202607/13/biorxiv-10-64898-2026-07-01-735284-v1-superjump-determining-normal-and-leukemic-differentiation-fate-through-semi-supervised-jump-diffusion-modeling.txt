ABSTRACT 18 

Single cell RNA-seq (scRNA) has provided unprecedented resolution into cellular and clonal 19 

heterogeneity. Computational approaches have enabled recovery of differentiation dynamics, yet 20 

current approaches do not evaluate discontinuous differentiation processes present in malignant 21 

leukemia. To address these gaps, we developed SupeRJump: a jump-drift-diffusion based 22 

supervised cell-fate model (https://github.com/namwob44/SupeRJump/ ). We deploy this approach 23 

in human bone marrow, murine aging hematopoiesis, and lentivirally barcoded mouse models of 24 

acute myeloid leukemia. Our framework introduces a semi-supervised pseudotime strategy to fit a 25 

jump-drift-diffusion model and batch correction for lineage fate predictions from absorbing 26 

Markov chains. We introduce metrics to quantify a cell’s skewness toward particular lineages, 27 

transitions through intermediate progenitor states toward terminally differentiated states, and 28 

discontinuous transition dynamics. We use these metrics to identify cells preferentially biased for 29 

differentiation, their underlying transcriptional networks, and gene programs responsible for 30 

differentiation discontinuity. 31

INTRODUCTION 33 

Hematopoiesis is arranged in a hierarchy of stem and progenitor cells capable of producing distinct 34 

cell fates. Alterations in these differentiation processes is a critical feature underlying 35 

hematopoietic disease including myeloid leukemia. Understanding the cellular and transcriptional 36 

context for differentiation biases may offer insights into maximizing therapies that normalize 37 

hematopoietic function. Single cell RNA-seq (scRNA) has provided a critical function in exploring 38 

insights into these cellular processes. 39 

40 

Computational approaches have made recovering differentiation dynamics tractably possible. 41 

Such tools have been built on diffusion map paradigms introduced by destiny[1], and other 42 

diffusion strategies have adapted stochastic network traversal methods to reconstruct 43 

pseudotime[2] and infer high density transitory states[3]. New strategies have emerged to infer 44 

cellular outcome from earlier progenitor cells based on reformulating Markov chains with 45 

absorbing states[4] , [5] , [6] , [7]. While scRNA does not uniformly sample all transition states in 46 

heterogeneous tissues, recent approaches have suggested highly sparse cellular states may 47 

represent unstable biological transition points[3]. Current approaches do not consider 48 

discontinuous differentiation processes often present in aberrant hematopoiesis such as leukemia. 49 

We aim to explicitly model discontinuous processes within the cell state transitions to account for 50 

traversal of under sampled sparse cell states. 51 

52 

To address these gaps, we developed SupeRJump: a jump-drift-diffusion based supervised cell-53 

fate model. We present a hypothesis generation tool that identifies cell populations poised for 54 

differentiation and transcriptional networks underlying these transitions. We also introduce metrics 55 

to account for traveling to intermediate states on their way to terminal states. To demonstrate the 56 

effectiveness of our strategies, we deploy this approach on multiple datasets including: 1) human 57 

bone marrow[4], 2) normal murine, aged hematopoiesis[8], and 3 ) barcoded murine acute myeloid 58 

leukemia ex vivo cultures. We use these datasets to showcase different aspects of the approach. For 59 

each of these datasets, we identify cells with preferential bias for differentiation lineages, and their 60 

underlying transcription factor activity. 61

Model overview: SupeRJump enables robust lineage predictions 64 

Our workflow is demonstrated in Figure 1a . After standard processing of scRNA we obtain labeled 65 

cell clusters. We provide a semi-supervised pseudotime that represents the expected ordering of 66 

cells. Afterwards, we fit our jump-diffusion model to build a cell-to-cell network. Our approach 67 

then aims to formulate the inference problem into two distinct phases, 1) inferring the 68 

differentiation outcome and 2) identifying transcription factor activities associated with 69 

differentiation. Regarding differentiation inference, we improve upon existing methods of cell-fate 70 

by accounting for discontinuous transitions in our cell-cell network using jump-diffusion, 71 

introducing batch correction to handle imbalances of heterogenous populations across replicates 72 

and conditions, and applying a semi-supervised signature as our pseudotime measure. With these 73 

improvements, we introduce a novel strategy that identifies and characterizes candidate cells that 74 

are preferentially biased toward particular lineages. In addition to calculating differentiation 75 

likelihoods for each cell to each lineage (fate probabilities), we also introduce metrics to account 76 

for intermediate state transitions termed ‘visitation probability’ and ‘weighted destination time’. 77 

To uncover potential mechanisms underlying lineage-specific differentiation, we deploy two 78 

primary strategies 1) analyzing gene ontology terms associated with discontinuous processes, and 79 

2) multivariate linear modeling to evaluate lineage fates as a function of inferred transcription 80 

factor activity. Collectively we use these approaches as a hypothesis generation tool to nominate 81 

mechanistic drivers of a lineage bias and output. 82

Jump incorporation accurately reflects pseudotime ordering 84 

Current diffusion policies implement k-nearest neighbors which limits the number of neighbors 85 

accessible to any given cell[7]. Our strategy expands the allowable neighbors through a 86 

probabilistic approach via jump-diffusion model fitting. Expanding the neighbors for each cell 87 

broadens the range of possible transitions, allowing us to capture rare events. Consequently, this 88 

expansion allows for faster traversal of the cell-cell network. To directly compare transition 89 

probability matrices (TPMs) with and without the jump model, we analyzed a previously described 90 

human bone marrow dataset[4]. Both TPMs utilize a Palantir pseudotime[4] ( Figure 1b ) and the 91 

same absorbing cells from CellRank2[7] for fair comparison. These absorbing states allow us to 92 

calculate both steps to absorption, and fate probability. We compare performance by correlating 93

compared to the jump TPM, the diffusion TPM had heavier tailed distributions which can be a 99

increase in steps to absorption for cells more disconnected from highly connected transition states. 103 

Jump diffusion models allow for a dynamic increase in neighbors for each cell, mitigating the over-104 

reliance on rare, high transitory cells. Our approach correctly orders the erythroid and myeloid 105 

lineages simultaneously, rather than sequentially, with monocytes, megakaryocytes and early 106 

erythroid cells being absorbed at a similar number of steps. Finally, we demonstrate the two 107 

strategies are strongly correlated (r=0.906), which suggests both models are primarily following 108 

similar dynamics. 109

In most approaches, TPMs are constructed by pooling all replicates and conditions into a single 128 

network, ultimately limiting the statistical power of unique biological replicates and failing to 129 

capture the heterogeneity of intermediate cell states across conditions. To account for cell 130 

composition heterogeneity, we employ a batch correction scheme inspired by a bulk-RNA seq 131 

strategy, ComBAT[9, 10]. This allows for sample specific fate probability determination, where 132 

we additionally restrict each biological condition to absorbing states represented in their own 133 

network, as opposed to across all samples. We returned to the data from Setty et al[4] to determine 134 

how batch corrected fate probabilities correlated with well-defined transcriptional programs. We 135 

evaluated lineage specific transcription factor expression by pseudotime for individual cell 136 

populations and samples ( Figure 2b ). Expectedly, we observed increased RNA expression levels 137 

of IRF8 and GATA1 for dendritic and erythroid lineages respectively. We next correlated 138 

expression levels with fate probabilities across all cell types and observed. IRF8 increases when 139 

probability for dendritic lineage increases, likewise the same trend occurs with GATA1 along the 140 

erythroid lineage. In this setting, replicates displayed consistent behavior in fate probabilities for 141 

both the sample-specific and global model. We posit that this previously inaccessible comparison 142 

between replicates will be critical for comparison of fate probabilities in experimental settings with 143 

severely imbalanced cell populations or in less well-defined cellular systems such as leukemia, 144 

both of which are explored in Figure 5 .145

Gene expression programs identify biological processes linked with jumps and differentiation fate 147

transcriptional programs associated with model fits that are best represented by discontinuous 149 

processes. Our strategy, which we call JumpROPE (Jump Relevant Ontology Pathway Extraction), 150 

enables hypothesis generation for discontinuous processes associated with transitions toward 151 

specific cell types. We fit a jump-model for each principle component-cell type pair, with a mean 152 

reversion to abstracted ‘eigen-cells’ (see Methods). The magnitude of the jumps corresponds to 153 

how large a deviation is needed within the principle component space to reach that cell type 154 

specific eigen cell (see Methods). To explore this approach, we fit the jump model on a scRNA 155

Uncovering preferential lineage bias in aging normal hematopoiesis 191 

Cell cluster identification in scRNA data can often result in an oversimplification of cell type 192 

identity where broad classifications can encompass several distinct cell-types possessing unique 193 

differentiation capacities. This is particularly problematic in leukemia where mature cell type-194 

specific gene expression programs are often blended with self-renewal programs present in earlier 195 

progenitors. Reference mapping to cellular atlases has offered increased resolution into these cell 196 

type assignments, but remain dependent upon expert curation from bulk gene expression data. We 197 

sought to complement these approaches by devising a fate probability-based approach for 198 

identifying biased cells within a broad cell population. We focused on GMPs, a heterogenous 199 

progenitor population capable of producing several differentiated cells including monocytes and 200 

neutrophils. 201 

For each state-fate cell pair, our bias detection relies on aggregating weighted negative log-202 

probabilities from: 1) a specific fate lineage (neutrophils), 2) membership of the lineage specific 203 

cell type (neutrophils), and 3) membership of the self-identified cell state (GMPs). We use this 204 

aggregated outlier score to account for skewness of a given GMP cell towards a given lineage. We 205 

then apply a secondary robust quantile regression filter (bias score) to identify additional biased 206 

cells that would have been masked by extreme skewing. Applying the cutoff on the outlier score 207 

directly yields n=134 cells, while applying the cutoff on the bias score yields n=1031 cells ( Figure 208 

4a ). We next applied this bias metric to the neutrophil and monocyte lineages separately allowing 209 

us to identify whether GMP cells were unbiased, biased for one lineage, or bipotent ( Figure 4b ). 210 

Differential expression analysis identified Irf8, a critical driver of monocyte and dendritic cell 211 

development, as one of the most enriched genes in monocyte-biased GMPs (n=515 cells) 212 

compared to neutrophil-biased GMPs (n=588 cells) (Figure 4cd ). We additionally identified 213 

upregulation of Ccr2, Ly86, and Ly6e in monocytic-biased GMPs, confirming an enrichment of a 214 

monocytic-gene expression program in these cells. Conversely, neutrophil-biased GMPs possessed 215 

upregulation of expected genes including Cebpe, Cd63, Elane, S100a9, and Gfi1 (Figure 4d, 216 

Supplemental Table 3 ). These findings were supported by geneset enrichment analysis of 217

Lentiviral barcoding supports intraclonal fate transitions 232 

We next sought to experimentally validate fate probability predictions using lentiviral barcoding. 233 

We have previously developed a murine leukemia model driven by co-mutations in Npm1 and 234 

Flt3. Critically, this penetrant model of leukemia retains differentiation potential to a range of 235 

mature myeloid fates including monocytes and dendritic cells[18]. To assess clone-fate 236 

relationships, we infected whole bone marrow cells from leukemic mice with a CROP-seq 237 

barcoding lentivirus encoding GFP ( Figure 5a )[19] , [20]. After 48 hours, infected cells were sorted 238 

on GFP+ expression levels. Sorted cells were expanded for 9 days in liquid culture with pro-239 

differentiation cytokine conditions (SCF+IL3) and then processed for single cell RNA sequencing 240 

(n=7348 cells). We identified 75 clones with >5 cells/clone (dashed line), with the top 10 clones 241 

representing 39.2% of cells in the dataset ( Figure 5b ). We recovered a range of cell types including 242 

primitive HSCs, MultiLin progenitors, classical dendritic cells (cDCs), baso-mast cell progenitors 243 

(BMCPs), and mature basophils, the latter two of which are common cell culture consequences of 244 

prolonged SCF+IL3 exposure ( Figure 5c; Supplemental Table 6 ). We once again assessed 245 

visitation probability between clusters ( Figure 2a ), but this time evaluating a fine to broad cell 246 

type naming resolution to resolve intermediate states ( Figure 5d ). Despite the BMCP abundance, 247 

this sample and metric demonstrated canonical differentiation transitions with the MPP1/2 248

We next sought to determine whether the rate differed between inter- and intra-clonal transitions. 290 

We returned to visitation probability (VP) ( Figure 2a ) and an additional measure, conditional 291 

mean first passage time (CMFPT), to quantify the probability and rate of cells transitioning toward 292 

a single transient state. We identified the MultiLin progenitor with the highest fate to cDC, which 293 

belonged to sgRNA-5, and calculated the VP and CMFPT for every HSPC to this cell ( Figure 5g ). 294 

Cells harboring the same guide (sgRNA-5) had higher probability toward this state, with sgRNA-295 

10 showing a similar distribution. Meanwhile the sgRNA-4 and sgRNA-9 possessed marginally 296 

lower probability. Meanwhile, HSPC populations have relatively quicker transitions to MultiLin 297 

but with a lower visitation probability. A MANOVA was conducted on the guides for both cell 298 

types and statistical significance was found (Pillai value 0.55, p-value =2.2x10 -16 ; Supplemental 299 

Table 8 ). We found the MultiLin sgRNA-5 cells were significantly different from all groups 300 

(p<0.001). Finally, we sought to determine if clones had preferential visitation to specific cell 301 

population, and devised a metric termed weighted destination time (WDT). We ranked WDT for 302 

the erythroid and lymphoid lineages, and identified a diametric opposition between the lineages 303 

across the top 10 clones ( " = − 0.745, p-value = 0.018) ( Figure 5h ). Collectively, these results 304 

suggests that cell-cell transitions are more likely to occur within a clonal lineage at both the 305 

terminal differentiation state, and transitory states in between. We anticipate that these 306

representing stem cell biology and properties of self-renewal. 309

DISCUSSION: 311 

Applying jump diffusion on the cell-cell network allows for an expanded number of neighbors of 312 

for each node over diffusion methods. This is due to the adaptive probabilistic neighbor’s search 313 

introduced. Allowing more neighbors increases the odds of capturing discontinuous processes. 314 

Conceptually, the closest related work to introducing jumps within a network is through a strategy 315 

called “lazy-teleporting”[28]. Instead of finding new edges within the network to act as jumps, we 316 

use jumps to tune a transition probability matrix within PC space. The jumps allow us to identify 317 

discontinuous processes in PC space for given cellular states. These processes can determine 318 

relevant gene programs. We demonstrate identifying these programs by splitting the positive and 319 

negative genes along a jump identified PC and perform gene Ontology. Future work will be aimed 320 

at more robust identification of gene signatures for specific processes, and tying explicit jumps 321 

with transcription factor activity. An example of a method to incorporate in the future is developing 322 

distributions of jump-related genes through gene trajectories to identify new genesets associated 323 

with eigen cells[29]. 324 

325 

Most trajectory analysis approaches aggregate all cells together across genotypes within a unified 326 

representation, permitting transitions into states that are not biologically accessible within a given 327 

system, such as populations that are absent or experimentally ablated. When dealing with 328 

absorbing Markov chains, it is imperative the absorbing states are obtainable for the respective 329 

genotypes. We propose to handle this by decoupling the network to their respective replicates, and 330 

performing batch correction on their fate calculations. Batch correction has been developed for 331 

pseudotime, but not for fates[30-32]. These batch corrections account for different effect size and 332 

cell samplings of replicates. Corrected fates more faithfully represent their biological replicates 333 

while ensuring absorbing states are contained within each replicate. Our modular framework uses 334 

fate calculations to identify preferentially biased cells for particular lineages. This is performed by 335 

using lineages and self-membership to determine an outlier score compared to the pseudobulk for 336 

a particular cell population. This method foregoes the need for arbitrary cutoffs on fates alone as 337 

it provides an adaptive policy. The biased cells allow for novel discovery towards the mechanisms 338 

of a particular fate. 339

Hypothesis generation tools have been designed comparing pseudotime and lineages to differential 341 

gene expression such as Tradeseq[33] and condiments[30]. We focus on generating hypothesis 342 

tools through fates, specifically in the context of preferentially biased cells. We use an empirical 343 

CDF outlier detection to identify biased cells as categorical variables. We demonstrate the use of 344 

bias cell detection through a classical development model of GMP differentiation along monocytic 345 

and neutrophil lineages. Although subclustering methods can identify cell subsets, our outlier 346 

detection approach offers the advantage of semi-supervised labeling guided by lineage fates 347 

selected by the user. A limitation of our approach is it relies on identifying cells that are heavy 348 

tailed compared to the bulk. If the bulk of cells have a high affinity/fate for a particular lineage 349 

only the outliers will be registered as biased. Furthermore, fates are aimed at identifying terminal 350 

states, and largely ignore intermediate ones of interest. We introduce visitation probability and 351 

weighted destination time to handle potentially underrepresented biased cells and evaluate their 352 

progression through intermediate progenitors. These metrics allow us to determine how quickly 353 

and likely a cell is to visit an intermediate cell state before reaching absorbing states. We 354 

demonstrate their application in the context of biased cell calls, where biased cells have a higher 355 

probability and faster travel times than nonbiased cells. 356 

357 

We identify the underlying mechanisms of what makes cells biased. We do this through performing 358 

differential gene expression and transcription factor activity. While we demonstrate the use of bias 359 

cell detection on known myeloid differentiation lineages, we envision the utility as a hypothesis 360 

generation tool. Our outlier detection technique allows for binning cells by nonbiased, uni-biased, 361 

and multi-biased to recover unique characteristics within a given population for lineages. This 362 

allows for understanding the current state through RNA expression, and nominating testable 363 

mechanisms through TF activity. We fit models pairing TF activity to fates which further inform 364 

which TFs are synergistic and antagonistic to particular lineages. This has potential to impact 365 

perturbation studies where nominated TFs can readily be performed on functional assays. A 366 

limitation we face is the reliance of existing gene-TF networks as our approach does not nominate 367 

new candidates for gene TF interactions. This is left to future work along with approaches to 368 

determine the efficacy of the nominated candidates. Overall, we have introduced novel 369 

mechanisms that improve exploratory analysis within scRNA. 370
