Abstract 7

We present FRACTEL, a statistical framework for region-level analysis of CRISPR 8

perturbation screens. FRACTEL aggregates gRNA p-values using a bounded minimum 9

across order statistics, preserving scale and enabling adaptive sensitivity to sparse or 10 

diffuse effects. Region-level null distributions are estimated via simulation, ensuring 11 

precise type I error control. Simulations and real CRISPRi/a datasets demonstrate 12 

improved power and replication rate over gRNA-level analyses. FRACTEL also informs 13 

experimental design, revealing trade-offs between gRNA redundancy and efficacy and 14 

identifying inherent limits in single-cell repression screens of lowly expressed genes. The 15 

method integrates with existing pipelines and supports diverse CRISPR screening 16 

applications. 17

Introduction 24 

High-throughput CRISPR screens have emerged as a scalable experimental approach 25 

for dissecting the effects of genomic perturbations on diverse phenotypes, including gene 26 

expression 1–6 , migration 7, and cell growth 5,8–15 . Initial applications focused on CRISPR-27 

Cas9 knockout screens in coding regions, producing insertions and deletions with 28 

variable efficiency, and disrupting gene function with incomplete penetrance and typically 29 

large effect sizes 10,16–18 . Expansions to CRISPR interference (CRISPRi) and activation 30 

(CRISPRa) screens with diverse epigenetic modifiers have facilitated targeting noncoding 31 

regulatory elements, reading out subsequent effects via cell growth or via gene 32 

expression of reporter constructs 1,19–21 . More recently, CRISPR screens coupled with 33 

single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) have mapped regulatory elements to genes in 34 

particular loci or genome-wide 9,16,22–30 . These Perturb-seq screens have identified regions 35 

crucial for controlling expression of specific genes (often referred as regulatory elements), 36 

elucidated gene networks, and linked genetic elements to phenotypic traits and eQTLs 22– 37 

> 24,28

. While the noncoding genome makes up >98% of human DNA and contains ~90% 38 

of trait-associated genetic variation, the regulatory logic of active elements remains poorly 39 

understood. Efforts like the ENCODE and Roadmap consortiums have mapped the 40 

activity of the noncoding genome and identified putative regulatory elements across cell 41 

types 31,32 ; CRISPRi/a screens have become a standard approach for dissecting the effect 42 

of these putative regulatory elements in their native genomic and cellular contexts 33 .43 

44 

A hallmark of CRISPR perturbation screens is the use of multiple guide RNAs (gRNAs) 45 

to target the same putative regulatory element. This redundancy increases the likelihood 46

Several aggregated region-level analysis methods have been proposed to address these 56 

shortcomings and estimate the effects of entire regulatory elements, rather than individual 57 

gRNAs. MAGeCK’s 34 robust rank aggregation (RRA) method has been widely applied in 58 

bulk CRISPR screens, ranking gRNAs across the experiment to score genes or regions. 59

activity. Alternative strategies, such as union-based pseudobulked aggregation or 62 

Bonferroni correction across gRNAs within a region, have also been implemented in 63 

pipelines like SCEPTRE 38,39 . While valuable, these methods have important limitations: 64 

rank-based approaches discard scale information that is present in the magnitude of the 65 

statistics and lose sensitivity in focused screens; tiling models can be underpowered 66 

when few gRNAs target a region or when gRNA effects are not linearly correlated; naïve 67 

multiple-testing corrections can be overly conservative. 68

To address these challenges, we developed FRACTEL (Framework for Rank Aggregation 85 

of CRISPR Tests within ELements), a region-level statistical framework that aggregates 86 

gRNA-level p-values using a bounded minimum across order statistics. FRACTEL 87 

preserves the scale of p-values, allows bound tuning to adapt to sparse or diffuse signal 88 

architectures, uses pre-computed null simulations, and controls element-level type I error. 89 

This approach integrates seamlessly with existing gRNA-level inference methods, 90 

offering a generalizable and computationally efficient approach to region-level inference. 91

Impact of bound parameter choice 168 

The bound parameter k in FRACTEL determines how many of the smallest ordered 169 

gRNA-level p-values contribute to the bounded-minimum statistic. Smaller values of k170 

favor detection of sparse, strong effects concentrated in a few gRNAs, whereas larger 171 

values allow moderate effects to accumulate across many gRNAs, improving sensitivity 172 

for diffuse signal architectures. 173

Power under varying gRNA number and efficacy 210 

While many compact validated genome-wide promoter-targeting libraries have been 211 

generated 10,14,40–45 , noncoding screens still require 10-20 novel gRNAs per element and 212 

typically have much smaller effect sizes on gene expression 5. Screening with more 213 

gRNAs per regulatory element can either sharpen or blur the signal, depending on how 214 

many of those gRNAs are genuinely functional. To examine this trade-off, we compared 215 

designs with ten and twenty gRNAs per element under identical baseline parameters, 216 

varying the number or proportion of working gRNAs while sweeping effect sizes across a 217 

broad fold-change range (in log scale). For each configuration, we estimated power using 218 

the FRACTEL test and then computed pointwise differences between the n = 10 and 219 

n = 20 curves where positive values indicate higher power for n = 10. 220 

221 

When the number of effective gRNAs was held constant, the designs with a smaller 222 

number of total gRNAs consistently maintained higher power to detect non-null effect 223 

sizes (Fig. 3a). The positive power difference between designs with n = 10 and n = 20 224 

reflects dilution by non-functional gRNAs in the larger library. As the number of working 225 

gRNAs increased, this advantage became progressively narrower—the peaks in the blue 226 

curves contract—because stronger cumulative signal allowed both designs to reach 227 

power saturation (detection probability approaching one) at ever-smaller effect sizes. In 228 

parallel, the n = 10 design becomes functionally saturated once all gRNAs are active, 229 

while n = 20 still contains half inactive gRNAs. Even near power saturation, n = 10 retains 230 

a slight edge due to its cleaner signal composition. The central dip at log 2FC = 0 231

Constraints from basal gene expression in repression screens 261 

Detection of strong repression at very low basal gene expression levels faces two distinct 262 

constraints—one practical, the other theoretical—that together define the lower boundary 263 

of statistical power for CRISPRi analyses based on maximum-likelihood estimation. 264 

265 

The practical limit arises when transcript abundance in the perturbed group is so low that 266 

few or no molecules are detected. In these cases, model fitting becomes unstable or fails 267 

altogether due to complete or near-complete separation of counts between conditions. In 268 

effect, when all measured counts in the perturbed (repressed) group fall to zero, the 269 

experiment ceases to provide the information required for statistical inference, regardless 270 

of the statistical method used. Analysis workflows often exclude such element-gene pairs 271

during quality control or testing 38,47 , which leads to a systematic bias against detecting 272

The theoretical limit persists even when detection is idealized. We conducted multiple 275 

simulations in parallel to assess the effect of baseline expression on power in the ideal 276 

case where test statistics were generated directly, removing measurement errors (Fig. 277 

S2). The simulations directly show clear asymmetry for small values of 𝛽 ଴ (baseline-278

increases. The underlying mechanism is inherent to maximum-likelihood estimation. As 280 

the true fold-change approaches zero, the estimated variance grows much faster than the 281 

change in mean, forcing the expected test statistic toward zero. This divergence is evident 282 

in the simulated curves, where the standard error inflates exponentially (Fig. S3). The 283 

system effectively enters a quasi-Bernoulli regime in which detection depends on whether 284 

any perturbed cell retains measurable transcript counts. 285 

286 

Together, these findings delineate the limits of inference for low-expression targets. The 287 

practical constraint restricts what can be measured, and the theoretical constraint limits 288 

what can be recovered even under perfect measurement. 289

Benchmarking on real datasets 330 

FRACTEL can be applied to aggregate any well-calibrated gRNA-level tests to produce 331 

element-level analyses of CRISPR screens. Many technical factors and model 332 

misspecification can confound CRISPR screen analysis, producing poorly calibrated 333 

analyses with high numbers of false positives across common analysis methods and 334 

foundational single-cell CRISPR screen datasets 39 . However, the conditional resampling 335 

methods proposed by Barry and colleagues (SCEPTRE) robustly address miscalibration 336 

in real-world data, producing well-calibrated p-values for individual gRNA-gene pairs 337 

across datasets. These are appropriate inputs for FRACTEL. 338

Discussion 425 

CRISPR-based perturbation screens have become indispensable tools for mapping the 426 

regulatory architecture of the genome, yet most analytical pipelines have been optimized 427 

to perform gRNA-level inference. This focus overlooks the fact that the primary unit of 428 

biological interest is the genomic region, leading to inflated region-level false discovery 429 

rates and reduced power when signals are distributed across multiple gRNAs. 430 

Furthermore, these negative effects can be amplified in increasingly common 431 

downstream analyses focused on the association of perturbations with identified cellular 432 

programs and networks. Our study introduces FRACTEL, a statistical framework that 433 

addresses this disconnect by aggregating gRNA-level evidence into principled region-434 

level inference. 435 

436 

Our approach offers several key advantages and some limitations. FRACTEL preserves 437 

the scale of gRNA-level p-values rather than normalizing them, allowing more information 438 

to be retained during aggregation while maintaining the FDR. In addition, robust rank 439 

aggregation is agnostic to the input test, allowing for future improvements in gRNA-level 440 

differential expression analysis that further address biological heterogeneity or technical 441 

factors. However, FRACTEL requires well-calibrated gRNA-level p-values, and many 442 

differential expression analyses inflate false-positives 39 , making them inappropriate 443 

inputs to FRACTEL. As such, we recommend using gRNA-level analysis from SCEPTRE 444 

as input to FRACTEL for scRNA-seq CRISPR screens, as those are typically well-445 

calibrated. Alternatively, we provide an empirical p-value transformation to enable 446 

calibration using negative controls (see Methods). Although we focus on statistical 447

small or moderate effect sizes. However, this difference is reduced by increasing 463 

FRACTEL’s bound. 464

Several practical considerations emerged from our analyses. Repression screens 473 

targeting genes with very low baseline expression face an inherent ceiling on statistical 474 

power. As repression drives expression toward zero, complete separation inflates 475 

standard errors, reducing significance even for large effect sizes. This limitation reflects 476 

the biology of sparse transcript detection rather than shortcomings of the model, 477 

underscoring the importance of pre-filtering low-expression targets or incorporating 478 

targeted enrichment strategies. FRACTEL also assumes approximate independence of 479 

gRNA-level tests, which is reasonable for most CRISPRi/a designs but may be violated 480 

in cases of overlapping gRNAs or shared sequence features; in these cases, other 481 

models designed for tiling experiments (e.g. CRISPR-SURF) may be more appropriate 37 .482 

483 

By enabling power analysis, our results also highlight design trade-offs between gRNA 484 

redundancy and efficacy. Adding non-functional gRNAs introduces noise and reduces 485 

power, while increasing the number of effective gRNAs substantially improves detection. 486 

These findings argue for broad coverage in exploratory screens to maximize the chance 487 

of including effective gRNAs, followed by validation experiments that prioritize high-488 

efficacy gRNAs. Incorporating predictive models of gRNA activity into library design may 489 

further optimize this balance. 490

Conclusion 509 

We developed FRACTEL, a statistical framework for element-level inference in CRISPR 510 

perturbation screens. By aggregating gRNA-level p-values through a bounded-minimum 511 

approach and calibrating the null distribution via simulation, FRACTEL achieves accurate 512 

type I error control while retaining sensitivity across diverse effect architectures. 513

Correcting Null Calibration 584 

FRACTEL requires uniformly distributed negative control (non-targeting or safe-targeting) 585 

gRNA-level p-values. However, methods often produce many false positives 38,39 , with the 586 

extent varying by screen and analysis. We provide a computational tool to transform all 587 

p-values using a linear interpolation of the empirical cumulative distribution function 588 

(ECDF) of the non-targeting control gRNAs. This transformation measures the 589 

significance of each test gRNA as a linear interpolation of the proportion of non-targeting 590 

control gRNAs with smaller p-values than the test gRNA. This forces the background 591 

control p-value distribution to be uniform and reduces the significance of all other gRNAs, 592 

effectively measuring signal in the screen relative to internal negative controls. 593
