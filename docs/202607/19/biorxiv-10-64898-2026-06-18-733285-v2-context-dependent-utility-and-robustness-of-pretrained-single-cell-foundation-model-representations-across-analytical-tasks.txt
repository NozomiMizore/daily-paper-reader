Abstract 36 

Single-cell foundation models (scFMs) have emerged as powerful representation 37 

learning approaches for single-cell transcriptomics. However, the utility and 38 

robustness of their pretrained representations across diverse analytical tasks and data 39 

conditions remain insufficiently characterized, particularly in zero-shot settings 40 

without task-specific fine-tuning. Here, we systematically analyze zero-shot 41 

performance of single-cell transcriptomic representations across 20 methods, 642 

downstream tasks and 1,607 datasets comprising nearly 21.8 million cells. We 43 

evaluate model behavior along three complementary dimensions: utility on original 44 

datasets, robustness to controlled changes in dataset structure, and exploratory 45 

associations between dataset characteristics and performance variation. Our results 46 

show that scFM performance is strongly task dependent, with no single method 47 

consistently outperforming others across cell- and gene-level analyses. Notably, high 48 

utility on original datasets did not necessarily translate into robustness under 49 

structural perturbations, and several top-ranking methods were sensitive to changes in 50 

cell number, gene number, class composition, class imbalance, and batch complexity. 51 

Conventional statistical and task-specific methods remained competitive in several 52 

settings, while greater computational cost did not consistently correspond to better 53 

performance. Driver analyses further identified task-specific associations between 54 

performance and dataset characteristics, including cell-type complexity, train-test 55 

class overlap, batch number, and regulatory target-set size. Together, these findings 56 

show that the zero-shot utility and robustness of pretrained scFM representations 57 

depend jointly on analytical task and dataset structure. Our study provides a practical 58 

basis for context-aware representation selection and underscores the importance of 59 

evaluating structural robustness alongside utility when developing and applying 60 

scFMs. 61

INTRODUCTION 63 

Single-cell transcriptomics is now generating data at unprecedented scale ( 1, 2 ), 64 

enabling detailed characterization of cellular heterogeneity across tissues (3), 65 

developmental stages ( 4) and disease contexts ( 5). As single-cell RNA-sequencing 66 

(scRNA-seq) atlases expand to hundreds of millions of cells, this growth has driven 67 

intense interest in large-scale representation learning for biology ( 6). Inspired by 68 

advances in foundation models in natural language processing (7-9 ), several 69 

single-cell foundation models (scFMs) have been developed to learn generalizable 70 

embeddings from large-scale pretraining (9-20 ). In particular, models such as 71 

scFoundation ( 10 ), UCE ( 18 ) and GenePT ( 15 ) have explicitly emphasized zero-shot 72 

applicability, proposing that pretrained representations can be reused across 73 

downstream tasks without task-specific retraining. If this premise holds broadly, 74 

representation learning could shift from task-centered model construction toward 75 

reusable analytical backbones for single-cell data. 76 

Despite these expectations, the extent to which scFMs have been incorporated 77 

into routine analytical workflows remains uncertain. Our citation-context analysis of 78 

1,330 studies citing original scFM publications suggests that direct downstream 79 

analytical adoption remains limited (Supplementary Note 1, Supplementary Fig. 1). 80 

While these models are widely referenced in the literature, their routine incorporation 81 

into practical analytical pipelines appears less common than anticipated. In practice, 82 

extensive fine-tuning demands substantial computational resources, mature software 83 

infrastructures and systematic hyperparameter optimization, conditions that are not 84 

universally available. Under such constraints, zero-shot deployment therefore 85 

becomes a fundamental prerequisite for practical adoption rather than an optional 86 

convenience. Researchers commonly extract pretrained embeddings directly for 87 

downstream analysis, particularly when processing large-scale atlases. Therefore, the 88 

critical question is whether zero-shot representations alone provide stable and 89 

reproducible advantages over simpler statistical baselines such as principal 90 

component analysis (PCA), which remain widely adopted because of their 91 

interpretability and computational efficiency. 92

To support the rapid development of this field, several benchmarking efforts 93 

have mapped an initial landscape of scFM capabilities across three complementary 94 

tracks (21-26 ). Technical benchmarks, such as scEval (25 ), focus on model 95 

optimization and training stability to provide guidelines for fine-tuning. Conversely, 96 

biological application-centric benchmarks, including BioLLM ( 23 ) and Wu et al. ( 21 ), 97 

investigate model performance in complex biomedical scenarios to guide model 98 

selection for specific biological tasks. Concurrently, critical evaluations targeting 99 

specific tasks, such as cell annotation ( 24 ) or batch integration ( 22 ), aim to investigate 100 

whether massive pretrained architectures can outperform simpler baselines. A general 101 

consensus from these diverse studies is that no single scFM consistently dominates. 102 

While these benchmark studies establish an essential landscape, three critical 103 

methodological gaps remain in evaluating practical zero-shot applicability. First, 104 

current benchmarks rely almost exclusively on widely adopted datasets, leaving the 105 

vulnerability of pretrained embeddings to structural data shifts, such as changes in cell 106 

or gene numbers, largely untested. Second, zero-shot cross-task assessments lack the 107 

algorithmic breadth and dataset scale required for comprehensive validation. Third, 108 

the quantitative relationship between zero-shot efficacy and specific dataset-level 109 

characteristics remains unexplored. Resolving these uncertainties is essential for 110 

single-cell analyses in translational research ( 27-29 ), as overlooking representation 111 

stability under realistic variability risks overestimating generalization claims while 112 

neglecting simpler, highly robust alternatives. 113 

Here we define the practical limits of zero-shot generalization in scFMs through 114 

a structured evaluation framework spanning both cell-level and gene-level analyses. 115 

We evaluated 20 representation methods across 6 downstream tasks on 1,607 datasets 116 

encompassing nearly 21.8 million cells. These include original datasets alongside 117 

their structural perturbations. Specifically, we characterize model performance along 118 

three complementary dimensions: (i) baseline utility on original datasets, (ii) 119 

robustness on structurally perturbed datasets and (iii) exploratory associations 120 

between dataset-level features and performance. By employing structured data 121 

augmentation to alter dataset properties, such as cell and gene count, we provide a 122

RESULTS 131 

Overview of the benchmarking framework 132 

To examine how scFMs perform under routine analytical conditions, we established a 133 

structured evaluation framework that assesses pretrained representations across 134 

diverse biological tasks and perturbations of dataset structural properties (Fig. 1). 135 

Rather than focusing solely on average task-level rankings, this design measures the 136 

robustness of zero-shot representations against realistic heterogeneity in single-cell 137 

data. Pretrained models transform scRNA-seq data into biological representations, 138 

including cell embeddings, gene embeddings and gene-gene attention scores (Fig. 1a). 139 

These representations were evaluated under zero-shot conditions, without 140 

task-specific fine-tuning. We examined six downstream applications spanning 141 

unsupervised cell clustering, supervised annotation and network inference, reflecting 142 

common analytical scenarios in single-cell studies (Fig. 1b). For each task, original 143 

benchmark datasets were paired with systematically generated variants produced 144 

through perturbations of dataset structure, including cell number, gene number, 145 

cell-type number, class imbalance and batch complexity. This matched design enables 146 

direct comparison between unperturbed and structurally altered datasets. Model 147 

behavior was characterized along three complementary dimensions (Fig. 1c). Utility 148 

was defined as performance on original datasets. Robustness quantified the magnitude 149 

of performance change under dataset structural perturbations. In addition, driver 150 

analysis examined associations between dataset-level characteristics and performance 151 

variation using multivariable linear regression model. Overall, this framework 152

Cell clustering under zero-shot representations 170 

We first evaluated zero-shot cell embeddings in the context of cell clustering across 171 

26 original datasets. Eighteen representation methods were compared, including 172 

scFMs together with PCA and scGNN ( 30 ) as reference baselines (Methods). For each 173 

dataset, embeddings were extracted without task-specific retraining and clustered 174 

using a unified scIB ( 31 ) workflow. Performance was assessed both on original 175 

datasets (utility) and on 339 datasets generated through perturbations of dataset 176 

structural properties (robustness). Clustering accuracy was quantified using adjusted 177 

Rand Index (ARI), normalized mutual information (NMI) and average silhouette 178 

width (ASW), capturing agreement with reference cell labels and geometric 179 

separability in embedding space. 180 

Under original dataset conditions, models exhibited clear performance 181 

stratification (Fig. 1b,d,f). Based on aggregated ranks, UCE4L, SCimilarity ( 16 ) and 182 

CellPLM ( 17 ) achieved the highest overall clustering utility (Fig. 1a, Supplementary 183 

Fig. 2-3). UCE4L and SCimilarity consistently ranked among the top performers 184 

across ARI, NMI and ASW, whereas CellPLM remained within the top tier for ARI 185 

and ASW with a modest decline in NMI. Across datasets, several scFMs significantly 186 

outperformed PCA based on paired comparisons, indicating that large-scale 187 

pretraining can improve recovery of cell-type structure under zero-shot settings. 188 

However, higher utility on original datasets did not necessarily translate into 189 

robustness under structural perturbations. Performance deviations under perturbations 190 

varied substantially across methods (Fig. 1c,e,g). Robustness rankings revealed a 191 

pattern distinct from utility rankings. CellFMmin (14 ), scBERTMean (20 ) and 192 

scBERTCLS exhibited smaller deviations across multiple metrics and ranked highly 193 

in overall robustness (Supplementary Fig. 4-5). Utility and robustness were not 194 

aligned. Methods with lower utility sometimes exhibited smaller absolute deviations, 195 

suggesting that limited dynamic range may partly constrain observed fluctuations. 196 

Robustness rankings alone therefore do not fully characterize practical performance. 197

To jointly examine clustering utility and robustness, models were mapped in a 198 

two-dimensional space (Fig. 1h), where lower ranks indicate better performance. 199 

Using asymmetric thresholds (top quartile for utility and median for robustness), we 200 

identified models combining high utility with acceptable robustness. In the overall 201 

space, UCE4L and CellPLM occupied the preferred region, indicating a balance 202 

between clustering utility and robustness. SCimilarity achieved strong utility but fell 203 

below the robustness threshold. Metric-specific projections further revealed 204 

heterogeneity. In ARI space, SCimilarity satisfied both thresholds, whereas in NMI 205 

space UCE4L and CellPLM entered the preferred region. In contrast, ASW rankings 206 

were reordered, with scFoundation uniquely satisfying both criteria, indicating 207 

divergence between geometric separability and label-consistency metrics. 208 

Drivers analysis identified systematic statistical associations between clustering 209 

performance and dataset structural properties. For ARI and NMI, cell-type number 210 

and total cell count exhibited the largest average absolute standardized coefficients, 211 

indicating that clustering performance is associated with population complexity and 212 

sample scale (Supplementary Fig. 6a). For ASW, cell-type number exhibited the 213 

strongest association, whereas total cell number and gene number showed weaker 214 

effects, suggesting that geometric separability is more sensitive to class structure than 215 

dataset size alone. Across models, the direction of association was largely consistent 216 

(Supplementary Fig. 6b). Heterogeneity across models was primarily reflected in 217 

effect magnitude rather than direction. These results suggest that clustering 218 

performance under zero-shot representations is systematically related to dataset 219 

composition, although effect sizes differ across models. In addition, the time for cell 220 

representation extraction ranged from approximately 1-2 minutes to over 30 minutes, 221 

with substantial variation in CPU and GPU memory usage (Supplementary Table 2). 222 

Computational cost did not directly track clustering utility, underscoring that model 223 

selection in routine single-cell analysis may involve trade-offs among utility, 224 

robustness and resource consumption. 225

Cell type annotation under zero-shot representations 244 

We next evaluated zero-shot representations for supervised cell type annotation across 245 

26 original datasets. Eighteen representation methods were compared, including 246 

scFMs together with highVariable ( 32 ) and scGNN as reference baselines (Methods). 247 

Stratified five-fold cross-validation was applied within each dataset, and embeddings 248 

were extracted independently for training and testing partitions without sharing 249 

intermediate statistics. A unified fully connected classifier was trained on all 250 

embeddings to ensure comparability across methods. Performance was assessed on 251 

original datasets (utility) and on 328 datasets generated through perturbations of 252 

dataset structural properties (robustness). Classification accuracy was quantified using 253 

F1-score, AUROC (area under the receiver operating characteristic curve) and 254 

AUPRC (area under the precision-recall curve). 255 

Under original dataset conditions, models exhibited clear stratification in 256 

supervised discriminative performance (Fig. 3b,d,f). Based on aggregated ranks, 257 

scFoundation achieved the highest overall utility, followed by highVariable and 258 

CellPLM (Fig. 3a, Supplementary Fig. 7-8). scFoundation ranked consistently at the 259 

top across F1-score, AUROC and AUPRC. Notably, highVariable remained 260 

competitive under the unified classifier, indicating that traditional statistical 261 

representations retain strong discriminative capacity in supervised settings. While 262 

AUROC values approached saturation for multiple models, larger separations were 263 

observed in F1-score and AUPRC. Cross-validation variance was low across folds, 264 

suggesting stable performance estimation (Supplementary Fig. 9). Under 265

Drug sensitivity prediction under zero-shot representations 309 

We next evaluated zero-shot representations in a phenotype prediction setting, 310 

focusing on drug sensitivity prediction across 14 curated single-cell datasets derived 311 

from DRMref ( 33 ). In contrast to clustering and annotation tasks that assess structural 312 

recovery or label discrimination, this task evaluates whether pretrained embeddings 313 

capture cell-state representations that supports drug sensitivity prediction under 314 

zero-shot conditions. For each dataset, a binary classification problem (resistant 315 

versus non-resistant) was constructed at the cell level. All methods provided cell 316 

embeddings as input features, extracted under zero-shot settings without fine-tuning. 317 

A unified dual-tower neural architecture integrated cell embeddings with drug 318 

molecular features encoded as 1,024-dimensional Morgan fingerprints derived from 319 

SMILES representations. All models were trained under identical cross-validation, 320 

optimization and architectural conditions to ensure that performance differences 321 

reflected embedding quality rather than classifier design (Methods). Performance was 322 

evaluated on original datasets (utility) and on 177 datasets generated through 323 

perturbations of dataset structural properties and domain-shift scenarios (robustness). 324 

F1-score, AUROC and AUPRC were used as complementary metrics. 325 

Under original dataset conditions, models exhibited clear stratification in 326 

predictive performance (Fig. 4b,d,f). Based on aggregated ranks, highVariable and 327 

scFoundation achieved the highest overall utility, followed by GeneCompass ( 13 ) and 328 

tGPT ( 19 ) (Fig. 4a, Supplementary Fig. 14-15). Notably, highVariable achieved the 329 

top comprehensive ranking and performed strongly across all three metrics, indicating 330 

that statistic-based features remain competitive in drug sensitivity prediction. 331

Batch integration under zero-shot representations 376 

We next evaluated zero-shot representations in the context of batch integration on 377 

seven widely used and representative multi-technology single-cell datasets. In contrast 378 

to supervised tasks, batch integration is an unsupervised integration problem that 379 

seeks to attenuate technical variation while preserving biological structure. Eighteen 380 

representation methods were compared, including scFMs together with Harmony ( 34 )381 

and scGNN as reference baselines. For all scFMs, embeddings were extracted under 382 

zero-shot settings without batch-specific fine-tuning and evaluated using a unified 383 

scIB workflow (Methods). Integration quality was assessed on original datasets 384 

(utility) and on 325 datasets generated through perturbations of dataset structural 385 

properties (robustness). Integration quality was quantified using complementary 386 

metrics. Biological conservation was evaluated using ARI, NMI and ASW to measure 387 

preservation of cell-type structure after integration. Batch mixing was evaluated using 388 

iLISI (inverse local inverse Simpson’s index), kBET (k-nearest-neighbor batch effect 389 

test) and batch ASW to quantify the degree of batch alignment in embedding space. 390 

Under original dataset conditions, integration utility exhibited clear stratification 391

DISCUSSION 559 

In this study, we established a structured framework to evaluate scFMs under routine 560 

analytical constraints. All embeddings are extracted strictly in a zero-shot manner, 561 

downstream pipelines are standardized, and performance is assessed on original 562 

datasets as well as under perturbations of dataset structural properties. Across 6 tasks 563 

and 1,607 datasets, no single method maintained consistently strong performance 564 

across datasets. Instead, method preferences exhibit strong task dependency, 565

indicating that zero-shot performance cannot be reduced to a single global ranking. 566 

Joint evaluation of utility and robustness revealed additional performance space 567 

beyond baseline rankings. For cell clustering UCE4L and CellPLM satisfied the joint 568 

utility-robustness criteria; scFoundation met the joint thresholds in cell type 569 

annotation; and highVariable together with scFoundation showed balanced 570 

performance in drug sensitivity prediction. In batch integration, Harmony ranked 571 

highest in utility, whereas UCE4L and SCimilarity met both thresholds. At the gene 572 

level, scGPT and Geneformer6L satisfied the joint criteria for gene function 573 

prediction, whereas GeneCompass showed high utility with lower robustness. In gene 574 

regulatory network inference, GeneCompass met the joint criteria, whereas a575 

cosine-similarity baseline showed competitive utility but weaker robustness. These 576 

patterns indicate that zero-shot generalization in scFMs cannot be inferred from utility 577 

alone and is better understood as a balance between utility and robustness under 578 

realistic analytical variability. 579 

Several broader implications emerge from these cross-task comparisons. First, 580 

utility and robustness can decouple. Methods that rank highly on original datasets can 581 

exhibit larger paired deviations under structural perturbations, as observed for 582 

SCimilarity in clustering and for Harmony and scGPT in batch integration. Reporting 583 

both baseline utility and perturbation-based deviation therefore improves the 584 

reliability of method recommendations, particularly when dataset composition differs 585 

from benchmark conditions. Second, baseline methods remain practically competitive 586 

in specific contexts when downstream pipelines are standardized and fine-tuning is 587 

avoided. For example, highVariable performed strongly in supervised annotation and 588 

met joint criteria in drug sensitivity prediction, and Harmony achieved the highest 589 

utility in batch integration. These observations suggest that gains from large 590 

pretrained embeddings are task- and data-dependent rather than universal. Third, 591 

computational cost varied substantially across methods and did not consistently track 592 

performance. Resource constraints can therefore rationally influence method choice 593 

without implying uniform losses in accuracy. This observation aligns with the 594 

accessibility challenges for large single-cell models (6) and supports reporting 595

Together, these findings refine the interpretation and boundaries of zero-shot 602 

generalization in scFMs. Utility on a fixed benchmark captures one dimension of 603 

practical value, but stability under shifts to dataset structure is a separate and 604 

complementary dimension that can reorder methods. A comprehensive evaluation 605

perturbations of dataset structural properties and from presenting recommendations 607

fine-tuning and used standardized downstream heads to isolate representation content. 610 

As a result, best-case performance achievable through extensive tuning or customized 611

Task 6: Gene regulatory network inference 913 

Dataset and ground-truth construction 914 

We evaluated foundation model capabilities for GRN inference using perturbation 915 

single-cell datasets and a ground-truth definition for TF targets. We curated 17 916 

datasets comprising 9,096 cells, each corresponding to TF overexpression (OE) or 917

min.features = 1000). Ground-truth TF targets were defined by a two-evidence 920

log2FC > 0.25, p-value < 0.05), and (ii) motif-supported candidates defined as the top 923 

5,000 genes ranked by the corresponding TF motif in the RcisTarget matrix( 44 ). The 924 

intersection of the two sets was used as high-confidence targets. Datasets with fewer 925 

than five targets were excluded to reduce indirect cascade effects (Supplementary 926 

Table 7). 927
